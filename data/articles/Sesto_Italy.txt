The results of a study on 111 obsidian artifacts collected during an archaeological campaign performed by the University of Siena near Sesto
Fiorentino (Florence, Italy) are reported. Earlier, we used the concentration ratios among some elements as reported in the literature to discriminate
the deposit sources. Later, optimizing the characterization and for having a more strong classification, multivariate chemiometric investigation has
been performed (multivariate statistical analysis in Principal Components and Discriminating Factor Analysis). The data obtained show three wellseparated groups connected to the relative sites in Lipari and in Sardinia (flows A and C from Arci Mountain).
Introduction
The obsidian, natural glass from volcanic origin
formed by rapid solidification from a liquid state (SiO2
as main component ranging between 66% and 75%), 1
has been widely used both in the Middle East and in the
Mediterranean area since the Neolithic period.2–11
Nowadays, in several archaeological sites even far from
the deposit sites, a lot of obsidian artifacts are found
documenting its importance and the wide trade ways. 2
For this reason the obsidian characterization through
trace element analysis is still involved in several
investigations carried out in provenance studies.
Among the different analytical methods used for the
element speciation such as optical emission
spectroscopy (OES), X-ray fluorescence (XRF) and
inductively coupled plasma emission spectroscopy (ICPES), the instrumental nuclear activation analysis (INAA)
is one of the widely employed in the determination of
elements at trace and ultra-trace levels in all materials
and, particularly, minerals, for its peculiar
characteristics: it allows to analyze few amount (mg) of
sample, to reach high analytical sensitivity, precision
and accuracy for each element and to avoid destructive
analysis even if it is invasive.12
In this paper, 111 obsidian artifacts coming from the
archaeological excavation of Neto-via Verga in Sesto
Fiorentino (Florence, Italy) have been analyzed by
INAA and their composition has been evaluated for
individualizing the provenance sites and the prehistoric
trade ways in the Mediterranean area. All the
archaeological artifacts studied are dated about 5000 b.p.
according to the not calibrated 14 C investigation. 13

Experimental
The artifacts have been investigated by INAA for
determining the concentration of some elements at trace
levels (ppm). Preliminarily, the artifacts have been
treated in ultra-sound bath for avoiding the superficial
contamination. The weight of each artifact varied
between 70 and 600 mg.
The neutron irradiation was performed in the
rotatory rack (Lazy Susan) of the Casaccia TRIGA Mark
II nuclear reactor. The irradiation time was 21 hours
with a neutron flux of 2.6.10 12 n.cm–2 .s–1 . The
integrated flux was of 2.0.10 17 n.cm–2 . The rotatory rack
speed was kept constant for having homogeneous
neutron flux and neutron energy.
Different materials of similar composition to the
obsidian minerals were chosen as secondary standards;
particularly, standards GRX1, GRX4 and GRX6 from
the United States Geochemical Survey (USGS),
constituted by silica materials from geological source,
and Coal Fly Ash from the United States National
Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) were
involved in this study. The uncertainty of the
methodology was tested measuring the above secondary
standards with solution of primary standards at different
concentrations: all the found data are in agreement with
the certified values except for Zn that is always
consistently lower. This is due to the high 46 Sc

Results and discussion
The composition of 111 obsidian artifacts
collected in the Sesto Fiorentino (Italy) area (Fig. 1) has
been compared to obsidian samples of well-know
geographical provenance to understand the deposit
areas.
From the literature 3,14 the authors considered
obsidian samples from the western Mediterranean
provenance sites such as Lipari, Sardegna (Arci
Mountain, sites A, B and C), Pantelleria e Palmarola
(Pontine Island) (Fig. 1). They represent standards
according to the geographical origin. On the basis of the
information reported in the literature, 2–11 it should be
possible to individuate the deposit sites of each obsidian
artifacts, comparing some elemental ratios, e.g., La/Sc
vs Cs/Sc. The La/Sc vs. Cs/Sc plot (Fig. 2) shows that
the samples used as standards make four well-separated
groups (Sardinia, Lipari, Pantelleria and Pontine island).
The analyzed obsidian artifacts are located in two
specific groups: Lipari and Sardinia A (SA) and Sardinia
C (SC), 3,14 with only one exception, sample 434, that
seems to be external to the groups (outlier).
Other useful considerations are possible analyzing
the correlation between Eu and Cs concentrations
(Fig. 3) and using the complete discrimination among
the sites, i.e., source discrimination (Fig. 4):2

The elements, Ce, Cs, La, Rb, Sc, Ta and Th, are not
sufficient to separate the obsidian artifacts in different
groups with high probability. For this reason, the
achieved results would be considered prudentially and
subjected to large uncertainty that can be increased using
statistical methods similar to those involved in the
provenance characterization and quality control of the
goods. 15
This is an evident multivariate problem: in fact, the
characterization of the geological-geographical
provenance of obsidian artifacts through a wide
“chemical composition profile” is a typical statistical
multivariate analysis. 16 A chemiometric investigation
has been performed to confirm statistically (very high
percent of accuracy) the information about the obsidian
provenance areas. For this scope the Discriminating
Factorial Analysis was used: 16 the characteristic issue
has been individuated in the geological-geographical
factor whereas the variables are the 12 elements present
in all the artifacts (Ce, Co, Cs, Eu, Fe, Hf, La, Na, Sc,
Sm, Th, U).

This explorative analysis based on the Principal
Component Analysis (PCA), has regarded a dataset of
the 12 chemical composition variables that, after
normalization, allow obtaining three principal
components describing 82.7% of the total variance of
the data matrix (increasing up to 90.9% with 4
components). After, using the discriminating factorial
analysis, linear composition models of the discriminant
functions are found and the position of artifacts
compared to the standards is obtained (Fig. 5). The
consequent statistical tests have allowed confirming the
separation of the investigated obsidian artifacts into two
well-identified groups, Sardinia (SA and SC) and Lipari,
respectively, with very high probability (95%).
Discriminant functions are used to determine which
variables discriminate between two or more naturally
groups. As one can see in Fig. 5, the function F1
discriminates the groups P (Pan), S (SA and SC) and L
(Lip) while it is not able to separate the groups S and O.
On the contrary, the function F2 allows to discriminate
clearly the group O (Pon). The basic idea underlying in

Conclusions
In this paper the concentration of 31 elements in 111
obsidian artifacts coming from the archaeological
settlement of Sesto Fiorentino (Florence, Italy), was
investigated by INAA. On the basis of the results, the
most probable provenance sites have been individuated.
All the elaborations performed, from statistical tests
reported in the literature (i.e., the La/Sc vs. Fe/Sc ratio,
the correlation between Eu and Cs concentrations,
the source discrimination) using chemiometric
techniques (Discriminating Factor Analysis), have
evidenced that the 111 obsidian artifacts (including the
sample 434 considered an outliner at the beginning)
came from two main sites in the western Mediterranean
area, Lipari and Arci Mountain (SA and SC), confirming
the presence of commercial trades and ways inside the
area since the Neolithic period. 2–4
