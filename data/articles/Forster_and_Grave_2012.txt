Non-destructive pXRF has the potential to expand sample populations of archaeological provenancing
studies by facilitating access to museum collections of artefacts. In this study, we use museum-curated
obsidian for the sites of Tell Brak, Mersin-Yümüktepe and Tell Arpachiyah and geological obsidian
samples from central Turkey to demonstrate that non-destructive pXRF is comparable to other techniques in differentiating between Near Eastern obsidian sources. Secondly, we use multivariate analysis
to evaluate whether non-destructive pXRF is sufficiently accurate and precise to enable comparison with
legacy datasets from previous analysis using PIXE and ICP-MS. Multivariate analyses show that instrumental offsets between the results from pXRF and other analytic techniques are less than source-tosource variation typical for most Anatolian obsidian outcrops and pXRF-identified geochemical groups
are directly comparable to legacy datasets. Non-destructive pXRF analysis indicates that during the
Chalcolithic, East Göllü dag and Nenezi dag continued to be the major sources of obsidian to communities in central Anatolia and the Bingöl range and mountains surrounding Lake Van supplied sites east of
the Levant. Variations in the analysed assemblages suggest a number of obsidian exchange networks
existed during this period.

1. Introduction
For the Neolithic and Chalcolithic communities of the Near East
(ca. 9500e3000 BCE), obsidian was a culturally significant and
valuable exchange item. With a limited number of volcanic sources
and a ubiquitous representation in the archaeological record, it
represents one of the earliest and most reliable archaeological
measures of long-distance exchange in this region (Balkan-Atli
et al., 1999; Shackley, 1998; Williams Thorpe, 1995). Recent
studies have demonstrated that changes in obsidian source preference e related to shifts in interregional social exchange networks
e are most evident when large numbers of artefacts are provenanced (Carter et al., 2008; Carter and Shackley, 2007). Apart from
material obtained from current excavations, there are two additional sources of archaeological and geological obsidian which
could serve to increase sample populations and reference material.
Museum collections represent an important resource that has
remained largely untapped due to the constraints of destructive
sampling techniques. Similarly, published obsidian analyses for this
region represent a source of legacy data. Non-destructive pXRF has
the potential to access and integrate these additional sources.
Obsidian is ideal for geochemical provenancing as outcrops are
relatively geochemically homogeneous with unique ‘elemental
fingerprints’. A variety of analytic techniques have been applied to
the chemical characterisation of obsidian artefacts including NAA,
PIXE, ICP-MS and XRF (Bourdonnec et al., 2005; Briois et al., 1997;
Carter et al., 2006; Gomez et al., 1995; Gratuze, 1999; Poupeau
et al., 2010). These techniques typically involve destructive
sampling, a factor which can preclude analysis in museum contexts.
While sampling for LA-ICP-MS is non-visible to the naked eye and
so preserves the integrity of artefacts, samples must be moved to
a laboratory for analysis, a condition that is generally prohibitive
when studying museum collections. Non-destructive portable
instrumentation offers new potential for research in museum and
excavation contexts as the conventional hurdles to chemical characterisation are removed, particularly for items that are rare,
valuable, fragile or culturally significant. Recent studies have
demonstrated the successful application of portable x-ray fluorescence (pXRF) to obsidian for non-destructive provenancing in Asian
and South American contexts (Craig et al., 2010; Jia et al., 2010;
Phillips and Speakman, 2009).
In this paper, we use non-destructive pXRF for analysis of
museum-curated obsidian artefacts from Tell Brak, MersinYümüktepe and Tell Arpachiyah. The aims of this study are twofold;
first, to determine whether Near Eastern obsidian sources can be
readily differentiated by non-destructive pXRF. The reliability of
quantitative non-destructive pXRF is strongly conditioned both by

1.1. Background
Analyses from a number of studies show that obsidian deposits
in central and eastern Anatolia supplied sites in the Near East from
the Epipalaeolithic to the Bronze Age (approximately 14,000e6000
BP) (Abbès et al., 2003; Briois et al., 1997; Cauvin et al., 1997;
Chataigner et al., 1998; Yellin et al., 1996). These deposits
supplied social exchange networks that were initially small, but
over time expanded and became more complex and interconnected, with an overlapping region in the northern Levant and
Upper Euphrates valley (Cauvin and Chataigner, 1998).
Turkey is the major source for obsidian to the Near East, with
outcrops in central and eastern Anatolia. In central Anatolia, the
major sources exploited were East Göllü dag and Nenezi dag. These
sources were used extensively and supplied communities
throughout the middle Euphrates, western Anatolia, and the
southern Levant. During the Chalcolithic, obsidian from East Göllü
dag travelled as far as Mesopotamia (Chataigner et al., 1998).
Although there are a number of other obsidian deposits in close
geographical proximity in central Anatolia (Hasan dag, Acıgöl dag,
Hotamıs ̧ dag), these sources had minimal impact and a limited
number of obsidian artefacts have been provenanced to Acıgöl dag
and Hotamıs ̧ dag (Carter et al., 2006; Yellin et al., 1996).
In eastern Anatolia, the sources primarily exploited were the
mountains of the Bingöl region (Orta Duz, Çavus ̧ lar, Çatak, Alatepe)
and obsidian deposits around Lake Van (Nemrut Dag and Meydan
dag). Obsidian from these sources supplied the Persian Gulf, the
Euphrates and the Levant and eventually reached as far west as
Catalhöyük (Carter et al., 2008; Chataigner et al., 1998). The Bingöl
mountains are comprised of both calc-alkaline (Bingöl B) and peralkaline (Bingöl A) deposits. Bingöl A is geochemically very similar
to Nemrut Dag and previous geochemical work has had difficulty
distinguishing between the two sources (Carter et al., 2008;
Chataigner et al., 1998; Khalidi et al., 2009). Like central Anatolia,
there are a number of sources that experienced no-to-minimal
known anthropogenic exploitation (eg. Mus ̧ , Suphan and Pasinler) (Bressy et al., 2005; Chataigner et al., 1998).
In this paper, we characterise obsidian from three major
archaeological sites in the region first excavated in the 1930’s: Tell
Arpachiya, Iraq; Mersin-Yümüktepe (Level XII), Turkey; and Tell
Brak, Syria. The location of these sites in relation to the archaeologically known major obsidian sources is illustrated in Fig. 1. The
British Museum bequeathed these artefacts to the Nicholson
Museum where they are currently held at the University of Sydney,
Australia.
Mersin-Yümüktepe, first excavated by John Garstang in 1936
(Garstang, 1943), was located in Cilicia on the trade route between
the major centres of Syria and western Anatolia. It thus occupied
a key position linking the Near East and Anatolia and is considered
a potential site for the redistribution and dissemination of obsidian
as far back as 6000 BC (Mellink, 1991). Previous analysis situates
Neolithic Mersin-Yümüktepe within the central Anatolian obsidian
circulation network (Butalag et al., 2008; Demortier et al., 2008).
With a stratigraphy that spans over 8000 years, the site is considered one of the most important sites in the Near East (Garstang,
1943).
Tell Arpachiyah, located near the Tigris in Iraq, was first excavated in 1933 by Max Mallowan. Several thousand chipped stone
tools were excavated from the Halaf period (ca. 5200e4500 BC), of
which more than half were obsidian (Healey, 2007). This site had
a significantly higher quantity of obsidian than surrounding sites,

argue that Arpachiyah had control over regional access to the

quantity of obsidian artefacts from this excavation indicates

Tell Brak, in northeastern Syria, was also excavated by Max
Mallowan in 1937e38. The site lies on the farming area of the Upper
Khabur Plain and was occupied from the sixth to the second
millennia BC. The mound is one of the largest archaeological sites in
Syria, rising to a height of over 40 m. A significant quantity of
obsidian (940 artefacts) was excavated and visual analysis indicates
that a large variety of sources are represented. Chemical analysis of
a selection of artefacts confirms exploitation of the Bingöl mountain obsidian sources as well as outcrops surrounding Lake Van
(Khalidi et al., 2009). Like Tell Arpachiyah, the quantity of obsidian
at Tell Brak is noteworthy as it was considerably higher in
comparison to neighbouring sites (with the exception of Tell
Hamoukar). On the basis of this high quantity of artefacts, relative
absence of debitage, and variety of sources exploited, Tell Brak is
thought to have been on a principal trade route and procured
obsidian in the form of finished blades and flakes, rather than
directly accessing deposits (Khalidi et al., 2009).
As the evidence indicates, these sites are important for understanding the distribution and exchange of obsidian over the
Neolithic to Bronze Ages in the Near East. Chemical characterisation
of their assemblages has made it possible to elucidate general
patterns in obsidian procurement and suggests obsidian was an
item of both short and long distance exchange in the region.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Samples
A total of 26 obsidian samples were analysed from MersinYümüktepe (Level XII) (n ¼ 17 flakes and fragments), Tell Arpachiya
(n ¼ 5 blades) and Tell Brak (n ¼ 4 flakes). Archival records indicate
that of the seventeen samples from Mersin, seven fragments belong
to one artefact that was broken, and two fragments are from
a second broken artefact (i.e. 17 fragments represent a total of ten
artefacts). Mersin-Yümüktepe Level XII was dated to the Chalcolithic period (ca. 4500e3000 BC) (Garstang, 1943). Archival records
indicate that the blades from Tell Arpachiya also date to the Chalcolithic period. Unfortunately, there is no data available on the
exact context of the obsidian flakes from Tell Brak. Although the site
was occupied from the sixth to the second millennia BC, we assume
a likely period for the artefacts is the Halaf or Chalcolithic
(5500e3000) (later Bronze Age obsidian in the Near East was
primarily used for exotic vessels or inlays rather than flaked tools,
as are the artefacts in this study (Coqueugniot, 1998)) (Fig. 2).
In addition to the archaeological samples, we analyse geological
samples from the main sources in central Turkey. The geological
samples used in this study are from the central Turkish sources of
East Göllü Dag, Nenezi Dag, Hotamıs ̧ D ag (sources I and II) and
Acıgöl Dag. As samples from eastern Anatolia were not available for
analysis, we drew on published data for reference source material
(Bourdonnec et al., 2005; Carter et al., 2006; Gratuze, 1999; Khalidi
et al., 2009; Poupeau et al., 2010; Ozdemir, et al., 2006) (Table 1).
This data is taken from different studies using a variety of analytic
techniques (PIXE or ICP-MS) and represents a range of potential
sources known to supply the Levant and Mesopotamia.
2.2. pXRF analysis
In this study, all pXRF analyses were conducted using a Bruker
Tracer III-V handheld XRF spectrometer. This instrument is
equipped with a rhodium tube and a Si-PIN detector with a resolution of ca. 170 eV FHWM for 5.9 keV X-rays (at 1000 counts per
second) in an area of 7 mm 2 . All analyses were conducted at 40 kV,
13 mA, using a 0.152 mm Cu, 0.025 mm Ti, 0.305 mm Al filter in the
X-ray path.
The methodological constraints for non-destructive pXRF analysis of obsidian are well-defined and a number of studies stipulate
the surface and material properties for reliable analysis (Forster
et al., 2011; Nazaroff, 2009; Shackley, 1998). For accurate and
precise analysis, the artefacts must be sufficiently thick to meet
‘infinite depth’ requirements (3 mm for standard obsidian
composition) and have a surface area that exceeds the spot size of
the instrument detector (ie. greater than 7 mm 2 ). The small analytic
window facilitates analysis of small pieces or artefacts with
a limited surface area suitable for analysis. The flattest surface
available was analysed to mitigate known attenuation effects
associated with irregular surface morphology. As sample
NM66.128.4 had only a limited area sufficiently flat and thick for
analysis, we were required to analyse over a thin paper label. In
a previous study, we demonstrated that very thin organic coatings
have a negligible effect on results for iron and heavier elements.
Lighter z elements such as calcium, however, are sensitive to such
surface interferences (Forster et al., 2011).
In order to balance time constraints with accurate analysis, we
evaluated the count time required to achieve a precision of 5%
standard error for a single analysis. Given obsidian is a homogeneous matrix, poor count statistics (associated with short analysis
time and/or low element concentration) is the primary source of
error. Ten replicate analyses from non-overlapping points on a flat
surface of the same sample of obsidian were conducted at 180, 240
and 300 s (results are reported for 300 s in Table 2). On the basis of
these results, we determined that 300 s would provide the most
accurate and precise results for the elemental concentrations
associated with Anatolian obsidian. Although the results show nine
analyses are required for precise analysis of niobium, this does not
apply to Anatolian obsidians as they have a greater concentration
than the sample analysed (the composition of this sample has been
verified using XRF).
So as to report quantitative results, a calibration algorithm was
constructed by analysing using pXRF a sectioned surface of 19
obsidian and rock samples of comparable matrix from central
Anatolia, Papua New Guinea, Australia and America. These samples
were selected because they have major, minor and trace element
composition known to cover a wide range of elemental concentrations (Table 3). The composition of the obsidian used to calibrate
was characterised previously using a Panalytical PW2400 XRF
spectrometer at the University of New South Wales. Calibration
software provided by Bruker (KTIS1CalProcess) was used to convert
intensity peaks into numerical results. The calibration software
applies Compton normalisation and matrix correction factors based
on the intensities of constituent elements.
Analysis permitted quantification of the following elements:
potassium (K), calcium (Ca), titanium (Ti), manganese (Mn), iron
(Fe), zinc (Zn), gallium (Ga), lead (Pb), thorium (Th), rubidium (Rb),
strontium (Sr), yttrium (Y), zirconium (Zr) and niobium (Nb).
2.3. Multivariate statistical analysis
In order to evaluate and identify how well non-destructive pXRF
can identify the potential sources represented in the sample, we
employ multivariate statistical analysis (Principal Components
Analysis (PCA) and Canonical Variate Analysis (CVA)) using JMP 8
(for more detailed description of the procedure see Grave et al.,
2008. While a number of studies use multiple bivariate plots to
identify compositional groups, PCA allows for several elements to
N. Forster, P. Grave / Journal of Archaeological Science 39 (2012) 728e736730be taken into account in the one analysis. Analysis was conducted
using the elements Fe, Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb as these elements are 1)
considered most reliable for non-destructive XRF (Forster et al.,
2011) and 2) are important geochemical markers for obsidian
provenancing as Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb are incompatible elements and
typically vary between volcanic sources (eg. Carter and Shackley,
2007; Khalidi et al., 2009; Shackley, 1998).
First, we identify the fragments which originated from the
broken artefacts of Mersin-Yümüktepe. Although obsidian outcrops
are relatively homogeneous, there can be drift in chemical
composition. Only on a very small scale (ie. an artefact) would we
expect the geochemistry to be unvarying (minor variation between
analyses occurs due to instrument noise and experimental error).
As a result, fragments from a single artefact show discrete groupings when the principal components are plotted.
Second, we apply multivariate analysis to the archaeological and
geological samples analysed using pXRF and assign provenance
based on geochemical groupings. As unknown sources are identi-
fied, we then extend the comparative data available by including
legacy data from previous studies (Table 1). The sources included
were identified as potential suppliers of obsidian based on their
geographical location and presence at nearby sites (Bourdonnec
et al., 2005; Bressy et al., 2005; Chataigner et al., 1998; Ozdemir
et al., 2006; Williams Thorpe, 1995).
Although pXRF is well-established as a reliable and valid technique for obsidian provenancing, recent studies have highlighted
a potential offset for results generated by pXRF and results generated using other analytic techniques (Drake et al., 2009; Nazaroff
et al., 2010). To evaluate whether quantitative pXRF is sufficiently
accurate and precise to allow provenancing based on legacy data,
we compare pXRF-generated results with legacy data (PIXE and
ICP-MS) in multivariate space.

We noted that two artefacts excavated at Mersin-Yümüktepe
broke into multiple pieces. To identify these fragments, we plotted
the first three components for the archaeological samples from
Mersin-Yümüktepe (Fig. 3a). There are two clusters apparent (n ¼ 7
and n ¼ 2) that correlate to the number of broken fragments. These
fragments are identified in Table 5. PCA indicates that the remaining
obsidian samples from East Göllü dag are related in composition,
which may indicate they originated from the same core.
PCA of the archaeological obsidian reveals five geochemical
groupings (labelled 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5 in Fig. 3b). Group 1 (n ¼ 4) is
characterised by relatively high concentrations of Sr. Group 2
(n ¼ 13) is comparatively trace element-depleted, with low levels of
Fe, Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb. Group 3 (n ¼ 4) can be distinguished by
levels of Fe, Zr, Y and Nb that are slightly elevated compared to
Groups 1 and 2. Group 4 (n ¼ 1) has a similar composition to Group
3, however has slightly higher levels of Fe, Sr and Zr, and lower
levels of Y and Nb. Group 5 (n ¼ 4) is most distinctive geochemically
and is compositionally-rich, with high levels of Fe, Rb, Y, Zr and Nb.
In order to identify the provenance of the archaeological
obsidian excavated from Mersin, Tell Brak and Tell Arpachiyah, the
first three principal components for geological and archaeological
samples were plotted and compositional groupings identified
(Fig. 3c). The geological samples for East Göllü dag and Nenezi dag
overlap with the artefacts from Mersin. This indicates Group 1
artefacts are derived from Nenezi dag and Group 2 artefacts are
from East Göllü dag. Groups 3e5 do not align with any of the
geological samples. Acıgöl and Hotamıs ̧ dag have significantly
different geochemical compositions to the archaeological samples
in the dataset. While this dataset is small and the results can only
be considered qualitative, the absence of obsidian from Acıgöl and
Hotamıs ̧ dag supports findings in other studies there was minimal
exploitation of these sources (Carter et al., 2006; Yellin et al., 1996).
Legacy data has the potential to expand the reference material
available to archaeological provenance studies. In Figs. 3d and 4,
pXRF analysis of geological samples for East Göllü dag and Nenezi
dag corresponds to results from studies using other analytic techniques. This indicates that pXRF analysis can be compared reliably
to legacy data if there are significant differences in the chemical
composition of source material. However, when the dataset is
geochemically similar (Fig. 3e), clusters appear which indicate that
multivariate statistical treatment does differentiate systematically
between pXRF and other analytic techniques. In this case,
a combination of multivariate techniques should be applied to
assign provenance with a greater degree of confidence. While
outside the scope of this study, a larger obsidian dataset analysed in
triplicate (using PIXE, ICP-MS and pXRF) would allow the calculation of the precise measurement offset between these techniques
(cf. Nazaroff et al., 2010).

3.2. Obsidian exchange during the Chalcolithic
Obsidian exchange is poorly documented for the Chalcolithic
period (Bressy et al., 2005). There are a number of studies investigating Neolithic obsidian provenance, however there are few
comparable studies pertaining to the Chalcolithic. While the small
quantity of archaeological samples limits our ability to observe
diachronic and geographic patterns obsidian procurement, our
study contributes to the growing body of legacy data and enables
a qualitative evaluation of obsidian circulation and exchange
networks at this time.

and ongoing procurement of obsidian from East Göllü dag and
Nenezi dag during the Neolithic (Butalag et al., 2008; Demortier
et al., 2008). Located approximately 200 km from East Göllü dag
and Nenezi dag and on a major trade route, Mersin-Yümüktepe was
within the ‘supply zone’ for these sources and well positioned for
the procurement and dissemination of obsidian to sites in central
Anatolia and the Levant. Within the archaeological record of Level
XII, there is evidence for both short and long distance trade, with
ceramic sherds provenanced to Macedonia and Alishar (central
Anatolia) (Garstang, 1943). Although Mersin-Yümüktepe was
clearly involved in long distance trade, current evidence indicates
that these exchange networks did not comprise of obsidian. Our
results, showing the procurement of obsidian from East Göllü dag
and Nenezi dag, indicate that central Anatolian sources continued
to be the major suppliers of obsidian to Mersin-Yümüktepe during
the Chalcolithic. This was a key site bridging Anatolia and the
Levant and had access to both obsidian from the east, however this
region was closely linked to exchange networks stemming from the
central Anatolian sources and did not procure obsidian from
eastern Anatolia.
The results reported in this study correspond to previous
research, which indicates that sites east of the Levant generally
exploited eastern Anatolian obsidian sources (Cauvin and
Chataigner, 1998; Chataigner et al., 1998; Coqueugniot, 1998).
Specifically, previous characterisation of obsidian from Tell Arpachiyah shows that during the Halaf period, obsidian was derived
from Meydan dag, Bingöl B and a peralkaline source (Bingöl A/
Nemrut dag) (Healey, 2007). Likewise, the artefacts from Tell
Arpachiyah in this study are derived from Meydan dag and Bingöl B.
This represents a distance of approximately 300e400 km between
the sources and site. A recent study of eight artefacts from Late
Chalcolithic Tell Brak found that the Bingöl region provided the
majority of obsidian to the community, with minor exploitation of
Meydan dag (Khalidi et al., 2009), indicating that obsidian was
transported approximately 250e350 km. The obsidian artefacts
from Tell Brak housed in the Nicholson Museum are also derived
from eastern Anatolia, however it was not possible to determine
definitely if they were from Bingöl or Nemrut dag.
During the Chalcolithic, trade networks were well-established
and obsidian circulation expanded, with central Anatolian sources
spreading to the Khabur Basin and as far as Mesopotamia and Tepe
Sabz in the southern Zagros (Cauvin and Chataigner, 1998). While
only a small selection of artefacts have been analysed in this study,
they form part of a growing body of evidence and allow for
preliminary conclusions to be formed. The results suggest that
although exchange networks expanded at this time, central
Anatolian sources are not commonplace in assemblages east of the
Levant and such long distance exchange was an irregular
occurrence.
In conjunction with previous analysis, the results of this study
suggest there are distinct differences in obsidian procurement
between sites in the Upper Euphrates. Although the sites of Tell
Arpachiyah, Tell Brak and Tell Hamoukar are approximately the
same distance from Meydan dag (ca. 300e350 km), Tell Arpachiyah
shows evidence of greater exploitation of this source. Furthermore,
there are differences between sites that are relatively close to one
another. Tell Hamoukar is only ca. 90 km from Tell Brak, however its
obsidian assemblage represents much higher proportions of
obsidian derived from Bingöl A and contains artefacts of unknown
origin (Khalidi et al., 2009). Occasionally obsidian is present at sites
at greater distances from a source, with obsidian from Pasinler in
northeastern Anatolia excavated Tell Kurdu in the Amuq Valley, but
absent from sites such as Tell Arpachiyah and Tell Brak (Bressy et al.,
2005). Such differences in assemblages and procurement patterns
suggest there is distinct variation in exchange networks and
obsidian circulation even within eastern Anatolia.
4. Conclusion
This study has shown that non-destructive pXRF is capable of
a level of resolution for Anatolian obsidian sources comparable to
results obtained through conventional destructive techniques.
Multivariate analysis (PCA and CVA) of geological samples and
legacy data extracted from the literature can be used to accurately
distinguish between geochemical groups. Although there is a slight
offset between results generated using pXRF and results generated
using alternate analytic techniques, multivariate analysis identifies

Our results show that during the Chalcolithic, Mersin-Yümüktepe procured obsidian from the central Anatolian sources of East
Göllü dag (n ¼ 7) and Nenezi dag (n ¼ 3). Conversely, for approximately the same period, Tell Arpachiyah procured obsidian from
Meydan dag (n ¼ 4) and Bingöl B (n ¼ 1). The obsidian artefacts
(n ¼ 4) from Tell Brak analysed in this study are derived from
eastern Anatolia. Given the similarity in chemical composition of
Bingöl A and Nemrut dag, it was not possible to determine their
precise provenance.
Without a comprehensive analytic regime for an assemblage
that is also well contextualised chronologically, it is not possible to
form definitive conclusions regarding changes in patterns of
obsidian procurement over time. However, the results presented
here indicate that during the Chalcolithic, East Göllü dag and
Nenezi dag continued to be the major sources of obsidian to
communities in central Anatolia. Likewise, obsidian from the Bingöl
mountains and surrounding Lake Van continued to be the major
sources of obsidian to sites east of the Levant. Distinct variations in
the analysed assemblages in terms of proportion and type of
obsidian source suggest different obsidian exchange networks were
in operation during this period.
The dual objectives of this study was to evaluate how well nondestructive pXRF can differentiate Near Eastern obsidian sources,
and whether we could directly compare these results with published datasets. We have demonstrated the viability of nondestructive pXRF for provenancing of Near Eastern obsidian artefacts held in museum collections. We have also shown that the data
generated by this non-destructive technique can be readily
compared with published datasets. This highlights the potential of
pXRF to greatly expand obsidian sample populations both by
accessing museum material from sites which may be no longer
directly accessible and by integrating this data with already published results. For the Near East, non-destructive pXRF will enable
new opportunities for an expanded analysis of patterns in obsidian
procurement and exchange networks.
