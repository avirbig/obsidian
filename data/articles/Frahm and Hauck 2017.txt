A B S T R A C T
Identifying the movement of lithic materials to reconstruct social networks has been a mainstay of research into
Palaeolithic cognition and behavior, but such datasets are often predicated on studies of cherts and similar
siliceous rocks, the origins of which can be difficult to establish conclusively. Yabroud Rockshelter II (YR2) in
southern Syria contained stratified Middle and Upper Palaeolithic layers and, therefore, played a key role in
defining the Levantine Palaeolithic. One obsidian scraper was found in Layer 4, which, via techno-typological
correlations with nearby sites, dates to ∼41–32 ka. Here we report our attribution of this scraper, based on its
elemental analysis, to the Komürcü outcrops at Göllü Dağ volcano in central Turkey, ≥700 km on foot. This
finding has three important implications. First, the earliest transport of obsidian into the Levant is usually
associated with the Epipalaeolithic Natufian cultural complex (∼14.5–11.5 ka); however, the phenomenon
dates farther back to a period following the Middle to Upper Palaeolithic transition. Second, Layer 4 is roughly
contemporaneous with Layer C at Shanidar Cave in northern Iraq, where two obsidian flakes were sourced to
eastern Turkey and/or the Caucasus, suggesting similar scales of interaction across the landscape. Lastly, the
chert assemblage is presumed to be local (≤10 km), but the obsidian scraper suggests that there are other fartravelled artifacts, underscoring that visual identification of cherts might be underestimating the extent of Late
Pleistocene mobility and networks.
1. Introduction
Most arguments regarding cognitive and behavioral differences
between Middle and Upper Palaeolithic hominins – commonly presumed to be Neanderthals and anatomically modern humans (AMHs),
respectively – are predicated on evidence that readily preserves within
the archaeological record. There is, for example, renewed interest in
pyrotechnology of the Pleistocene (e.g. Berna and Goldberg, 2008;
Daniau et al., 2010; Roebroeks and Villa, 2011; Courty et al., 2012;
Twomey, 2013; Bentsen, 2014). Heat treatment of lithic materials and
sophisticated adhesive recipes are thought to reflect advanced cognitive
capabilities (Wadley et al., 2009; Wadley, 2013; Wadley and Prinsloo,
2014). Contrasting the use of symbolic objects in Upper Palaeolithic
(UP) assemblages with Middle Palaeolithic (MP) ones has also been an
important – and hotly debated – line of investigation (e.g., Mellars,
1989, 1996; d'Errico et al., 2003, 2005; Botha, 2008, 2010; Zilhão et al.,
2010; Peresani et al., 2011; Finlayson et al., 2012; Roebroeks et al.,
2012; inter alia). Other research foci include the rate and spread of
technological innovations (Mellars, 1998; Klein, 2003; Wynn and
Coolidge, 2008) and differences in subsistence practices or technologies
(Binford, 1985, 1989; Stiner, 1994; Marean and Assefa, 1999; Villa
et al., 2005; Adler et al., 2004, 2006; inter alia).
A mainstay of such research has been identifying the movement of
lithic raw materials (and other exotic items) across the landscape in
order to investigate the scales of social networks. Given that such
networks can function as a means to buffer against resource and climate
unpredictability via access to distant ecological zones (Whallon, 1989;
McBrearty and Brooks, 2000), a lack of long-distance social connections
has been suggested as one factor in the Neanderthals' demise (Gamble,
1999). The evidence, though, is not conclusive. Villa and Roebroeks
(2014) note that, in European UP assemblages that record distances >
300 km, nearly all involve the transport of shells, not lithic materials.
Many efforts to establish the geographic sources of lithic materials rely
on macroscopic identification of chert and similar siliceous rocks
(Demars, 1982, 1990a, 1990b; Chadelle, 1983; Geneste, 1985; Turq,
1988; inter alia). Thus, claims of long-distance transport are met with
skepticism as a matter of arithmetic. Attributing a chert artifact to
outcrops at a distance of 10 km means that one can reliably discern

From 1930 to 1933, German archaeologist Alfred Rust excavated a
series of rockshelters in the Skifta Valley, near the town of Yabroud
(sometimes transliterated as “Yabrud” or “Jabrud”), ∼60 km northeast
of Damascus. Collectively, these shelters comprise a nearly complete
archaeological record of the Palaeolithic in the Levant (Rust, 1933,
1950). Yabroud Rockshelter I contained Lower Palaeolithic (LP) and
MP layers, Rockshelter II contained MP and UP layers, and Rockshelter
III had Epipalaeolithic (EP; in particular, Natufian) layers. More than
10,000 lithic artifacts were recovered from Rockshelter I alone.
Yabroud Rockshelter II (hereafter “YR2”), which is the focus of our
study, contained a sequence of stratified layers, ∼ 3 m deep, with dense
lithic deposits as well as intact ash concentrations (i.e., combustion
remnants, most likely hearths), attesting to little post-depositional
disturbance. As a result of Rust's excavations, this locality played an
important role in defining the Levantine Palaeolithic (e.g., Bordes,
1955, 1962; Heinzelin, 1966; Solecki and Solecki, 1966, 1986, 2007,
inter alia; Farrand, 1970; Ziffer, 1981; Gilead, 1991; Pastoors et al.,
2008; Bretzke et al., 2011; Bretzke and Conard, 2012; Shimelmitz et al.,
2016). Rust's assemblages are housed at the Institute of Prehistoric
Archaeology, University of Cologne (Germany), where they have long
been a focus of research (Bakdach, 1982; El-Kassem, 2001; Frank, 2004;
Hauck et al., 2014).
In YR2 Layer 4, Rust (1950) reported the presence of a single
obsidian artifact (“das einzige Stück Obsidian aus dem Gesamtinventar,” 80) along with pigment (ochre), adhesive (bitumen), and grinding
stones as well as bone tools, perforated shells, and a hearth. Regarding
the obsidian tool, identified as a scraper (“Obsidianschaber”), he
discussed its potential origins:
Es ist dies das einzige Stück Obsidian aus den Jabruder
Kulturschichten. Dieses vulkanische Glas kommt am Orte nicht
vor, doch stehen in südlicher und südöstlicher Richtung in einer
Entfernung von etwa 100 km große Lavafelder an. Es ist mir
allerdings unbekannt, ob dort Obsidian vorliegt. Jedenfalls läßt sich
das Glasartefakt bisher keineswegs für eine Wanderungsoder
Handelsweghypothese auswerten.
[This is the only piece of obsidian from the Yabroud cultural strata.
This volcanic glass does not occur in the region, but there are lava
fields in the south and southeast at a distance of about 100 km. It is,
however, unknown to me whether there is obsidian. In any case, the
glass artifact cannot be evaluated by any means for a migration or
trade route hypothesis.]
Thus, Rust (1950) recognized that this raw material was non-local;
however, at the time, he had no way of knowing its volcanic origins
were much farther than – and in nearly the opposite direction of – the
Eṣ-Ṣafā basalt flows to the southeast. Ziffer (1981:78–79, 90) also
discusses the obsidian scraper and other finds from YR2 Layer 4:
It is a rich assemblage in lithic material (507 tools and 374 waste
blanks), as well as in bone tools (5 bi-conical points) and pierced
snails... There are also, for the first time in the site, ochre and
bitumen (asphalt) remains, beside some grinding stones probably
used with relation to these materials. Rust suggested the bitumen
remains as a [basis] for handling certain flint tools. There is also a
piece of obsidian in the collection. The flint in general is very
colorful, with red, [violet], brown, and yellow shades dominating...
The intensity of hearths used in all the layers and their position, one
over the other, together with quite a large quantity of lithic material
in all of the layers, the existence of bone-tools, shells, and obsidian
fragments in layer 4, all make Yabroud II [an] intensive settlement,
and maybe a “base-camp” site, during the Upper Palaeolithic period
in Syria.
Gilead (1991:142) similarly noted the significance of these finds
(although he erroneously referred to “a few flakes of obsidian” rather
than a single obsidian scraper):
A few flakes of obsidian from Level 4 of Yabroud II, found with
perforated shells, ochre, bitumen on a few flint tools, and grinding
stones, are also important indicators of long-distance transport...
The only sources of obsidian in the Near East are near Lake Van in
Eastern Anatolia and the Çiftlik area of Central Anatolia, so the
obsidian of Yabroud II had been transported some 600–900 km.
Only during the early Neolithic do we find obsidian artifacts
distributed over comparable distances.
Indeed, the development of obsidian sourcing by Colin Renfrew and
colleagues (Cann and Renfrew, 1964; Renfrew et al., 1965, 1966, 1968;
Dixon et al., 1968; Cann et al., 1968, 1969; Renfrew, 1969) coincided
with the “Neolithic Revolution,” especially the origins of agriculture
and urbanism, as an emerging topic of interest. Hence, obsidian
sourcing became linked to “Neolithization” in the Near East. It was
hoped that the spread of the “Neolithic package” could be traced via the
transport and trade of obsidian. Early results implied that Neolithic
villages were far from isolated. As obsidian moved, so too could
technological or social innovations. Such interpretations were consistent with the extreme rarity of obsidian in Mesopotamian and Levantine
assemblages until EP and Neolithic times (Cauvin, 1991). During the
Palaeolithic, obsidian largely remained near its Anatolian sources
(Kuhn et al., 2015). Consequently, an initial “gold rush” (Özdoğan,
1994:423) of obsidian sourcing in the Near East focused on the
Neolithic, as has more recent work (e.g., Bressy et al., 2005; Carter
et al., 2006, 2013; Poupeau et al., 2010; Orange et al., 2013; Ortega
et al., 2014; Ibáñez et al., 2015; Campbell and Healey, 2016; Frahm
et al., 2016a; Khalidi et al., 2016).
Here we report our findings regarding the origin of YR2 Layer 4
obsidian scraper, a small flake that has been steeply resharpened to the
point where its utility is essentially exhausted and which has experienced post-depositional breakage. We chemically analyzed this scraper
using non-destructive portable X-ray fluorescence (pXRF) and compared its composition to a collection of 230 geo-referenced obsidian
specimens. Our data establish that this scraper matches the Kömürcü
obsidian outcrops on the slopes of the Göllü Dağ volcanic complex in
central Turkey. Therefore, the rockshelter and obsidian source are >
500 km apart linearly (which crosses into the Mediterranean Sea)
and > 700 km on foot following the least-cost path across the landscape. This result has three key implications. First, the earliest transport
of obsidian into the Levant is usually associated with the EP Natufian
cultural complex (∼14.5–11.5 ka; Akkermans and Schwartz, 2003:82;
Khalaily and Valla, 2013; Ridout-Sharpe, 2015), after the emergence of
this phenomenon in Southwest Anatolia (i.e., Öküzini Cave,
∼ 18–14 ka; Carter et al., 2011). The YR2 obsidian scraper, however,
attests that this phenomenon has roots deeper in time, dating much
farther back into the Pleistocene. Second, a number of techno-typological correlations of the YR2 assemblage to those at nearby sites suggest
an age of ∼ 41–32 ka for Layer 4, corresponding to the second half of
Marine Isotope Stage 3. This appears to be roughly contemporaneous to
Layer C at Shanidar Cave in northern Iraq, where obsidian flakes were
sourced by Renfrew and colleagues to eastern Turkey and/or the
Caucasus. This means that at least one of the Shanidar flakes was
moved about the same distance as the YR2 scraper at about the same
time, suggesting similar scales of interaction across the landscape.
Lastly, the remainder of the YR2 lithic assemblage, composed of chert
and related siliceous rocks, is thought to derive from raw materials <
5–10 km away. The obsidian scraper, which was transported > 700

2. Pleistocene obsidian transport in the Near East
In Mesopotamia and the Levant, obsidian artifacts – especially ones
for which their sources have been chemically established – are rare in
Pleistocene contexts. Perhaps the best known case is Shanidar Cave in
northern Iraq (Fig. 1). The site's small obsidian flakes and famous
Neanderthal skeletons are often mentioned together in a way that
implies contemporaneity. Instead, the flakes were found in the EP
(Layer B) and UP (Layer C) strata, not the Neanderthal-bearing MP
(Layer D) stratum (Solecki, 1963). Radiocarbon dating at the time
indicated Layers B and C had ages of ∼ 12–11 ka and ∼ 35–29 ka,
respectively, with a long discontinuity between them (Solecki, 1963).
These ages predate recent advances and calibrations for 14 C determinations, which suggest that Layer C is closer to ∼ 40 ka cal BPHULU near
the bottom and ∼ 33 ka cal BPHULU near the top. Two of the obsidian
flakes from Layer C were analyzed by Renfrew et al. (1966), who
attributed one to their “Group 4c” (either Nemrut Daǧ volcano or the
Bingöl A sources; Fig. 1) and the other flake to their “Group 1e–f” (for
which we now know there are several possibilities, most likely either
the Kars obsidian sources in northeastern Turkey or one of the
numerous Armenian obsidian sources).
At Zarzi Cave, also in northern Iraq, Dorothy Garrod (1930) found
“two small fragments of obsidian,” one of which was identified by
Renfrew et al. (1966) as Group 4c obsidian. There are no radiometric
dates for Zarzi, but Hole and Flannery (1967) proposed an age of
22–12 ka. At nearby Palegawra Cave, thought to be more-or-less
contemporaneous to Zarzi, Braidwood and colleagues unearthed several
obsidian tools (Braidwood and Howe, 1960), but none were chemically
sourced. Bone collagen yielded two uncalibrated 14 C dates of
14.4–13.3 ka (Turnbull and Reed, 1974), which, again when adjusted
using a modern calibration curve, implies an age closer to ∼ 18–16 cal
BP HULU. Given subsequent technical advances (e.g., ultrafiltration
methods, calibration curves), these dates should be considered estimates. To the west in the Levant, there are rare (and usually unsourced)
obsidian artifacts found in EP/“Proto-Neolithic” contexts ∼ 14–12 ka
(e.g., two bladelets at El Kowm I in Syria, one bladelet at Dhour Choueir
in Lebanon; Cauvin, 1991), only becoming more frequent at later
Natufian settlements such as Ain Mallaha (Khalaily and Valla, 2013).
Most Mesopotamian and Levantine sites with obsidian date later
during the Holocene (e.g., Mureybet IA in Syria; Cauvin, 1991). Thus,
Periods 0 and 1 for Near Eastern obsidian distribution, as conceptualized by Cauvin and Chataigner (1998; based largely on the Maison de
l'Orient et de la Méditerranée system), are ∼ 14–12 ka and ∼ 12–10.3 ka,
respectively (Fig. 2c–d). Their synthesis, spanning ∼ 14–6 ka (Fig. 2b;
Chataigner et al., 1998), essentially duplicates the patterns of Neolithic
obsidian distribution observed by Renfrew and colleagues (Fig. 2a;
Dixon et al., 1968). JR2 lies at the confluence of “diffusion” or “contact
zones” (sensu Chataigner et al., 1998 and Dixon et al., 1968, respectively) of Central and Eastern Anatolian obsidians, suggesting that, even
during the Neolithic, obsidian artifacts found near Yabroud could have
originated in either region of Turkey. It should be noted, however, that
there is only one sourced artifact (i.e., a blade from Zarzi Cave) for all of
Period 0 (Fig. 2c) and its timing is debatable. Consequently, the
Neolithic pattern can only be presumed to reflect that specific era

3. Yabroud Rockshelter II (YR2)
YR2 is one of several rockshelters along the Skifta Valley, where the
Wadi Skifta incised into an Eocene limestone plateau at an elevation of
1400 m asl (Fig. 3). The shelter, which opens to the southeast, is
∼ 33 m across and ∼ 12 m from the entrance to back wall. One segment
of the shelter was used as a Roman or Byzantine sepulcher with tombs
cut into the wall. Rust excavated a 4 × 5 m (15 m 2
) segment that
contained ∼ 3 m of stratified deposits (Fig. 4). This portion of YR2
contained the main MP–UP occupation area. The adjoining test trenches
dug by Rust – and a series of small (and unpublished) test pits dug by

exhibit signs of heating, likely due to combustion in the hearths (Fig. 4).
Opinions vary regarding attribution of the YR2 lithic assemblages to
a certain UP “culture” or “industry” (Belfer-Cohen and Goring-Morris,
2003; Kuhn et al., 2003). Levantine UP assemblages are frequently
classified within a framework of two lithic traditions: (1) the blade/
bladelet-based “Ahmarian” tradition and (2) the flake-based “Levantine
Aurignacian” tradition (e.g., Gilead, 1991; Gilead, 1981; Marks, 1981;
Bergman, 1987, 2003; Belfer-Cohen and Goring-Morris, 2003). Technological features considered Ahmarian include: one reduction strategy; single-platform cores; unidirectional reduction to remove straight
blades/bladelets; laterally retouched blades, sometimes shaped into “el
Wad” points; and, in the northern Levant, reduction with opposed
striking platforms (Azoury, 1986; Davidzon and Goring-Morris, 2003;
Kuhn et al., 2003; Marks, 2003). Levantine Aurignacian features
include a variety of reduction strategies to create flakes, blades, and

The YR2 Layer 4 assemblage includes elements of both Ahmarian
(e.g. abundant blades and bladelets, el Wad points) and Levantine
Aurignacian (e.g., twisted blades and bladelets) traditions (Hauck et al.,
2014). Specifically, it has two major components: (1) blades and
bladelets along with their associated cores and debitage and (2)
endscapers produced from thick blades or, preferably, flakes removed
from cores (Hauck et al., 2014). It should be kept in mind, however,
that the coarse nature of Rust's excavation likely biased the technotypological proportions found in the collected assemblage (Hauck et al.,
2014). This mix of reduction strategies and tool types has led to
different classifications for the Layer 4 assemblage. Belfer-Cohen and
Goring-Morris (2003) attribute Layer 4 to the Levantine Aurignacian
tradition, while Schyle (1992) and Kuhn et al. (2003) assign it to the
Ahmarian. Bretzke and Conard (2012) note that Layer 4′s emphasis on
blades and unidirectional reduction favor an Ahmarian classification,
while carinated scrapers, twisted blades, and multiple reduction
strategies easily fit the Levantine Aurignacian. Readers interested in a
detailed techno-typological analysis of the Layer 4 lithics are referred to
Hauck et al. (2014).
At present, the YR2 chronology is somewhat imprecise. Rust did not
collect samples for 14 C dating, of course. Thus, the dates for YR2 are
based on techno-typological correlations to nearby UP sites while
acknowledging that a taxonomic approach is far from perfect. Most of
the correlations have been made to Ksar Akil in Lebanon, ∼ 100 km
west (Azoury, 1986; Bergman, 1987; Ohnuma, 1988; Williams and
Bergman, 2010). Ziffer (1981) attributes YR2 Layers 4 and 5 to the
“Levantine Aurignacian B II” of Ksar Akil Layers 8 and 9, which
corresponds to Phase 4. We examined the YR2 Layer 4 assemblage
(Hauck et al., 2014) and concluded that it best correlates to Ksar Akil
Phase 3. One set of Bayesian models for Ksar Akil's radiometric dates
has yielded a Phase 3 start ∼ 40–35 ka (Douka et al., 2013), with a
large spread due to uncertainties in the models' parameters. A more
recent set of Bayesian models places the start of Phase 3 at ∼41–39 ka
and the end near ∼ 39–37 ka (Bosch et al., 2015). If one instead favors
Ziffer's (1981) attribution nearly four decades ago, YR2 Layer 4 still
remains older than ∼ 36–32 ka (Layer 6, Bosch et al., 2015).
Therefore, a series of correlations and dates points to ∼ 41–32 ka
for YR2 Layer 4, making it essentially contemporaneous with Layer C of
Shanidar Cave at ∼40–33 ka. Given that, by modern standards, Rust's
excavation methods were coarse, greater temporal resolution is almost
certainly unrealistic. Pastoors et al. (2008) propose that, out of an
abundance of caution, Rust's layers should be regarded as artificial units
that reflect a general chronostratigraphic trend rather than discrete
occupation levels. Therefore, a highly conservative chronological
assessment for the scraper would ascribe it to the second half of Marine
Isotope Stage (MIS) 3 (∼ 60–57 to ∼ 29–27 ka), which would remain
valid even if this tool was an intrusion from a layer above or below.
It can still be argued that, despite the coarse nature of Rust's
excavations, the layers were, to a large extent, in situ with limited postdepositional disturbance. For example, Gilead (1991:128) stated, based
on a reexamination of the YR2 lithic assemblages at Cologne, that
Layers 4 and 2 “are clearly different from Layers 3 and 1” in terms of
their techno-typological products (e.g., “Layers 4 and 5 are dominated
by blades”). Pastoors et al. (2008) reexamined Layers 10 to 5 and
concluded that the assemblage exhibited typical features of the MP–UP
transition in the Levant. The observed hearths, they propose, further
bolster Rust's interpretation of the layers (at least where the hearths
were preserved and encountered but not necessarily throughout the
entire shelter). Pastoors et al. (2008:54) also suggest that, with the
exception of small debris not collected by Rust, “the reduction
sequences within these assemblages are complete... which in turn
supports the idea of homogenous assemblages.” Furthermore, they
(2008:62) “suggest... with great caution” that the sedimentary change
between YR2 Layers 7 and 6 might correlate with a climate shift (e.g.,
the Heinrich 4 event, ∼ 42 ka), but they “cannot exclude local
explanations for this observation.” Ash lenses, as illustrated in Rust's
(1950) section (Fig. 4), and in situ lithics were observed onsite by
Pastoors et al. (2008) in exposed profiles of the Columbia excavations.
Finally, after decades of study (Ziffer, 1981; Bakdach, 1982; El-Kassem,
2001; Frank, 2004; Pastoors et al., 2008; Bretzke and Conard, 2012;
Hauck et al., 2014), there are no known inter-layer refits. Considered
together, the lines of evidence suggest that Rust's layers reflect some
degree of chronostratigraphic reality and integrity.
4. Chert and its potentially local sources
Before focusing on the only obsidian artifact, it is worth reemphasizing that there were also 506 chert tools and 374 chert flakes,
fragments, and cores in Layer 4. That is, 99.8% of the tools are chert.
As mentioned in the Introduction, Ziffer (1981:90) noted that the chert
was “very colorful” and included red, violet, yellow, and brown shades.
El-Kassem's (2001) macroscopic analysis of the YR2 assemblage suggested, based on an abundance of weathered cortical surfaces, that most
of the chert derived from secondary deposits. Rust (1950) noted the
existence of such secondary deposits within the vicinity. Citing the
work of Solecki and Solecki (1995) at Rockshelter I, Shea (2003:355)
claims the occupants were “selective, travelling long distances to collect
more siliceous flints and bypassing low quality flint nodules eroding
from the bedrock in front of the caves.” Neither Solecki and Solecki
(1995) nor Shea (2003), however, specify what source(s) or distance
they had in mind. El-Kassem (2001) proposes that chert from both
rockshelters largely originated from a secondary deposit ∼ 5 km to the
northeast. Pastoors et al. (2008) observe that a few artifacts within the
lithic assemblage exhibit a chalky cortex indicative of primary outcrops. Various chert outcrops occur in Eocene marls and Cretaceous
limestones < 10 km from YR2 (Wolfart, 1966; Dodonov et al., 2006).
Hence, Pastoors et al. (2008:50) suggest that, “although we cannot rule
out that some raw materials were transported over longer distances, the
overwhelming majority was certainly collected from the immediate
vicinity and is therefore of purely local [≤ 20 km] origin.” In short,
with the exception of obsidian, lithic materials appear to have been
collected primarily or exclusively from secondary sources (i.e., alluvial
deposits, deflated surfaces) in the vicinity, and neither material
availability nor transport are assumed to be constraints on the lithic
technology at YR2.
5. Methods and materials
The YR2 obsidian tool was analyzed using portable X-ray fluorescence analysis (pXRF) and compared to a collection of obsidian
reference specimens to identify its origin.
5.1. The YR2 obsidian tool
The lone obsidian artifact at YR2 (Fig. 5) is a small tool:
28 × 25 × 10 mm in size and 6.5 g in mass. Rust (1950) called it the
only occurrence of a rundschaber (round scraper) at the site. It has also
been classified as an endscraper-on-flake, others of which are made of
chert in Layer 4 (Hauck et al., 2014). Within such a framework, chert
endscrapers tend to be larger (45 ± 9 mm in maximal dimension) but
not, on average, thicker (10 ± 4 mm) than the obsidian one. Rust's
illustration (Fig. 5b) is oriented improperly and tends to exaggerate
features around the perimeter of the tool. As noted in Section 3, there is
abundant edge damage in the Layer 4 lithic assemblage, adding to the
ambiguities of distinguishing fine retouch from edge damage, especially
for a more brittle material than chert. A conservative classification is
that the tool is a retouched flake, for which the original striking
platform is absent. It has been worked with steep scalar-stepped-backed
retouch, so steep that its utility is essentially exhausted. The tool looks
E. Frahm, T.C. Hauck Journal of Archaeological Science: Reports 13 (2017) 415–427
420more like a déjeté or transverse scraper when it is oriented properly
(Fig. 5c), but a more general classification of “scraper” seems appropriate given the degree to which the tool has been resharpened to the
point of exhaustion and damaged post-depositionally. The dorsal surface has a coarse surface, which may be cortex sensu stricto or a
fracture plane due to a weakness or thermal stresses within the obsidian
deposit as it cooled. A flow band within the obsidian can also be
observed, and at least once, it affected the propagation of a crack. The
obsidian scraper is likely smaller than chert ones due to a greater degree
of reduction over an extended use-life during long-distance transport
from its source.
5.2. Portable XRF procedures
The YR2 scraper as well as the obsidian reference specimens were
analyzed with a Thermo Scientific Niton XL3t 950 GOLDD instrument.
To produce an incident X-ray beam, this instrument has a 2-W, Aganode X-ray tube. Its settings (e.g., its voltage and current) can vary in
conjunction with a series of built-in X-ray filters to optimally fluoresce
different portions of the periodic table. Our elements of interest (and
other spectral features pertinent to data processing) were measured
using the “main” X-ray filter and its associated tube settings (40 kV and
≤ 50 μA). To measure the characteristic X-rays emitted from a specimen, this instrument has a 25-mm 2 Si drift detector with an energy
resolution ≤ 155 eV in practice. The incident beam is ordinarily ~ 8mm in diameter (~ 50 mm 2
); however, this instrument's built-in
collimator (i.e., “small spot” mode) can reduce the beam size to
3 mm (~ 7 mm 2 ). The collimator was used due to many small specimen
sizes in our obsidian reference collection (Section 5.4). The instrument
was operated while mounted in a portable Thermo Scientific SmartStand, which, together with a built-in camera, enabled precise specimen positioning. Each measurement took 60 s.
Measured X-rays must be “corrected” for various phenomena that
occur in a specimen (e.g., X-ray absorption and attenuation, secondary
X-ray fluorescence). Our study used the fundamental parameters (FP)
approach, which has been routinely used in certain XRF applications for
decades (de Boer and Brouwer, 1990). After interlaboratory testing,
Heginbotham et al. (2010) showed that the most accurate XRF
instruments used FP correction in conjunction with standards for
calibration (i.e., what they labelled “FP with standards”). Such an
approach was used here. The instrument's factory-set calibration, which
is intended to be applicable across a wide range of concentrations, can
yield accurate values for certain elements in obsidian but inaccurate
values for others. Further work is needed to “fine tune” the calibration
for obsidian.
Here our pXRF measurements were further calibrated using a series
of obsidian specimens that were previously characterized using multiple analytical techniques. This approach is favored by the
Archaeometry Laboratory at the University of Missouri Research
Reactor (MURR) (Glascock, 2011; Ferguson, 2012). Our data here were
calibrated using a set of 22 obsidians from Southwest Asia (Armenia,
Georgia, and Turkey). These specimens were previously analyzed with
(1) neutron activation analysis (NAA) and (2) energy-dispersive XRF
(EDXRF) at MURR using their standard protocols (Glascock et al., 2007,
2008) and with (3) electron microprobe analysis (EMPA) at the
University of Minnesota using published procedures (Frahm, 2012a).
Use of this obsidian standard set has already been demonstrated to yield
high reproducibility and accuracy in earlier publications (Frahm,
2014a; Frahm and Feinberg, 2015).

This study follows a typical XRF approach to element selection

2008, 2011). Consequently, they are among the most often used
elements for XRF-based obsidian sourcing (e.g., Shackley, 1988,
1995; Ferguson and Skinner, 2003; Carter and Shackley, 2007; Craig
et al., 2007; Speakman et al., 2007; De Francesco et al., 2008; Ogburn

5.4. Obsidian reference collection
For this study, the tool's composition was not compared to published
data in the literature. Instead, our collection of geo-referenced obsidian
specimens from across Southwest Asia, spanning from the Aegean to the
Caucasus, was also analyzed using pXRF. The collection, composed
primary of small (< 1 cm) specimens, has been mounted in epoxy discs
and polished flat for EMPA-based obsidian studies (Frahm and
Feinberg, 2013a, 2013b; Frahm, 2014b; Frahm et al., 2016a). In this
set are 595 obsidian specimens from central Turkey (often termed
“Central Anatolia”) and eastern Turkey (“Eastern Anatolia”). It includes, for example, more than a hundred specimens from the Anatolian
sources of peralkaline obsidian: Bingöl A and the Nemrut Daǧ source
complex (Frahm, 2012b). The remainder of these specimens are
calcalkaline obsidians, which occur throughout Turkey and the rest of
Southwest Asia. About half of the specimens were sufficiently large for
EMPA, but the pXRF analyses, even with a small-spot collimator,
included too much of the mounting material for reliable data (i.e.,
the data also reflected the epoxy). The result was a set of 230 specimens
for comparison to the scraper (these data are all available in the
Supplementary material).
6. Sourcing results
Fig. 6 shows scatterplots of four elements – Rb, Sr, Zr, and Fe – for
the pXRF analyses of the YR2 obsidian scraper as well as 230 geological
obsidian specimens. The plots demonstrate that this scraper matches
the Kömürcü obsidian outcrops on the eastern slopes of the Göllü Dağ
volcanic complex (Fig. 7). Therefore, the site and obsidian source are –
linearly – > 500 km apart; however, that path crosses into the Mediterranean Sea. On foot, the shortest possible path over the landscape
is > 700 km (Fig. 1). At Göllü Dağ (called Çiftlik by Renfrew and
colleagues), there are several chemically distinct obsidians. According
to one study (Binder et al., 2011), there are seven or eight different
obsidians at this volcano. Obsidian outcrops near Komürcü village and
east of Kayırlı village – known in the literature as “Komürcü” and “East
Kayırlı,” respectively – can be difficult to discern. As a result, their
obsidians are sometimes combined into a single chemical “type” called
“East Göllü Dağ” (Poidevin, 1998; Carter and Shackley, 2007). Subtle
but measurable differences between them do, however, exist (e.g.,
Poupeau et al., 2005; Hancock and Carter, 2010; Binder et al., 2011),
and pXRF can make this distinction (Frahm and Feinberg, 2013a).
There has been, over the decades, variability in elemental data for
Komürcü obsidian. Such variability is, to an extent, inevitable given
differences in analytical techniques and procedures. Hancock and
Carter (2010) observe that, “although analytical chemistry is not a
democratic process, the agreement of specific elemental concentration
data between (among) independent analytical techniques adds credibility to the relative accuracy of their numbers” (245). Table 1 shows
15 datasets for Komürcü obsidian from a variety of analytical techniques. The overall mean values have excellent agreement (within one
standard deviation) with our pXRF data for Komürcü obsidian.
7. Discussion
It is worth stressing at this point that, although YR2 reflects an early
instance of long-distance movement of Göllü Dağ obsidian, obsidian
was used by mobile foragers throughout the Palaeolithic when near the
sources, both in Anatolia (Slimak, 2004; Slimak et al., 2008; Kuhn et al.,
2015) and in adjacent regions (Adler et al., 2012, 2014; Frahm et al.,
2016b). These populations often produced entire assemblages out of
obsidian so that its transport over the local landscape can reflect day-today patterns of mobility and social networking. Long-distance transport, on the other hand, does not necessarily occur through such
frequent or routine encounters, but it remains archaeologically visible
via “exotic” materials, such as far-flung obsidian artifacts found in the
Levant. These cases, however, are rare, meaning that we are left to
discuss small sample sizes, such as a single tool that was moved at least
700 km (although Rust did not collect the small lithic size classes at
YR2, so the presence of, for example, obsidian resharpening flakes
cannot be addressed).
Regarding the processes that carried this singular artifact at least
700 km from Komürcü to YR2, it seems more likely that the scraper
passed from individual to individual – and, therefore, that neighboring
groups were, at least periodically, in contact across this area – than that
the very same individual visited both the source and site, although this
is not inconceivable. Nor do we know how long it took for this tool to
travel this distance. It could have taken a year or perhaps generations. A
lack of obsidian discovered at intermediate and nearby UP sites (e.g.,
Ksar Akil) implies that it was not a common occurrence. Consequently,
the presence of Göllü Dağ obsidian at YR2 has three key implications
for the creation and maintenance of Near Eastern social networks.
First, the earliest transport of Anatolian obsidian into the Levant is
widely considered to be as a phenomenon associated with the EP
Natufian complex. Instead, the YR2 obsidian scraper dates to a period
following the Middle to Upper Palaeolithic transition. We have thus far
not specified which Late Pleistocene hominin(s) inhabited YR2. No
human fossils were recovered by Rust or the Columbia University team.
MP assemblages are generally thought to reflect Neanderthals, while UP
assemblages are assumed to reflect AMHs. These associations may,
though, be subject to challenge, especially in a region such as the
Levant where Neanderthals and AMHs coexisted. The last dated
Neanderthal in the Levant was found at Geula Cave B, Mount Carmel,
near Haifa. Charred bones yielded a 14 C age of ∼ 44 ± 2 ka
(Wreschner, 1967), but a date from this time must be regarded with
caution. Modern radiocarbon techniques would likely yield a date
closer to ∼48 ± 2 ka cal BPHULU . Consequently, archaeologists working in the Levant tend to cite a date range, frequently ∼45–35 ka (e.g.,
Shea, 2002), for the Neanderthals' disappearance in the region. Such a
range, though, overlaps with our age estimate of ∼ 41–32 ka for YR2
Layer 4. Perhaps the strongest speculation again stems from comparison
to Ksar Akil. Two AMH fossils, nicknamed “Egbert” (Ksar Akil 1) and
“Ethelruda” (Ksar Akil 2), were found in the layers beneath Phase 3
(Douka et al., 2013; Bosch et al., 2015). Egbert, in the Early Ahmarian
Phase, is dated to 43.3–42.8 ka, whereas Ethelruda dates beyond the
14 C range (> 45.9 ka) in the Initial UP Phase (Bosch et al., 2015).
Accordingly, our hypothesis is that Layer 4 reflects AMH occupations;
however, either fossils or precise dating are needed in order to
conclusively establish which hominin(s) occupied YR2 during this time.
Second, given the limited chronological resolution of these excavations, the best available age estimates place the YR2 Layer 4 obsidian
artifact as essentially contemporaneous with those in Layer C of
Shanidar Cave (∼ 40–33 ka). They might not have been truly contemporaneous, but the temporal resolution with which we must work
allows no greater precision. Renfrew et al. (1966) attributed one of the
Shanidar Cave flakes to Nemrut Dağ volcano (∼ 310 km linearly,
≥ 450 km on foot; Fig. 1) or the Bingöl A deposits (∼430 km linearly,
≥ 460 km on foot). The other flake matched their “Group 1e–f”
obsidian type, so it was transported ≥ 500 km linearly and ≥700 km

It is also worth considering far-travelled Pleistocene obsidian
artifacts outside the Near East as a means for comparison. Moutsiou
(2012) tabulated long-distance obsidian transfers in Eastern Africa
during the Early, Middle, and Later Stone Age (n = 52). During each
period, there was one or two instances of obsidian artifacts at linear
distances of ∼ 120–200 km from their geological sources (n = 2 for the
Early and Later Stone Age; n = 1 for the Middle Stone Age; Moutsiou,
2012: Fig. 1). She also tabulated data from central and southeastern
Europe, where Palaeolithic populations used the “Carpathian” and
Aegean obsidian sources. In the MP, one artifact was moved a linear
distance of ∼ 210 km. The rest (n = 9) were < 125 km from their
sources. Transfer distances increased during the UP: there are six cases
of artifacts at linear distances ∼ 400–500 km from the sources
(Moutsiou, 2012: Fig. 1). These sites each have a single obsidian
artifact and, to varying degrees, younger ages than our estimate for
YR2 Layer 4 (e.g., Aggsbach B in Austria: ∼ 27 ka, Bacho Kiro in
Bulgaria: ∼ 29 ka, Kraków-Zwierzyniec I in Poland: ∼ 31 ka, KremsHundssteig in Austria: ∼33–27 ka). Hence, UP central and southeastern Europe exhibits a trend in long-distance obsidian transport
similar to that in the UP Levant and Mesopotamia but ostensibly during
a later time. Based on the literature, there appears to be few instances of
obsidian transfers ∼ 120–200 km in Eastern Africa (Moutsiou, 2012),
but new data for Later Stone Age forager-fisher and pastoralist
interactions in Kenya contradict this interpretation and reveal such
transfers were frequent (Frahm et al., 2017), although across much
shorter distances than that reported here between Göllü Dağ volcano
and YR2.
Finally, it is particularly striking when a scraper, made of obsidian
from a far-flung source, is surrounded by what appears to be functionally equivalent tools made of raw materials available < 10 km from the
site. The presence of the obsidian scraper suggests a potential – and,
perhaps, the great likelihood – for far-travelled chert tools at YR2. The
mobile foragers who occupied YR2 most likely would not have arrived
at the rockshelter empty-handed with the exception of a single small,
nearly exhausted scraper. Even if this tool had been passed from
individual to individual, it might well have been acquired at a
considerable distance from YR2. That is not to suggest that the entire
lithic toolkit of its owner was far-travelled or even non-local. Nor is this
issue limited only to visual chert sourcing. It is challenging even by
sophisticated geochemical means to assign chert artifacts from
Cretaceous limestone beds (such as occur in the Levant) to a particular
outcrop (Ekshtain et al., 2017). Consequently, “exotic” chert artifacts
could be scattered among those from local outcrops. Therefore,
reconstructions of Late Pleistocene hunter-gatherer mobility and social
networks based on visual – and even geochemical – identification of
chert sources may only be minimum estimates that considerably
underestimate the geographic extent of such phenomena.
8. Conclusions
Using the movement of lithic materials to recognize long-distance
social networks has been a mainstay of studies into cognition and
behavior; however, datasets are often predicated on cherts and similar
siliceous rocks, the origins of which can be challenging to establish
conclusively. YR2 offers the rare opportunity to chemically determine
the geographic origin of a far-travelled tool in a Palaeolithic context.
Rust (1950) recognized that the obsidian tool unearthed at YR2 was
non-local, but he had no way of knowing that it came from farther away
– and in a nearly opposite direction – than he imagined. Using pXRF
and 230 reference specimens, we show that this scraper matches the
Komürcü obsidian outcrops on the eastern slopes of Göllü Dağ volcano
in central Turkey, ≥ 700 km on foot. Unfortunately, the chronology of
YR2 is imprecise, and it will remain so for the foreseeable future. For
now, we must rely on techno-typological correlations that suggest an
age of ∼ 41–32 ka for YR2 Layer 4 and, in turn, this obsidian scraper,
which was discarded at the end of its extended use-life. This finding has
three key implications for the creation and maintenance of Near Eastern
networks during a period following the Middle to Upper Palaeolithic
transition. First, the earliest transport of obsidian from Anatolia into the
Levant is often regarded as a phenomenon associated with the EP
Natufian complex. The YR2 scraper attests, however, that it has much
deeper temporal roots, reflecting the behavior of hominins during the
last half of MIS 3. Second, YR2 Layer 4 seems contemporaneous to
Layer C at Shanidar Cave in Iraq, where two obsidian flakes were
sourced to eastern Turkey and/or the Caucasus. Thus, at least one of
these flakes was moved about the same distance as the YR2 scraper,
suggesting that there were other large-scale contacts across the Near
East at this time. That is, ostensibly concurrent obsidian artifacts at YR2
and Shanidar Cave could be elements of a larger phenomenon. Third,
the rest of the YR2 assemblage, composed of chert and similar siliceous
