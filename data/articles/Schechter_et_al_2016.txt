at Ein Z ippori, Israel
H.C. Schechter, A. G opher, N. Getzov, E . R ice, A. Yaroshevich and I. M ilevski
Abstract: A total of 266 obsidian items were found during four seasons of excavation at the Late Prehistoric site of Ein Zippori,
Israel. Most of the assemblage was assigned to the Wadi Rabah culture. The obsidian artefacts originated in three Anatolian sources
– Cappadocian Göllü Dağ and eastern Anatolian Bingöl A and B. Several colour and texture differences were noticed on some of
the items from the different sources. The presence of items such as flakes, blades, core trimming elements, cores and tools reflect
some on-site production. The high ratio of tools and bladelet segments to other debitage items may indicate an import of prepared
bladelets and tools to the site as well. Pressure was dominantly used, probably alongside percussion, and signs of non-specialized
production may be apparent. Tool design and retouch characteristics point to a highly standardized transverse arrowhead production
(or import), several different bead-making traditions, and a distinction between the shaping (retouch) of blade and bladelet tools,
perhaps indicating a distinction in use.

INTRODuCTION
The site of Ein Zippori is located in the lower Galilee, less
than 5 km west of Nazareth, on the southern bank of Nahal
Zippori and the northern slopes of Giv’at Rabi (Jebel el Ayin)
(fig. 1). A salvage excavation of the site was conducted by the
Israel Antiquities Authority between the years 2011-2014,
along both sides of route 79 (fig. 2).
The excavations exposed a large, multi-layered site
(Milevski et al. 2014;1 Milevski and Getzov 2014). Several late
prehistoric settlement sequences were defined:
– Pre-Pottery Neolithic (PPN) B-C, dated to the 10 th –9 th
millennia cal. BP, were found in several areas (general
Stratum X);

The archaeological remains
The Ein Zippori Wadi Rabah layers are massive and show
several stratigraphic units. Architectural remains are fragmented with plans for only single buildings. Large assemblages of pottery vessels, flint tools and flint industry waste
were found, alongside a rich ground-stone assemblage and
imagery items such as palettes, decorated bones and seals.2
The fauna is relatively rich, containing ovicaprines, pigs and
cattle. Residue analyses conducted on several pottery vessels
of the WR and EBA layers indicate that olive oil was probably
produced and stored in these vessels (Namdar et al. 2015).
Burials were found in several areas within the site. A number
of newborns or babies were buried in pottery vessels or parts
of them. A fine stone bowl with a string of tens of stone beads
was found as a probable offering deposit of one of these burials
in area E.

PRESENTATION
OBSIDIAN IN THE SOUTHERN LEVANT
Obsidian first appears in the Southern Levant in the
Natufian (Khalaily and Valla 2013), can be found in small
quantities in PPNA sites3 and as larger assemblages during the
PPNB,4 when bladelet pressure debitage technology is also
introduced to the Levant (Gopher et al. 1998). Obsidian presence declines in the later parts of the Pre-Pottery Neolithic5
and becomes rare in the Early Pottery Neolithic.6 Obsidian
becomes common again in later Pottery Neolithic sites WR.7 It
is not however, distributed evenly between sites and may be
concentrated in exchange centres (Garfinkel 2008 and 2011;
Prausnitz 1970; Schechter et al. 2013). Obsidian again
decreases at the beginning of the Chalcolithic Ghasullian (e.g.,
Gilat in Yellin et al. 1996) and practically disappears before
the EBA (Garfinkel 2011 but see Rosen et al. 2005).
Obsidian items found in the southern Levant had to have
been imported, as there are no obsidian sources in the region.
The nearest sources are located in Central (Göllü Dağ, Nenezi
Dağ) or Eastern (Bingöl A, Bingöl B, Nemrut Dağ, Meydan
Dağ, Sarikamiş, Arteni) Anatolia and only obsidian from these
sources was found in the Southern Levant to date (Perlman
and Yellin 1980; Delerue 2007). Obsidian found in PPN sites
in the region generally came from central Anatolian sources,
while obsidian from eastern Anatolian sources appeared during the PPN and greatly increased during the PN (Perlman and
Yellin 1980; Yellin and Frachtenberg 1992; Delerue 2007;
Delerue et al. 2012). Core issues concerning the movement and
trade of obsidian in the greater region were first discussed by
Renfrew et al. (1966 and 1968), who constructed the ‘downthe-line’ model to explain the apparent decrease in obsidian
quantity as distance from the source grows. However, as they
pointed out, the large assemblage from Jericho (CrowfootPayne 1983: Tables 2-8) does not fit this model, as is the case

RESEARCH OBJECTIVES
In this paper, we present the obsidian assemblage from Ein
Zippori. Different technological issues concerning bladelet debitage by pressure and on-site production are discussed, as well as
different typological issues concerning obsidian tools. A chemical provenance analysis is presented and implications concerning the origins of the obsidian items are discussed. The obsidian
assemblage is further reviewed in light of diachronic and synchronic comparisons – both in light of earlier data known in the
region and as part of the WR culture in comparison to other WR
assemblages. Conclusions concerning the place and importance
of this obsidian assemblage are drawn at the end of the discussion. This obsidian assemblage enriches our knowledge of
southern Levantine obsidian during the WR, reinforces previous
conclusions and provides insight into local site-hierarchy concerning obsidian distribution and production.
DATINg THE OBSIDIAN ASSEmBLAgE
fROm EIN ZIppORI
Through the 2014 excavation season, 266 obsidian items
were found at Ein Zippori. Most of them were found in WR
layers (71.8%), some in EBA IB layers (18.8%), and a few in
general EBA (3.1%) or top-soil (6.3%) layers. Since obsidian is
generally not found in EBA sites in the Southern Levant, and is
common in WR sites, it is safe to suggest that the obsidian at
Ein Zippori was used in the WR settlement, and is residual in
the EBA layers, due to post-depositional mixing processes.
Single items may be dated to the earlier Pottery Neolithic on a
typological basis (an Amuq type arrowhead and perhaps a
spear-head), and their chronological assignment is discussed
below. We therefore analyse all the obsidian items as part of
one assemblage assigned to the WR culture.

TECHNOLOgY
A tendency towards bladelet8 production and consumption is
apparent from the assemblage and some technological tendencies are noticed. Of the 97 bladelets found, 90.7% were produced
by pressure (fig. 3: 1), characterized by abraded punctiform
butts, flat or small bulbs, parallel sides and arrises, no ventral
ripples, distal curve, etc. (Inizan et al. 1999: 76-78; Pelegrin
2012); and the rest were produced by percussion (Pelegrin 2000)
or show mixed marks. The dominant use of pressure to produce
obsidian bladelets is wide spread in the region (Binder 2007)
and well known from many sites in the area.9
8. A bladelet has a maximum width of 12 mm, maximum length of 50 mm
and has at least 2:1 length-to-width ratio when complete. Fragments with
characteristically parallel sides and arrises were also determined as bladelets.
9. E.g. Hagoshrim in Gopher et al. 1998 and 2011; Schechter et al. 2013;
Nahal Zehora in Delerue et al. 2012.
Very few of the bladelets are complete (N=7, 7.2%) and
those measure 15-40 mm in length. Most bladelets are broken.
Bladelet width ranges from 4 mm to 12 mm, yet 86.6% cluster
ca 6-10 mm, averaging at 7.8 mm. Most bladelets are 2 mm
(52.6%) or 1 mm (38.1%) thick, and few (9.3%) are 3-4 mm
thick. Of the bladelet fragments, 56.7% are medial, 37.8% are
proximal and 5.6% are distal; 23.7% of the bladelets are lateral,
produced from the sides of the debitage plane, while 76.3% are
central. Experiments show that lateral bladelets average at
18-28% in a single production sequence from a bladelet core
(Astruc et al. 2007), and their frequency at Ein Zippori supports on-site production.
The direction of production of the bladelet could be noticed
on 77.3% of the bladelets; of them 88.0% were produced from
unipolar cores and 12.0% from cores with opposed platforms.
Bidirectional scars appear at Ein Zippori on bladelets and
cores (fig. 3: 3). This was also noticed at Hagoshrim (Gopher et
al. 2011; Schechter et al. 2013), Damishliyya I (Astruc et al.
2007) and Tel Kurdu (Healey 2004: Fig. 10.4). Bidirectional
debitage by pressure seems to be “illogical”, as put by
M.-L. Inizan (1999: 78), thus the reduction process probably
involved intermittent percussion and corrective removals, from
primary and opposed striking platforms, resulting in bidirectional appearance.
Six cores were found at the site, two of which are broken.
Of the complete cores, each has a different shape-pencil shape
(fig. 3: 4), pyramidal (fig. 3: 2), trapezoid, prismatic—which
are expected morphologies of pressure bladelet cores (Inizan
et al. 1999: 78 and fig. 31). According to the scars on the debitage planes, all cores were used to produce bladelets and one
was formerly used to produce blades, whose scars still appear
on the back of the core. The production method on all cores
was pressure, yet on two cores signs of terminal production by
percussion appear. Two cores had changed orientation—the
striking platform and debitage plane were moved to a different
face of the core—, in both cases flipped over so that the base
became the new striking platform, resulting in the appearance
of bidirectional cores (fig. 3: 2). Core maintenance includes
abraded sides and retouch along or across back (fig. 3: 2-3)—
presumably to dull sharp edges and ease handling (Marder
2002: 184). Retouch appears along the line of points of percussion, presumably as preparation for further production, while
abrasion on the striking platform presumably helped stabilize
the punch/crutch (Binder 2007).
To summarize, the obsidian chaîne opératoire was aimed
at producing bladelets by pressure from cores, which have
abraded striking platforms and abraded, retouched or flat
backs, sides and bases. The cores may have formerly been used

Different types of Core Trimming Elements (CTE) were
found in the assemblage, and they are generally typical standard bladelet production CTEs. The 23 CTEs include: nine
overshot10 bladelets (fig. 4: 5), two overshot blades (fig. 4: 2),
one overshot flake (which is a core tablet, fig. 4: 3), three ridge11
blade/lets (fig. 4: 1, 6), one core tablet12 (fig. 4: 4) and seven

1, 6). In all cases the ‘mirror-flat’ sides meet at the ridge at a
right angle, which is surprisingly accurate. Precise right angles
were also recorded on cores at Ein Zippori and are a striking
phenomenon at Hagoshrim (Gopher et al. 2011; Schechter et
al. 2013). The tablet is a successful removal of the striking
platform, after several failed attempts resulting in multiple
hinges, struck from the centre of the debitage plane (fig. 4: 4).
All the items included here in ‘other CTEs’ are different forms
of removal of parts of the debitage plane. They may be removed
from the proximal, medial or distal parts of the core, and all
show removals in mixed directions, mostly transverse. A typical kind of removal in the standard bladelet industry is an item
removed transversely, across the proximal part of the front of
the core, removing part of the striking platform and part of the
debitage plane, where they meet along the points of percussion
(fig. 4: 7). Two such items were found at Ein Zippori.

TOOLS
The typological division of the 98 tools found at Ein
Zippori is presented in Table 2. The most dominant tool group
at the site is retouched items, accounting together for 71.4% of
the tools. Of them 62.9% are retouched bladelets (fig. 5: 1-2),
25.7% are retouched blades, 10.0% are retouched flakes and
one item (1.4%) is a retouched chunk. The percentage (out of
all the tools) of retouched blades (18.4%) and retouched bladelets (44.9%) exceeds the percentage of their unretouched counterparts from the assemblage as a whole (blades 2.3% and
bladelets 36.5%, table 2), presumably reflecting a degree of
selection in favor of blade/let blanks for tools. Not only simple
blade/lets were chosen as blanks—two ridge blades and two
overshot bladelets were also made into tools. Among the
retouched flakes we include a worked flake, rubbed and
abraded all around, which may be a bead preform or used so
that all its retouch was abraded (fig. 5: 3).

Retouch on blades and bladelets
The character of the retouch may be compared to show differences and similarities in the two production trajectories. The
location of retouch on blades is usually bilateral (80%), while on
bladelets it may be bilateral (56.4%) or unilateral (41.0%). Most
retouch on both blades and bladelets tends to be direct (struck
from the ventral face towards the dorsal face: 53.3% for blades,
43.6% for bladelets), yet other types of retouch do appear. Inverse
retouch (struck from the dorsal face towards the ventral face)
appears more on bladelets (10.3%) than on blades (6.7%).
Alternate retouch (direct on one lateral edge and inverse on the
other) appears equally on both blades (20%) and bladelets
(20.5%), while more of the bladelets show alternating retouch
(alternating between direct and inverse retouch on the same lateral edge. 23.1% for bladelets, 13.3% for blades).

Notches
Notches13 account for 12.2% of the tool assemblage. They
appear mainly on bladelets (50%) but also appear on a flake
(the abraded flake, fig. 5:3), a core tablet, a CTE and more.
Most notches are 3-7 mm wide although three are quite large
and measure 15-17 mm. The direction of the notch does not
seem to matter as 50% of the notches are inverse, 44% are
direct and one (6%) is bifacial. A single item may have one
(58.3%), two (33.3%) or even three (8.3%) notches. Most of the
multi-notched items are bladelets; they have two parallel
notches near the proximal, or less commonly, the distal end,
presumably used for hafting (fig. 5: 4). Most notched items
have other retouch on them (66.6%), mostly fine retouch. One
item counted in the notches has drill-attempt marks on both
faces (see below fig. 7: 5).
Truncated blades
Four truncated blades were found, all truncated by direct
retouch. One blade is truncated only on the proximal end,
another only on the distal end, while a third is truncated on
both ends and has some retouch along both laterals. The fourth
truncated blade is broken on one end and truncated on the
other. It has a rectilinear shape, a direct abrupt back along one
edge and some inverse retouch along the other. More importantly, what was probably once the sharp edge, was dulled by
abrasion, perhaps due to some form of use. This item is too
flimsy to be defined as a sickle blade but it resembles this tool
type. Shape-defined sickle segments made on obsidian (with
no sickle-sheen, as reaping does not create this visual effect on
obsidian) were also found in the Early and Late ubaid layers at
Kosak Shamali (Nishiaki 2003).
Arrowheads, Corner-Thinned-Blades (CTB)
and burins
These items are all formal standardized tools. They appear
in small numbers in the assemblage but have great importance
to the character and chronology of the assemblage.

Obsidian transverse arrowheads are rare but parallels can be
found at Hagoshrim (Schechter et al. 2013) and at the Late
Neolithic layers at Sabi Abyad (Copeland 1996: 296-297), where

in other WR sites such as Nahal Zehora II (Barkai and Gopher

highly standardized obsidian transverse arrowheads from Ein
Zippori and those mentioned above. The transverse arrowheads
from Hagoshrim range 8-19 mm in width and 12-25 in length.
They show various metric configurations, and generally tend to
be smaller, with less oblique and irregularly outlined truncations
(personal observation). None of the transverse arrowheads from
Hagoshrim has pressure retouch and two have concave truncations, typical to flint transverse arrowheads (Rosen 1997: 39)
and not known at Ein Zippori. The obsidian transverse arrowheads from Sabi Abyad also have different metric configurations
(more stout or more elongated), and though traces of pressure
retouch do appear on them, their oblique sides were shaped by
snaps (Copeland 1996: Figs. 4.3, 6 and 4.17, 14 and 18). The flint
transverse arrowheads from the Sabi Abyad cache are substantially more elongated and are usually covered by pressure
retouch (Copeland 1996: Fig. 4.17, 1-10). Flint transverse arrowheads from Nahal Zehora II are all substantially smaller than the

retouch on the dorsal face; inverse, flat covering pressure

straightening the oblique edges towards the tip. Parallels can
be found at Musular (Özbaşaran 2000: 136 and fig. 14), where

Bipolar, naviform blade cores were ‘mass-produced’ in the
PPNB production centre of Kaletepe in Central Anatolia, nearby the Göllü Dağ sources, and blades were exported (Binder
and Balkan-Atli 2001). Obsidian bipolar cores were also found

found in the Southern Levant. This large point may be compared to similar items made of flint found at the PN layer 2 at
Munhata (Gopher 1989: 91 and fig. 38.6 and 8).
Arrowheads in general are not common in WR assemblages, and those made on obsidian are even less so. Transverse
arrowheads are known from WR contexts, and Amuq points
are typical to the Middle and Late PPNB as well as to the
beginning of the PN.
Five Burins were found at Ein Zippori. All were produced
on flakes and all have other retouch on them besides the burin
blow. The first is a broken dihedral axis burin, with one burin
blow along a break, the second along a retouched edge and
more direct retouch along the edge opposite the burin point.

striking platform. The axis burin blow is ventrally angled,

Two bladelets were defined as Corner-Thinned-Blades
(CTB). CTBs were defined by Y. Nishiaki (1990) as “obsidian
blades and bladelets with one or more corners deliberately
thinned by lamellar retouch”, and considered as tools. Both
bladelets from Ein Zippori have one lamellar ventral removal
along one edge. One originates from a break (fig. 5: 5) and one
from the proximal edge. They are both quite flimsy and should
be considered with reservation. CTBs usually appear in PPN
contexts (Nishiaki 1990) but as shown at Hagoshrim (Schechter
et al. 2013), and at Sabi-Abyad (Copeland 1996), they are found
in WR and parallel contexts as well.
BEADS
Four obsidian beads were found at Ein Zippori. Three of
the beads are disc-shaped (fig. 7: 2-4) and one is a perforated
proximal end of a broken bladelet which may have served as a
pendant (fig. 7: 1). The bladelet was polished on the dorsal face
and along one lateral edge and then the proximal end and the
other lateral edge were rounded by invasive direct pressure
retouch. The biconical hole was drilled last. Of the three disc
beads, two were found near each other, in the same locus
(B. 10585, L. 1094, area A; fig. 7: 2, 4), and the third in a different area altogether (L. 9029, area C.; fig. 7: 3). The three
disc beads look similar but show several technological differences. Two were made on flakes (fig. 7: 2, 4) and one on a blade
(fig. 7: 3). Predominantly direct retouch was first used around
the bead perimeter to give them a round shape; the beads were
then partially polished on the dorsal face, and the one made on
a blade was also polished on the ventral face; finally a biconical hole was drilled, equally from both faces on the blade and
predominantly from the ventral face on the flakes. Similar
obsidian beads were found at Hagoshrim (Schechter et al.
2013) and at Horvat ‘uza (Lieberman-Wander 2009).

TECHNO-TYPOLOGICAL DISCuSSION
Obsidian bladelet industries in the greater Fertile Crescent
area are traditionally produced by pressure (Binder 2007). The
pressure production industry requires great skill and expertise,
especially in the preparatory stages (Inizan et al. 1999: 76;
Pelegrin 2012). It is presumably performed by specialists in
selected sites such as the workshops near the obsidian sources
(Binder and Balkan-Atli 2001) and, very differently, at
Hagoshrim (Gopher et al. 2011; Schechter et al. 2013). The
production process leaves behind typical industrial waste such
as primary elements (PE), large preparatory flakes, unsuccessful cores, CTEs such as ridge blades and core tablets, overshooting blades, etc.
Several aspects of the obsidian assemblage from Ein
Zippori imply that prepared obsidian items were imported to
the site, alongside on-site production. The local production
included the later stages of production and was probably not
solely based on pressure or performed by specialists.
029-052-Schechter.indd 40 12/04/16 09:56The obsidian assemblages from the Wadi Rabah occupations at Ein Zippori, Israel 41Paléorient, vol. 42.1, p. 29-51 © CNRS ÉDITIONS 2016
Diverse CTEs and cores were found at the site, reflecting
obsidian production on-site. The core shapes are standard and
the CTEs are typical to bladelet production industries.
However, there are hardly any primary elements or debris and
no large preparatory flakes. This indicates that cores were
brought to the site ready-made, and maintained during production on-site by the removal of different CTEs.
Tools make up a large percent of the assemblage (36.8%.
table 1) and there are very few debitage items per tool (1.5).
Many more debitage items are expected during the production
of tools on-site, as can be seen in the flint assemblage. About
60% of all the flint from Ein Zippori has been sorted to date,
including 80-90% of the flint originating in the WR layers.
These assemblages constitute almost 200,000 items; of them
ca 150,000 belong to WR culture. In the flint assemblage, that
was clearly extensively worked on-site, debitage items comprise 44.9% of the assemblage and tools 12.1%, making the
ratio 3.7:1. This situation may also be compared to the different
layers at Hagoshrim: obsidian tool percentages plummet from
42.7% in layer VI, where on-site production did not take place,
to 17.0% in layer V and 9.3% in layer IV, when on-site production appeared and then intensified (Schechter et al. 2013:
Fig. 8.b). It is therefore suggested that obsidian bladelets,
arrowheads, beads and such, were imported to the site prepared, alongside ready-made bladelet cores, and that only limited on-site obsidian production was performed at Ein Zippori.
The production of bladelets and other items, which did take
place on-site at Ein Zippori, was presumably performed by
local knappers and not by obsidian specialists. Obsidian bladelets produced by pressure tend to be highly standardized in
both metrics and quality of production: they have abraded
punctiform butts, flat bulbs, no ventral ripples and straight parallel sides and arrises (Inizan et al. 1999: 76-78; Pelegrin
2012). At Ein Zippori, 64.2% of the assemblage shows mixed
signs of being produced by pressure, most of it comprised of
bladelets or tools on bladelets. Of these presumed pressureproduced items, substantial percentages show non-parallel and
winding sides and arrises (26.1%), ventral ripples (55.8%!) and
different types of butts (not abraded or punctiform) (9.1%).
This may point to a situation where inexperienced knappers
tried to perform this foreign technology. A different option
may be that these items were not produced by pressure after
all, despite their general appearance, but rather that local knappers, well-practiced in producing fine flint bladelets,15 performed their expertise using gentle percussion on obsidian
15. For WR bladelets production, see Barkai and Gopher 2012: 772-773 and
781; Gopher and Barkai 2012: 1117.
bladelet cores prepared for pressure debitage. It may also be
suggested, based on the low professionalism and bidirectional
removals, that pressure and percussion were used intermittently depending on skill, core maintenance needs, etc.
Blades and bladelets represent two different technological
and typological trajectories. There seems to be a difference in
the tendencies to import blades or bladelets from the different
sources, or to produce blades or bladelets from material from
the different sources (see below). Blade and bladelet tools were
shaped and retouched somewhat differently. While blades
were mainly retouched bilaterally, presumably used on both
sides, bladelets were uni- or bilaterally retouched equally.
Blades were mainly retouched with direct retouch, while
bladelets show a tendency toward more divers retouch direction. Like blades, most of the bladelets have direct retouch but
they also have alternating and inverse retouch, twice as much
as blades do. It is possible that blades were rarer, their use prolonged by using both sides, and shaped more traditionally,
resulting in less diversified retouch. These differences reflect a
separation between blades and bladelets. There is a certain
separation in the materials they were made on; they were
shaped somewhat differently, probably for different uses, and
were not conceived as one type of tool.
Only few tool types were made on obsidian. Except for
arrowheads and burins most tools are retouched, truncated or
notched flakes, blades or bladelets. The situation is different in
the flint assemblage. As mentioned above, most of the WR flint
assemblage has been analysed, and includes 12.1% tools and
3.8% cores. As there are a few excavation areas, each including
several WR sub-strata, these frequencies vary in the different
assemblages, with tools ranging from 8-23% and cores from
3-5%. The obsidian tool and core percentages diverge from this
range (more tools, 36.8%; less cores, 2.8%). The variety of
tool-types made on flint includes retouched, truncated, notched
and denticulated items; scrapers and perforators of different
types, burins, bifacial tools, arrowheads, and sickle blades.
Thus, even though the analysis of the large flint assemblage
from the site is still on going, it is clear that the flint tool-type
list is more diverse, and as a raw material, obsidian was treated
differently than flint. This may indicate that obsidian was
reserved for specific uses, due to its physical properties or its
high cost as an import.16

pROVENANCE DISCUSSION
All the obsidian tested from Ein Zippori originates from
Anatolian sources, as does all other tested obsidian found in
Israel.20 The percentages of items originating in each of the
above mentioned sources are presented in Table 4, where the
predominance of items originating from eastern Anatolian
sources is clearly apparent. This variety of sources, with a tendency towards eastern Anatolian sources, is known from the
WR of other sites in the area as well (Delerue 2007: 447-452).
It is traditionally accepted that during the earlier PPN period
obsidian was imported to the Southern Levant from the central
Anatolian Göllü Dağ sources (Yellin and Frachtenberg 1992)
and only later, perhaps due to local political changes in the
source regions (Binder 2007), did obsidian from eastern
Anatolian sources start appearing in this area (Perlman and
Yellin 1980). The latter is clearly reflected in the predominance
of eastern Anatolian obsidian items found at Ein Zippori.

(Perlman and Yellin 1980; Yellin and Frachtenberg 1992).
The ratio of obsidian to flint can be used to estimate the
intensity of obsidian use, especially by comparison between
sites. It seems that the intensity of obsidian use was greater during the PPNB than during the WR in general (table 7); the WR
of Hagoshrim IV being the exception, with conspicuous obsidian presence.
Additionally, while the PPNB assemblages are usually
characterized by flake dominance and an abundance of
debris—together accounting for 69% of the assemblage from
Nahal Lavan 109 (Burian and Friedman 1988: Table 1) and
49.8% of the assemblage from Motza—, 23 the WR assemblages
are dominated by bladelets, and especially pressure produced
bladelets, as is evident at Hagoshrim (Schechter et al. 2013)
and Nahal Zehora II (Delerue et al. 2012). Pressure produced
bladelets were also found at the EPPNB site of Horvat Galil
(Gopher 1997), but not at other PPNB sites. This northern
technology (Binder 2007) was therefore permeating into the
Southern Levant during the PPN, but only became wide spread
in the WR culture (Gopher et al. 1998).
The WR obsidian assemblages also usually include additional obsidian artefacts, such as beads, vessels and more, not
reported in any earlier site. Disc beads and bladelet-pendants
were found at Hagoshrim (Schechter et al. 2013), Horvat ‘uza
(Lieberman-Wander 2009) and more. Bracelet and vessel frag23. Yet blades are more common than flakes in the debitage (Khalaily et al.
2007: Table 2).

Hagoshrim, and Kabri is famous for its obsidian mirror and
giant core (Stekelis 1958; Prausnitz 1969 and 1970: 166; Gopher
et al. 2011).
These general characteristics of WR obsidian assemblages
were also noticed at Ein Zippori. The obsidian assemblage
from Ein Zippori originated in three different sources— central Anatolian Göllü Dağ and eastern Anatolian Bingöl A and
B—with predominance of eastern Anatolian sources. The
obsidian to flint ratio at Ein Zippori is 1:657, comfortably situated within the range for other WR sites (table 7); this ratio is
lower than at PPNB sites, as expected. The obsidian assemblage at Ein Zippori is dominated by pressure produced bladelets and tools, and includes additional obsidian body ornaments/
artefacts such as beads and pendants. This assemblage is thus
typical to the WR, as was presented above.
Besides pressure produced bladelets and tools, the assemblage includes other debitage items, mainly from the later
stages of the chaîne opératoire, such as flakes, blades, CTEs
and cores. Obsidian was imported to the site in the form of
produced bladelets, tools and beads, as well as preformed and/
or fully prepared bladelet cores. Some on-site production took
place, by local knappers, who may have not had great expertise
at knapping obsidian. The production involved both pressure
and percussion and was aimed at producing bladelets. Bladelets
and blades were retouched somewhat differently and were
probably used for different purposes.
This relationship, between import and on-site production,
varies between different sites of the WR culture. A comparison
between Nahal Zehora II, Ein Zippori and Hagoshrim IV may
reflect different attitudes towards obsidian, and the different
roles each of the sites had concerning obsidian and its movement

not evenly distributed in the area, and it seems to have been
concentrated in production and exchange centres (i.e.,
Hagoshrim, and see Garfinkel 2008 and 2011). Production was
not, however, restricted to these centres, and was performed on
a local site scale, perhaps by non-specialists (i.e., Ein Zippori).
The products of the obsidian industry also reached sites where
no production took place (i.e., Nahal Zehora II).
Obsidian is a rare and exotic raw material in the Southern
Levant, which had to be brought from afar. As such, it probably
played a role in socio-politics and economy and might also have
significance in the symbolic-ritual array. The large burnt core
from Hagoshrim (Gopher et al. 2011; Schechter et al. 2013) may
be an example from the Southern Levant, while the burnt house
at Arpachiyah may be mentioned as an example in other regions
(Campbell 2000). In addition to all that, obsidian items must
have had utilitarian uses as well (Gopher et al. 1998 and 2011).
The obsidian from Ein Zippori represents a component of
the multiple relationships the Southern Levant as a whole, and
this site in the Lower Galilee in particular, had with the Northern
Levant. These connections are also expressed in other media
such as the stamp seals found at the site, reflecting possible interaction with the northern Halaf culture, and the engraved stone
plate and bone item,24 reflecting an artistic expression of ideas,
common to various cultures in this space and time.
