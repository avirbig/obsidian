Handheld portable XRF (HHpXRF) has received considerable recent attention in archaeology, especially
for obsidian sourcing. Published studies largely suggest a high potential for success. HHpXRF, though, has
been met with debate and scepticism. Concerns fall into three categories: (1) low accuracy and precision,
(2) data correction schemes and calibration, and (3) ignorance of fundamental XRF issues such as
specimen size limits and morphology effects. The result is that archaeological interpretations in HHpXRFbased studies are cast into doubt. Should interpretations be believed if authors did not follow the
perceived best practices? To address such questions, the validity of HHpXRF for Near Eastern obsidian
sourcing was tested under deliberately “sub-optimal” conditions. Irregular obsidian debitage near the
minimum size limits was sourced with a success rate of 94% (based on A-rank matches) to 100% (based
on A- and B-rank matches) even under conditions chosen to mimic “off-the-shelf” operation.

1. Introduction
Portable XRF (pXRF) has received considerable recent attention
in archaeology, especially for sourcing obsidian artefacts. Among
these studies, the label “pXRF” has been applied to a range of
instruments, from small “deconstructed” benchtop systems
capable of use in a museum (Craig et al., 2007; Galvão et al., 2009)
to handheld analysers readily brought to excavations. A review of
200 articles specifying the use of “pXRF” revealed that more than
half involved the use of transportable multi-component systems
restricted to museums, laboratories, and field houses (Frahm and
Doonan, under review). Here, though, I only consider the selfcontained instruments, about the size, weight, and shape as
a cordless drill, that can be used at excavation sites, on surveys, or at
outcrops. These analysers are often designated “field-portable XRF”
(fpXRF) and “handheld pXRF” (HHpXRF) in the literature. I use
HHpXRF here to avoid obfuscation with relevant acronyms (i.e.,
“FP” is used for the “fundamental parameters” correction scheme,
which I discuss later). Furthermore, the fact these instruments are
“handheld” does matter. To achieve such extreme portability, these
analysers sacrifice performance. In particular, the instruments use
less-than-ideal geometric arrangements of miniaturised, lowpower components that run on batteries, and the processing of Xrays is done by on-board electronics and software rather than
external systems. Despite trade-offs, such portability is one reason
the analysers have captured our community’s interest.
Although great interest has been expressed in application of
HHpXRF to obsidian sourcing (particularly at conferences and
specialised workshops), to date fewer than two dozen studies have
been published, many in this journal (e.g., Phillips and Speakman,
2009; Craig et al., 2010; Jia et al., 2010; Nazaroff et al., 2010;
Sheppard et al., 2011; Burley et al., 2011; McCoy et al., 2011;
Millhauser et al., 2011; Forster and Grave, 2012; Goodale et al.,
2012). Overall these published studies suggest excellent potential
for successfully tracing obsidian artefacts back to their geological
sources. This should not be surprising considering the great
successes of XRF-based obsidian sourcing for more than 45 years
(e.g., Parks and Tieh, 1966; Jack and Heizer, 1968).
Use of HHpXRF, however, has been met with scepticism. The
concerns fall into three basic categories: (1) low accuracy or
precision, (2) data correction schemes and instrument calibration
(or lack thereof), and (3) ignorance of fundamental XRF problems,
including minimum size limits and surface morphology effects.

principally because of a perceived lack of analytic rigor” (1674).
Thus, archaeologists are put in a difficult position. Should the
results of HHpXRF-based obsidian sourcing be believed if the
authors did not follow what others assert are the best practices?
Might the conclusions still be correct if the authors did not use
a certain calibration? Are archaeological interpretations untrustworthy if HHpXRF data do not match published compositions of
obsidian sources? A desire to respond to these and similar questions using empirical data led to the experiment that I discuss here.
I completely agree with Shackley (2010) that there is “potential
misuse” of HHpXRF (18), especially when authors offer no reason
for using this novel technology in place of tried and tested laboratory XRF instruments (hereafter “labXRF”). There will always be
obsidian artefacts beyond the reach of labXRF, such as artefacts in
museum collections and countries with export restrictions but
without the means for chemical analysis. With HHpXRF, selecting
artefacts for sourcing could be influenced less by export limitations
and more by the intellectual framework in which the study is
conceived. Furthermore, freeing XRF from the laboratory enables its
use in new methodological e and thus theoretical e frameworks,
rather than just being treated as a cheap and easy replacement for
laboratory techniques. For example, HHpXRF also provides
a potential to analyse artefacts as they are unearthed, leading to
new research designs in obsidian sourcing and informing excavation strategy and interpretation in the field. It is such novel applications, allowing use of XRF in ways previously not possible, that
hold the highest potential for revolutionising obsidian sourcing.
Thus, regardless of its limitations, there is great value in establishing HHpXRF as a successful tool, even if that necessitates
methodological changes to source artefacts.
A “successful” sourcing technique must first be defined, of
course. The relevant evaluation concept is validity, not accuracy
(Frahm, 2012a). Neff (1998) defines validity in sourcing research:
“The instrument is a valid indicator to the extent that composition
really does measure ‘source’ as a location in geographic space and
not some other concept” (116). Can the technique distinguish the
geological sources and assign artefacts to them? In other words,
can use of the technique yield, not geochemical data, but archaeological data? Hence, validity is established through artefacts’
correct source assignments, and it is only how success is defined
in the context of this experiment. In other contexts, it might be
desirable to define success in terms of generating data sufficiently
accurate to allow inter-laboratory geochemical data comparisons
(Frahm, 2012a, 2012b). In this test, however, “archaeological
accuracy” (Shackley, 2005:7, emphasis added) of the results was
the metric. Inter-laboratory comparisons are, though, still
addressed in Section 6.
Here I describe a test of HHpXRF’s validity for obsidian sourcing
under deliberately “sub-optimal” conditions. Acquiring data
compatible with existing XRF databases was not a priority in this
test, nor was there an effort to maximise accuracy. Validity is
established using an artefact-by-artefact source-assignment
comparison of HHpXRF and electron microprobe analysis (EMPA)
data for the same set of obsidian artefacts and geological specimens. Throughout the test, “sub-optimal” conditions were used:
a simple data correction scheme, no calibration beyond a default
“soil” mode, irregularly shaped artefacts near the minimum size
limit, and a circa-2002 analyser. This approach sought to replicate
the most basic “off-the-shelf” operation of such an instrument. The
intent of the test was not to establish new “best practices” or
recommendations for future research. Rather, “off-the-shelf”
operation was simply a parameter for the test conditions.
The results show that, with an appropriate methodology,
HHpXRF can be valid for obsidian sourcing despite the “suboptimal” conditions. Fifty artefacts’ HHpXRF-based source assignments matched the EMPA-based assignments with a success rate
between 94% (based on A-rank matches) and 100% (based on Aand B-rank matches). Of course, using deliberately sub-optimal
conditions is not recommended for analytical work outside the
context of such a test. These results, however, suggest existing
HHpXRF-based Near East obsidian sourcing studies may well be
valid even if the authors did not follow labXRF methods (cf.,
Goodale et al., 2012: “the same strict protocols applied in
laboratory-based XRF analyses, involving instrument calibration,
evaluation of inter-instrument performance, and comparison to
accepted geologic standards, need to be applied,” 875).
Consequently, even if the geochemical data are only internally
consistent, archaeological interpretations may still be made from
the sourcing results. For example, in this test, HHpXRF confirmed
that obsidian from Göllü Dag in central Turkey reached as far east as
Tell Mozan in northeastern Syria during the Bronze Age. Not only
did the HHpXRF results reveal the volcano where the obsidian
originated (Göllü Dag) but also the specific outcrops (near Kömürcü
village). These results are corroborated by an independent analytical technique (EMPA), meaning they are reproducible. Additionally,
the results make archaeological sense (i.e., the artefacts are
assigned to an obsidian source where lithic workshops are well
attested).
2. Background: HHpXRF issues
As noted in the Introduction, the debates surrounding HHpXRF
may be summarised as three issues: (1) low accuracy and/or
precision, (2) raw data correction and instrument calibration, and
(3) ignorance of XRF challenges. The authors of obsidian sourcing
studies have dealt with these issues differently, but most seek to
mitigate one or more of them through their methods.
2.1. Issue #1: low accuracy and precision
Accuracy is the closeness of agreement between a measurement
and the “true” value of the measured quantity, and precision is the
agreement among a series of measurements under identical
conditions. Precision is essential because sourcing studies involve
measuring geological specimens and artefacts, seeking discrete
clusters in the chemical data to differentiate sources, and recognising those characteristic signals in artefacts. For a particular
sourcing study, precision is most important. Accuracy becomes
essential only when one relies on previously acquired or published
data (“legacy data”) for the geological sources. Given there are
always differences between among data collected in different
laboratories or with different techniques (e.g., Glascock, 1999), the
majority of obsidian sourcing does not follow this paradigm. That is,
geological specimens are typically analysed using the same
instrument in the same laboratory as the artefacts. It follows,
therefore, HHpXRF should follow that same method. In some cases,
this is not possible, so accuracy becomes a concern. This is
addressed, based on an inter-laboratory comparison, in Section 6.
All analytical techniques are affected by some baseline level of
random phenomena that limit precision. For XRF, X-ray emission
and subsequent detection are subject to statistical fluctuations.
Other factors limit accuracy. For example, a systematic error (such
as a flawed calibration) affects accuracy, not precision. A review of
the literature reveals that assessments of HHpXRF’s accuracy vary.
In a recent issue of this journal, Grave et al. (2012) analysed
Australian lithics and discuss if their HHpXRF data are best
described as “semi-quantitative” or “quasi-quantitative,” implying
E. Frahm / Journal of Archaeological Science 40 (2013) 1080e1092 1081the results lack the accuracy to be considered quantitative.
Sheppard et al. (2011) state that comparing the accuracy of HHpXRF
to “lab based geochemistry has produced variable results” (48).
Others have more favourable conclusions. For example, Liritzis and
Zacharias (2011) conclude HHpXRF is “efficient and accurate” (136)
while Craig et al. (2010) claim artefacts’ compositions corresponded
well to legacy data, indicating accuracy. Still others are agnostic.
Goodale et al. (2012) assert that the “accuracy of [HHpXRF] data for
geological materials has not yet been adequately addressed or
evaluated” (883). This seems, at least superficially, at odds with the
ready adoption of HHpXRF in environmental testing and the earth
sciences (Frahm and Doonan, under review).
Why such different assessments? Accuracy reflects instrumentspecimen-analyst interactions. Even with the same instrument,
different specimens and analysts’ choices effect different accuracies
and systematic errors. Precision, however, involves whether or not
random error obscures structure in the data. Ideally, one would be
certain HHpXRF data are sufficiently precise that artefacts could be
sourced regardless of accuracy and calibration (i.e., systematic
errors).
2.2. Issue #2: calibration and correction
How an instrument converts raw X-ray counts into elements’
concentrations is the principal factor in its accuracy. The correction
and calibration are intended to reduce systematic error, which
improves accuracy, not precision. Hence the accuracy of any technique rests largely on an analyst’s choices regarding correction
scheme and calibration standards. These processes are not necessarily error-free. Mark and Workman (2003) note 15 sources of
error in this process, including variations within standard materials
and uncertainty in correction models. A detailed discussion of
correction and calibration in XRF is beyond the scope of this paper,
and such a discussion is also hampered by variations in terminology
amongst researchers (i.e., what some researchers call “calibration”
others call “correction” or “standardisation”). Thus interested
readers are directed to Shackley (2011), but the discussion here will
be a simplification of complex procedures.
Simply put, the measured X-ray intensities from a specimen
must be adjusted for a variety of effects (called “correction” here)
and converted to elemental concentrations (“calibration” here).
There are three essential HHpXRF correction models: Compton
normalisation (CN), fundamental parameters (FP), and empirical
correction curves. CN measures X-rays inelastically scattered (i.e.,
Compton scattered) by specimens to normalise the elements’ X-ray
signals and help to compensate for differences in morphology,
texture, and density. FP involves iterative corrections to raw X-ray
counts based on the measured chemical composition, accounting
for expected differences in X-ray emission, absorption, secondary
fluorescence, and other phenomena. Lastly, correction curves are
empirical: the raw X-ray counts are corrected based on a fit to those
measured from a collection of standards. Calibrations, in contrast,
are either built-in (“off-the-shelf” calibrations include mining, soils,
alloys, precious metals, and plastics) or custom (based on the
measurement of standards and often used in conjunction with
correction curves).
Empirical correction curves using custom calibrations are
regarded as the best practice for labXRF but are rarely used in
HHpXRF obsidian sourcing. Commonly “off-the-shelf” corrections
and/or calibrations are used instead. There are two major trends.
First is the use of CN or FP with custom calibrations using obsidian
standards (e.g., Craig et al., 2010; Burley et al., 2011; Forster and
Grave, 2012). Second is the use of FP with a proprietary “soil”
calibration (e.g., Golitko et al., 2010; Millhauser et al., 2011;
Vázquez et al., 2011; Goodale et al., 2012).
Even when correction curves and custom calibrations are used,
it is often unclear when that procedure was done, either ininstrument or during post-processing (see Section 6). Indeed,
there is no consensus (or even a discussion) whether data should be
adjusted in-instrument or in subsequent data processing. Regardless, one must have confidence in the data, notably its internal
consistency (i.e., precision). Shackley (2010) asserts that ignoring
fundamental XRF issues, like specimen size and morphology
effects, threatens the assumption of internally consistent data.
2.3. Issue #3: fundamental XRF issues
Minimum size limits is one of the issues well known in labXRF
but easily overlooked using HHpXRF. Davis et al. (2011) conclude
that, based on labXRF experiments, error greatly increases when
artefacts are less than 2-mm thick or 10-mm in diameter. Others
corroborate their conclusion (Eerkens et al., 2007; Lundblad et al.,
2008). Additionally, it is argued that specimens should entirely
cover the instrument’s detector window and/or X-ray beam (the
latter being invisible and, therefore, hard to ensure is covered). The
window size ranges from just a few millimetres in new instruments
to 20 mm in old ones (although the X-ray beam diameter is often on
the order of 5e10 mm). Thus, small obsidian debitage is difficult to
analyse accurately, and Shackley (2010) holds analysing such
artefacts “impinges on the fallacy of internal consistency” (19).
Specimen morphology is another issue. The ideal specimen for
XRF has a flat and smooth surface because X-rays are sensitive to
topography. Surface irregularities can introduce additional error
into measurements. Consequently, for labXRF, ideally specimens
are either (1) polished flat or (2) ground into a powder and fused.
Both HHpXRF and labXRF can analyse irregular surfaces on artefacts
with the understanding that there will be additional error, though
its severity is difficult to quantify. Liritzis and Zacharias (2011)
report HHpXRF data “are affected by surface roughness effects,
more so than desktop [labXRF] instruments” (132).
Most HHpXRF obsidian studies endeavour to minimise such
effects through method, that is, critical selection of artefacts.
Frequently researchers select artefacts with flat surfaces (e.g.,
Phillips and Speakman, 2009; Nazaroff et al., 2010; Burley et al.,
2011; Forster and Grave, 2012). Goodale et al. (2012) only analysed artefacts “with relatively flat surfaces that were large enough
to cover the detector window completely... Specimens that did not
lay flush against the detector window, were too small to cover the
entire window, or showed clear visual signs of surficial weathering... were excluded” (879). Using similar criteria, Sheppard et al.
(2011) excluded “irregularly shaped lithic shatter” (48). A second
approach to mitigating surface effects through method is to rely
mainly on the so-called “mid-Z heavy elements” (Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Nb),
which Forster et al. (2011) conclude are least affected by surface
morphology. There is, though, still little found in the literature
regarding the performance of HHpXRF to analyse irregular obsidian
debitage.
3. Methods & materials
The experiment described here was meant to push, even
outright violate, the three HHpXRF issues considered above. First,
there was no effort to optimise accuracy and precision for obsidian
(i.e., both were evaluated during the course of this experiment, but
there was no direct optimising). Second, CN correction was used
with a default “soils” calibration. Third, the artefacts are irregular
chip debris. Many of them are either near or below the minimum
size limits for XRF, and most are also smaller than the detector
window (20 mm) on the circa-2002 analyser used for this
experiment. The geographical/archaeological context is similarly

areas, and the ancient inhabitants of Tell Mozan in northeastern
Syria are known to have used obsidians from at least six different
sources (Frahm, 2010).
3.1. Archaeological artefacts
The 50 artefacts in the test were excavated from Tell Mozan
(Fig. 1). The export of lithic artefacts was limited by Syria’s Directorate General of Antiquities and Museums to small, irregular
debitage. To gain approval, my selection criteria were that the
artefacts had to be (1) flaking debris or other non-diagnostic fragment (i.e., no prismatic blade segments) and (2) less than 20 mm
across (Fig. 2). These criteria fit Rosen’s (1997) definition of “chip
debris.”
3.2. Geological reference collection
The artefacts were compared to 504 geo-referenced Anatolian
geological obsidian specimens, which were analysed with the same
instruments. The specimens were collected in 1991 by George “Rip”
Rapp, Regents Professor of Geoarchaeology Emeritus, University of
Minnesota-Duluth and Tuncay Ercan, Directorate of Mineral
Research and Exploration, Turkey. The collection includes specimens from archaeologically important obsidian sources across
Anatolia, including 95 different outcrops at 21 source areas. The
specimens also have various sizes and morphologies, and most of
the irregularly shaped specimens are smaller than 20 mm.
3.3. Analytical instruments
For this particular test, I used a Thermo Scientific NITON XLt
analyser. The XLt series of analysers was introduced in 2002, was
superseded by newer models in 2007, and ended production in
2009. The X-ray source of the analyser was a 40-kV tube with Ag
anode target, and the detector was a Peltier-cooled Si-PiN diode
covered by a 20-mm detector window. The current models have
superior silicon drift detectors (SDDs) and smaller detector
windows. Furthermore, older models have less powerful electronics and thus use less sophisticated algorithms.
The EMPA analyses were conducted using the JEOL JXA-8900
SuperProbe at the Electron Microprobe Laboratory, Department of
Earth Sciences, University of Minnesota-Twin Cities. My analytical
procedures are documented elsewhere (Frahm, 2010, 2012b), as are
my data evaluations based on labXRF and NAA data from the
Archaeometry Laboratory at the University of Missouri Research
Reactor (MURR). Readers interested in MURR instrumentation,
specimen handling, and so forth are forward to Glascock et al.
(2007) and the centre’s website.
3.4. Analytical choices
With any technique, an analyst’s choices may affect precision as
much as the instruments. In XRF, for instance, the choices regarding
operating conditions range from straightforward (e.g., how long
and how many times to measure a specimen) to complex (e.g.,
selection of X-ray tube cathode and filter materials for optimally
exciting elements in a particular portion of the periodic table). In
addition, analytical techniques have no predetermined accuracy,
and analysts’ choices, informed by their theoretical and practical
“know how” (i.e., connaissances and savoir-faire in the terminology
of Pierre Lemonnier), largely determine accuracy. To minimise the
effects of choice on this validity experiment, the only choices I
made were those that a user must make: (1) which correction
model and calibration to use and (2) for how long to measure the
specimens and artefacts.
For correction and calibration, I selected the most basic
approach for the experiment: the CN correction model with the
built-in “soils” calibration. This involved the fewest choices, such as
no need for choosing calibration standards or a regression scheme.
The “soils” mode also determined which elements would be
measured: As, Ca, Co, Cr, Cu, Fe, Hg, K, Mn, Mo, Ni, P, Pb, Rb, Sc, Se, Sr,

One must also choose for how long to measure the emitted Xrays. In published HHpXRF sourcing studies, this time varies
between 60 (Golitko et al., 2010) and 300 s (Nazaroff et al., 2010;
McCoy et al., 2011; Forster and Grave, 2012). Thus, I decided to
measure the artefacts and specimens for 300 s. Precision depends
on the number of X-ray counts and the stability of the instrument.
For low counts, counting statistics prevail. For high counts, instrument stability prevails. Counting for 300 s ensures sufficient X-ray
counts, so the resulting precision largely reflects the inherent
stability of the analyser.

3.6. Source assignment procedures
Figs. 4 and 5 are three-dimensional scatterplots of the artefacts and geological specimens analysed with HHpXRF. Such
scatterplots were used to (1) identify which elements best discerned the obsidian sources and (2) ensure that the artefacts’
compositions fell in the clusters of geological specimens. The
most information-bearing elements are Mn, Rb, Sr, Ti, Zn, and Zr,
and clustering of the artefacts with the geological specimens
implies that there are no methodological deficiencies (e.g.,
missing sources). To assign artefacts to sources, use of Euclidean
distance (ED) calculations replicates use of scatterplots quantitatively with three or more elements. ED is a straightforward
distance measure with no assumptions, meaning choice did not
play a role in specifying a priori groups or the number of clusters. First, the data were transformed so elements such as Zr
(which varied between 76 and 2778 ppm) did not overwhelm
elements that varied less than one order of magnitude (like Rb,
which varied between 77 and 627 ppm). Then EDs were calculated between each artefact and geological specimen for three
element combinations: (1) Rb, Sr, Zr; (2) Mn, Rb, Sr, Zn, Zr; and
(3) Mn, Rb, Sr, Ti, Zn, Zr. The result was a set of three-, five-, and
six-dimensional matrices of similarity coefficients. A “nearest
neighbour” search of these coefficients identified the ten
geological specimens “closest” (i.e., most similar compositionally) to each artefact. From those sources among the nearest
neighbours, an “A-Rank” was given to the most frequent
obsidian source, and a “B-Rank” to the second most frequent

4. Results: comparing source assignments
Table 3 is an artefact-by-artefact source-assignment comparison
using HHpXRF and EMPA data. Here I consider the EMPA-based
assignments to be “truth.” Of 50 artefacts, 47 have the same
source assignments between HHpXRF and EMPA. Only three artefacts have mismatches. One of the mismatched artefacts is amongst
the smallest and thinnest of the test set, and the other two have
highly irregular surfaces. In each mismatch, the HHpXRF-based Arank has a low probability (i.e., 50% or less of the nearest neighbours belong to that source), and the B-rank is always correct and
often (i.e., for two artefacts) has a relatively high probability (i.e.,
30% of the nearest neighbours).
Additionally, in all three mismatches, there are known
geochemical similarities between the obsidian sources involved.
Two of the three mismatches involve Meydan Dag and Tendürek
Dag. These volcanos are not only chemically similar but also
geographically adjacent. Chataigner (1998) suggested that eastern
obsidian-bearing lava flows of Meydan Dag may be covering
western flows of Tendürek Dag. Indeed, as I show elsewhere
(Frahm, 2010: 512e514), Rapp and Ercan mistook a Tendürek Dag
obsidian outcrop for a Meydan Dag one. The third artefact
mismatch is Bingöl B obsidian identified as Acıgol. These sources
are also chemically similar (e.g., Acıgol is Renfrew’s group 1eef
and Bingöl B is 1g). Given the error introduced by small, irregular artefacts, occasional mismatches involving these sources are
neither surprising nor detrimental.
Thus there is a sourcing success rate between 94% (based on
A-rank matches) and 100% (A- and B-rank matches) with
HHpXRF data. This is congruent with the 97% agreement in
Millhauser et al. (2011), who attributed Mesoamerican artefacts
to probable sources using HHpXRF, labXRF, and neutron activation analysis (NAA). These authors, in contrast to my test,
worked to minimise error due to artefact size and surface
irregularities. They used a default “soils” calibration with FP
correction and document significant inaccuracies between the
HHpXRF and NAA datasets for two obsidian standards. Yet their
source assignments were highly successful. My test corroborates
their finding of HHpXRF validity in obsidian sourcing despite
accuracy issues.
An ability to distinguish the chemically similar Bingöl A and
Nemrut Dag obsidians was not anticipated for this experiment.
Numerous researchers using labXRF, NAA, and other techniques
report an inability to differentiate these obsidians (e.g., Abbès et al.,
2001, 2003; Rosen et al., 2005; Bellot-Gurlet and Poupeau, 2006;
Carter et al., 2008; Khalidi et al., 2009). The results here suggest that
HHpXRF suffers from the same problem. Two artefacts in Table 3,
marked with asterisks, are assigned to Nemrut Dag using the
method discussed here; however, I ultimately attributed them to
Bingöl A using a geochemically based metric (Frahm, 2012c). This
subtle distinction is most likely a case when the sub-optimal
conditions were detrimental to the result, and following best
practices, like analysing larger artefacts with flat surfaces, might
mean success.
5. Archaeological implications
The overall obsidian-source patterns for Tell Mozan based on
the HHpXRF and EMPA data are quite similar, particularly for the
relatively small sample size of 50 artefacts. Lithic specialists
frequently study assemblages of hundreds (or thousands) of stone
tools. On such scales, a success rate of 94% or better with HHpXRF
yields archaeologically valid source patterns with statistically
significant samples, even if a small fraction of artefacts are
misattributed.
In this case, the HHpXRF data independently corroborate one
of the most interesting results from the EMPA data. Long-distance
exchange of Central Anatolian obsidians, including those from
Göllü Dag volcano (Fig. 1), is generally considered to be a Levantine and southwestern Anatolian phenomenon, a notion reinforced by early (e.g., Renfrew et al., 1966, 1968) as well as recent
(e.g., Chataigner, 1998; Chataigner et al., 1998; Cauvin and
Chataigner, 1998) obsidian distribution maps. The HHpXRF
results, however, confirm that three artefacts from the palace

including Bingöl A and Nemrut Dag, which is problematic for the
reasons discussed.
6. Discussion: what of accuracy?
There are arguments, some cogent, that HHpXRF data should be
sufficiently accurate to be compatible with existing obsidian source
databases. The speed of analyses using HHpXRF negates much of
the impetus for doing so. If a large collection of geological specimens from likely obsidian sources may be analysed in a few days
using the same instrument as the artefacts, there is much less need
for inter-laboratory compatibility. It also removes inter-technique
error, a problem that has plagued obsidian sourcing from the
start (e.g., when Renfrew and colleagues switched from optical
spectroscopy to NAA). The “internal consistency” of HHpXRF seems
acceptable for regional-scale studies and targeted research designs.
There has yet to be an archaeological study in which Near East
obsidian data must be consistent with Mesoamerican obsidian
data. Still there are situations when compatibility is important and
accuracy becomes essential.
Of the obsidian specimens analysed with HHpXRF for this test,
eighteen were also analysed with both labXRF and NAA at the
MURR Archaeometry Laboratory, whose staff have endeavoured to
make these two analytical techniques compatible for obsidian.

7. Conclusions
This test was intended to replicate “off-the-shelf” operation for
a challenging set of artefacts, and it corroborates what a few
previous studies elsewhere in the world have found. While accuracy assessments have been variable in these studies, the HHpXRF
analysers were sufficiently precise to produce valid source assignments. This was the conclusion, for example, of Nazaroff et al.
(2010) based on their test with Mesoamerican obsidian artefacts.
They found the HHpXRF instrument “is able to effectively distinguish between the El Chayal, Ixtepeque, and Pachuca geochemical
source groups... [Thus it] is a valid technique... However, systematic
error is introduced that influences the accuracy” (894). Nazaroff
et al. (2010), though, followed the “best practices” for HHpXRF.
Their artefacts “were positioned with as much contact as possible
with the instrument’s surface,” and any “irregularly shaped
samples were placed with the smoothest side positioned for analysis” (889). In addition, they used a series of previously analysed
obsidians as calibration standards to construct a correction curve.
None of these perceived “best practices” were followed in my
experiment, and I reached the same conclusions regarding validity
as Nazaroff et al. (2010).
It must be that using HHpXRF, even under “sub-optimal”
conditions, does not necessarily violate the “Provenance Postulate,”
the core of sourcing theory. Even with systematic error from the
(lack of) calibration and random error due to problematic artefacts,
the compositions of these Near Eastern obsidians, as measured
using HHpXRF, have greater inter-source than intra-source variations. Hence HHpXRF is valid for sourcing the Tell Mozan obsidian
artefacts back to their sources. Pollard et al. (2007) state that, for
sourcing, an analytical technique requires “sufficient accuracy and
precision” (15). As this test shows, the HHpXRF precision is suffi-
cient for sourcing; however, accuracy is not essential when the
geological specimens and artefacts are analysed using the same
instrument under the same, albeit sub-optimal, conditions. Thus,
HHpXRF can easily be adapted to obsidian sourcing by mitigating
its weaknesses through method (which may or may not be the
same methods routinely used for labXRF). This includes the
weaknesses of “off-the-shelf” calibrations, which may be mitigated
through analysing reference standards.
It must be reiterated that the particular experiment I report here
is not intended to establish an operating procedure for future
research or suggest that there is no need for standard practices (I
have argued elsewhere if favour of rigorous procedures and their
evaluation; Frahm, 2012a, 2012b). The question at hand was: Can
the archaeological results of HHpXRF-based obsidian sourcing be
reliable if the researchers did not follow what others hold are the
best practices? These test results suggest yes. Hence there is little to
no reason to doubt, for example, that Forster and Grave (2012)
correctly assign Tell Arpachiyah artefacts to the Meydan Dag and
Bingöl B sources simply because their correction scheme was based
on CN rather than FP. Other methodological issues in a sourcing
study, such as missing sources or failure to capture the variations
within a source, are more likely to jeopardise the archaeological
interpretations than mere use of HHpXRF.
Finally, it is worth noting that HHpXRF (or any other technique)
is not inherently valid or invalid. Use in a certain application is what
makes it valid or not. In this case, despite sub-optimal conditions,
the test demonstrates that HHpXRF is a largely valid technique for
obsidian sourcing in the Near East. Geographic region (i.e., the
numbers and chemistries of sources, the elements useful for source
discrimination and the detectability at their concentrations) is
a variable in the validity of HHpXRF-based obsidian sourcing,
meaning validity tests elsewhere in the world, such as Nazaroff
et al. (2010) in Mesoamerica and Jia et al. (2010) in Southeast
Asia, are still necessary. Publishing analyses of international reference standards, though, does not establish validity. It only establishes accuracy and sometimes precision. Only demonstrating the
correct source assignments of obsidian artefacts or specimens can
establish a technique’s validity.
