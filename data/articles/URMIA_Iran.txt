PROVENANCE STUDIES OF CHALCOLITHIC OBSIDIAN
ARTEFACTS FROM NEAR LAKE URMIA, NORTHWESTERN
IRAN USING WDXRF ANALYSIS
K. A. NIKNAMI
Department of Archaeology, University of Tehran, Enghlab Street, Tehran, Iran
A. C. AMIRKHIZ
Iranian Centre for Archaeological Research (ICAR)
and M. D. GLASCOCK
Research Reactor Center, University of Missouri, Columbia, MO, USA
In 2005–2006 we initiated a major archaeological survey and chemical characterization study
to investigate the long-term use of obsidian along the eastern shores of Lake Urmia, northwestern Iran. Previous research in the area suggested that almost all archaeological obsidian
found in this area originated from the Nemrut Dagˆ source located in the Lake Van region of
Anatolia (Turkey). More recent research on obsidian artefacts from the Lake Urmia region has
identified a significant number of obsidian artefacts with compositions different from the
sources near Lake Van. This suggests that the obsidian artefacts are from a yet to be identified
geological source, but possibly one that was not too distant. In order to advance our knowledge of Iranian obsidians and eventually refine provenance criteria we analysed obsidian
from 22 Chalcolithic sites and some source areas. The compositions of both obsidian source
samples and artefacts were determined using wave length dispersive X-ray fluorescence
spectrometry (WDXRF). This paper presents results from the trace elemental analysis of both
geological and archaeological obsidians, providing important new data concerning the diachronic relationship between lithic technology and raw material in the north-west of Iran.
KEYWORDS: LAKE URMIA, OBSIDIAN, PROVENANCE STUDIES, WDXRF
INTRODUCTION
The role of obsidian as an important material in the inter-regional economic and social development of ancient societies, has gained special significance in archaeological investigations.
Although such studies are common in many regions of the world, chemical characterization studies
are rare in Iran. However, there are a few cases in which Iranian researchers (e.g., Khademi et al.
2007; Ghorabi et al. 2008), as well as archaeologists from other countries (e.g., Renfrew 1977;
Blackman 1984), have attempted to analyse obsidian artefacts from prehistoric sites in order to
explain its distribution as a major commodity in the network of Neolithic settlements. Although
many of the investigations have focused on the physical characteristics of obsidian artefacts, they
have also increased our understanding of the obsidian distribution network in Iran.
The first scholar to render a quantitative analytical approach for obsidian provenance studies
in Iran was Renfrew, who in 1977 attempted to create a model based on data from western

in which large quantities of obsidian were found and, consequently, it would have been possible
for obsidian to have been traded into the interior of Iran.
A contrasting view exists (Rafi’far 1991), based on tentative studies of obsidian technology in
eastern Anatolia (e.g., the Çayönü site). This view maintains that although it may be possible to
assume a common geological origin for Çayönü and Jarmo obsidians, it does not seem likely that
this obsidian entered the Deh Luran Plain and Ali Kosh site in the same way, given the
technological similarities between Ali Kosh and that of obsidians from the Banan Mountains of
the Kerman Province and eastern Afghanistan. It therefore seems possible that alternative trade
routes for obsidians may have existed.
A majority of the archaeologists who have investigated obsidian in Iran believe that with the
beginning of the Chalcolithic and the succeeding Bronze Age the obsidian trade routes changed.
Specifically, it is believed that consumption of locally available obsidian was preferred. It is
emphasized that existing trade routes around Lake Urmia would have facilitated the movement of
locally available obsidian throughout the region. Additionally, it can be argued that the Lake
Urmia trade routes facilitated the development of obsidian trade, from resources of Armenia, the
Caucasuses, and Lake Van on the one hand, and the transmission to the interior of Iran on the
other (Blackman 1984). It seems that, with the shift from the Neolithic to Chalcolithic and then
the Bronze Age, the quantity of obsidian implements in the majority of archaeological sites
around Lake Urmia increased considerably.
The provenance of these artefacts typically has been attributed to sources in the Lake Van
region of eastern Anatolia by several archaeologists (e.g., Blackman 1984, Badalyan et al. 2004).
There is evidence, however, that the provenance of the implements might be attributed to the
volcanic complexes near the flanks of the Sahand and Sabalan mountains (Burney 1964; Chaychi
2004; Khademi et al. 2007; Lamei et al. 2007).
Despite the large quantities of obsidian found in the Lake Urmia region, provenance studies of
these obsidians have been lacking to date. Hence, we attempted to study obsidian implements and
resources by conducting an archaeological survey along with geochemical studies. Such an
approach is based on two assumptions: (1) obsidian produced by different volcanic events are
chemically distinct from one another; (2) the chemical composition of a given obsidian flow is
homogeneous (Constantinescu et al. 2002). In 2005 and 2006, a field survey was initiated in the
eastern Lake Urmia area and, as a consequence of this study, 56 prehistoric sites from Chalcolithic to Bronze Age were discovered, from which 60 pieces of obsidian artefacts were
sampled. Additionally, four obsidian sources were located. We used WDXRF followed by
statistical analysis of the data to investigate the relationship between the obsidian implements and
the geological sources. Here, we present the first set of results from trace element analysis of
obsidian from eastern Lake Urmia.
CULTURAL BACKGROUND AND GEOLOGICAL SETTING
As shown in Figure 1, Lake Urmia is a saline lake some 140 km long, 15–50 km wide, and
located at an elevation of 1280 m a.s.l. in the northern part of the Zagros Mountain chain. The
lake constitutes an internal drainage basin fed by local rivers, such as Zarrine Roud and Simine

© University of Oxford, 2009, Archaeometry 52, 1 (2010) 19–30Roud. Most of the basin represents areas left by the recession of the lake and is characterized by
saline alluvial soils. The region in which Lake Urmia is located is a remarkable zone according
to the complexity of its geological evolution and geomorphological variability.
Ecosystems and human settlements of the lake’s marginal regions have been affected by the
patterns caused by water-level fluctuations of the lake. Significant variability in water level
occurred during the Pleistocene, as reflected in the wider alluvial terraces around the lake (Kelts
and Shahrabi 1985; Bottema 1986). There are two faults stretching on two laterals of the Urmia
depression that separate near Maku, one of which joins the Zarrineh Rud River west of Lake
Urmia and is named the Zarrineh Rud or Urmia fault; the second one, which is named the Tabriz
fault, passes east of Sahand and extends toward the south-east to join the Sultanieh fault (Alayi
Taleghani 2003). The Tabriz fault is one of the most active faults in northwestern Iran. Many
major earthquakes on this fault have been reported during the history of Iran and there is evidence
of the existence of active tectonic plates in its northern areas (Hessami et al. 2003). Much of

Sahand is located about 60 km from Lake Urmia and 40 km south–south-east of Tabriz. It is
one of the highest-altitude peaks in the province of Azarbaijan and is regarded as a stratovolcano
with calc-alkaline chemical characteristics. A considerable number of volcanic domes occur in
the vicinity of this volcanic system. Based on a potassium–argon dating, the age of this volcano
is c. 0.8–1.2 million years (Innocenti et al. 1982), but Karakhanian et al. (2002) suggest that it
was active until the Holocene.
Sabalan is located in 90 km west of Caspian Sea, and the city of Ardabil is located along its
eastern foothills. It is mainly of an andesite structure with an elevation of 4811 m, making it the
highest peak in northwestern Iran and the second highest volcanic crest in Iran. Potassium–argon
dating suggests an age of 1.4 to 5.6 million years (Innocenti et al. 1982), but according to
Karakhanian et al. (2002) it appears that this volcano was active during the Holocene.
The Maku volcanic system is located about 40 km east of Maku, near the border between Iran
and Turkey. An L-shaped region (30 km width on the vertical side and 25 km width on the
horizontal side) is located between the Maku and Nakjavan faults. Karakhanian et al. (2002)
recognized that outcrops of lava flowed here from the eastern Anatolian volcanoes.
Ararat is a twin volcano. The Minor Ararat has an extension of 1000 km2 along the eastern side
of Nemrut Dag. There is evidence from the third millennium bc implying Ararat eruptions.
Additionally, historical documents report that a major earthquake and volcanic eruption occurred
in ad 1840 (Karakhanian et al. 2002). Its lava may have diffused into some parts of the Maku
region.
Long-term archaeological investigations in northwestern Iran started with excavations at the
prehistoric site of Yanik Tepe in eastern Lake Urmia by Charles Burney (Burney 1961, 1962,
1964) and were continued at other sites, such as Hasanlu in the western Lake Urmia area, by
Robert Henry Dyson (Dyson 1968), Haji Firuz (Voight 1983), Dalma (Hamlin 1975) and Pisdeli
(Dyson and Young 1960). Subsequent chronological and cultural recognitions and comparisons
were based on the archaeological data from these first excavations in the region, which led to
identification of the Neolithic period in Haji Firuz, previously regarded as being in cultural
horizon of Hasuna in Mesopotamia (Voight 1983). Chalcolithic cultural material excavated at
Dalma was also compared culturally with that of the Ubaid at Jebel Hamrin in Iraq (Oates 1983).
The material culture of Yanik, which takes its name from a site of the Late Bronze Age, had a
widespread expansion from Luristan in the south (Talai et al. 2004) toward the north into
Armenia (Chataigner et al. 2003), and is called the Trans-Caucasian or Kura Arax culture.
The process of cultural development in eastern Lake Urmia is not well known, but it appears
that a local culture may have developed during the early Chalcolithic (c. 5500 bc). Evidence
supporting the region’s environmental suitability for human adaptation at this time period comes
from sedimentological and palynological information gathered from Lake Urmia (Bottema 1986;
Djamali et al. 2008).
OBSIDIAN ARTEFACTS
During the archaeological surveys conducted in the region in 2005 and 2006, 60 obsidian samples
were collected from 22 prehistoric sites. Obsidian use in the majority of the archaeological sites
east or west of Lake Urmia dates from 5500 to 3500 bc (Chalcolithic) and are attributable to
type 3C obsidians (Voight 1983), although there are a few obsidian types that seem to have been
used much later by Bronze and Iron Age peoples (Mahdavi and Bovington 1972; Ghandgar
22 K. A. Niknami, A. C. Amirkhiz and M. D. Glascock
© University of Oxford, 2009, Archaeometry 52, 1 (2010) 19–302003), and by historic groups of people. Some sites represent a single occupation period, but
multi-component sites containing successive layers of different cultures from the Chalcolithic
until the most recent times of the Islamic era exist throughout the region.
It is important to emphasize that the Palaeolithic period in this region is ambiguous from an
archaeological point of view. We lack a clear picture of the Palaeolithic itself, and the subsequent
cultural transition from Palaeolithic to Neolithic in this part of Iran. It is likely that water-level
fluctuations of Lake Urmia during the late Pleistocene caused a number of Palaeolithic sites to be
submerged. It is also difficult to discuss the climate during this period and whether or not the small
number of Neolithic sites is the result of restricting environmental factors or post-depositional
processes, which both may have led to the disappearance of such sites. The only distinct finding is
a sudden increase in the number of Chalcolithic sites, which account for 90% (20 sites out of 22)
of the sampled sites, a few of which are from a single period, but occasionally with different cultural
layers. A Bronze Age occupation was evident in 68% of the sites and, for unknown reasons, Iron
Age sites represent only 23% of the total sites. In reality, a limiting factor of this research is the
inability to assign artefacts to precise chronological periods, because the samples analysed do not
originate from secure stratigraphic contexts. With the exception of a few samples, most artefacts
were surface collected. We attempted to specify sample dates by comparing them to the similar
diagnostic artefacts found within the chronological layers of controlled excavations.
OBSIDIAN SOURCES
Obsidian is a vitreous rhyolite formed by the rapid cooling of highly viscous lavas (Yacobaccio
et al. 2004). In order to facilitate the identification of geological sources of obsidian, we focused
on the geophysical and the geochemical features of the zone where it was possible to have
obsidian veins or nodules, according to local volcanoes. Although we did not locate any sources
in the most probable areas, the survey did yield positive results. A total of four sources were
identified and sampled for analysis. All were located about the same distance from the archaeological sites (c. 100–150 km, except for Leylan which was at a distance of 50 km). Figure 1
shows the locations of archaeological sites and obsidian sources. The geographical distribution of
obsidian sources in the eastern Urmia region is characterized below.
The first obsidian source is Gonag Giran, located along the southern face of the northern
Bozgoush Mountain and some 17 km south-east of the city of Nir. The obsidian source is linked
to a major volcanic structure mapped by Darvishzadeh (2004). The Bozgoush complex is related
to Pliocene activity, and its main lithological structure contains trachytic lava with intercalated
rhyolite and obsidian layers. In lava flows, obsidian is present in very thin bands and sometimes
inside a micro-crystalline rhyolitic matrix. Although an intact obsidian flow layer is not well
exposed, isolated obsidian nodules and cobbles are sometimes found on the surface. The samples
are often a dark smoky colour and have a fine-grained texture.
The second obsidian source (Shirin Boulag) is located on the same structure of the Bozgoush
complex as Gonag Giran but along the southeastern part of that mountain. Obsidians here are
related to a large structure of andesite dacitic lavas that may have flowed downwards from the
northern Bozgoush volcanoes at different times. The geochemical characteristics of this source
exhibit a completely different bulk composition from Gonag Giran, and it may be that eruption
mechanisms and the degree of devitrification were repeated at different times. Obsidians from
Shirin Boulag show a higher degree of perlitization and are mainly of poor quality. They occur
as small nodules, some exceeding 5 cm on a side and light-green in colour. None of our obsidian
artefact samples matched this source.

10 kg. There is no evidence of rhyolitic units reported for the southern Sahand and Leylan
localities, but the nearest rhyolitic context assumed to be associated with obsidian is reported in
the Bukan highlands, extending to Takht-e-Soleiman (Tekab region) which is located a distance
of 40 to 80 km from Leylan (Vosoghi Abedini and Ghorbani 2001). Leylan obsidian is dark,
smoky, and has a relatively fine-grained texture. The majority of our artefacts can be attributed
geochemically to this source.
In early stage of our research, we were concerned with the lithic fractions collected from the
banks of Garesu River, on the northwestern flank of Sabalan Mountain close to Meshkin Shahr
city. They are physically very similar to those obsidians that we collected from Bozgoush
complex, but our further analysis showed that these materials are not obsidians but instead they
are black cherts that have weathered and flaked away from their original rhyolitic contexts. Our
survey in other parts of this region failed to locate additional obsidian source materials.
A total of four source samples were collected for analysis, one sample from each of the sources
at Gonag Giran, Shirin Boulag, Leylan and Meshkin.
EXPERIMENTAL
Geochemical characterization of the artefacts and volcanic source glasses was performed using
the instrumental WDXRF analysis facilities at the Faculty of Science, University of Tarbiat
Modarres located in Tehran. Because of the destructive nature of WDXRF analysis and the
necessity to limit damage to archaeological materials, we used discarded flakes and broken pieces
of artefacts. A total of 16 elements were measured by WDXRF in each artefact and source
sample, using analytical procedures described previously (Khademi et al. 2007). In brief, the
obsidian samples were analysed on a PANalytical (Philips) PW 2404 (WDX) sequential machine
equipped with a rhodium X-ray tube. The tube was operated at 25 mA and 66 kV to generate
X-ray intensity data. Geochemical groupings are based on a series of bivariate plots used to
compare groups of artefacts with sources of archaeological obsidian as described by Cobean
et al. (1991) and Glascock et al. (1998). The WDXRF results are shown in Table 1.
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Bivariate plots of the major, minor and trace element data for the obsidian artefacts and source
specimens from the Lake Urmia region were studied to find possible compositional groups. As
shown in Figures 2 and 3, the artefact data suggest that at least three compositional groups are
present. The group containing the largest number of artefacts is Group A, with 47 (84% of the
total). Groups B and C were related to four artefacts (7%) and five artefacts (9%), respectively.
The four remaining artefacts (7%) were not included in any groupings because they were
noticeably different from the three main groups and from each other. Means and standard
deviations for the three obsidian compositional groups are listed in Table 2.
The differences between these three geochemical types are revealed through a direct comparison of their average compositions. Group A has the lowest concentrations for Fe, Ce, Hf, Rb, Y,
Zn, and Zr. Group B has the highest concentrations of TiO2 , Ce, and Zn. Group C has the highest
concentrations of Fe, Na2 O, Ba, and Zr.

CONCLUSIONS
Our investigation in the eastern region of Lake Urmia indicates that at least three obsidian sources
are located within the zone surrounding the lake. The three obsidian types identified in this study
are from different magma chambers with different elemental compositions, each indicating a
complex evolutionary history. Two of the sources located at Leylan and Gonag Giran were
exploited to produce obsidian implements. These findings indicate that the local population had
a strong reliance on local sources of obsidian, primarily the Leylan source. This discovery also
demonstrates that a short-distance trade network existed among local Chalcolithic peoples from
the sixth through the mid-fourth millennium bc. All of the Chalcolithic sites exploiting obsidian

Our results confirm the earlier assertions of Burney (1962, see also Burney and Long 1972),
who claimed that the eastern region of Lake Urmia must have utilized locally available obsidian
sources. These conclusions are also supported by previous studies of Lamei et al. (2006) of
obsidian elemental analysis of Aslanduz Tepe in Moghan (dating to the Early Bronze Age),
claiming a local obsidian source for their artefact samples. They made an exploratory comparison
between obsidians of Nader Tepe of Aslanduz with sources from the Caucasus, Armenia and
Turkey. Their source samples were completely different in composition when compared with
specimens from those areas, thus they came to the conclusion that the obsidian artefacts at
Aslanduz might have been derived from a local source, the location of which was unknown.
In the present study, four artefacts have elemental compositions which do not correlate with the
known sources (Bellot-Gurlet et al. 2008). This demonstrates the need for additional obsidian
source studies in the region. In particular, more work is needed to determine the, as yet unknown,
sources of the artefacts, and to investigate why the source of Shirin Boulag was not used. Finally,
it should be noted that studies of obsidian from Iran are still in an early stage. We need to collect
more source samples and conduct more analyses, such as XRF, PIXE and neutron activation
analysis (NAA), which provide accuracy and precision and will help us to explain the complexity
of the obsidian sources in the region in greater detail.
