a b s t r a c t
The Göllüdag obsidian lava flow system is relatively complex. More than ten different obsidian outcrops
and knapping workshops have been identified around this volcano. Their importance for long and short
distances obsidian supply has already been demonstrated.
One of the main problem encountered when studying the Göllüdag obsidian flows, is the great
chemical similarity of some of them. The use of the geochemical approach to solve the problems of
obsidian trade around the Göllüdag is therefore insufficient. A multi-disciplinary program joining
archaeology, geochemistry and geomorphology, was thus started to understand the relationships
between the different obsidian flows and to draw the pattern of the Göllüdag obsidian trade.
Our archaeological and geomorphologic approach will help us to understand which obsidian outcrops
were available during the Palaeolithic and Neolithic periods.
Also our geomorphologic approach, linked to intensive geochemical analyses, will help us to understand the spatial variability of the geochemistry of the obsidian outcrops. Lithic technology will also give
information about the different knapping methods used at the workshops.

1. Introduction
The importance of the Göllüdag obsidian knapping workshops for
long and short distances obsidian supply during the Neolithic period
has already been demonstrated (William-Thorpe, 1995; Cauvin and
Chataigner, 1998; Chataigner, 1998; Gratuze, 1999). Obsidian from
this volcano is found in Aceramic Neolithic sites of Cyprus (Briois
et al., 1997; Finlayson et al., 1998; Peltenburg et al., 2000;
McCartney and Gratuze, 2003) and in different Pre-pottery
Neolithic (PPNA and PPNB) sites of the Levant and Mesopotamia
(Chataigner, 1998; Abbès et al., 2001). During the Early PPNB, its
exploitation is based upon craft specialisation (Balkan-Atlı and Binder,
2001; Binder, 2002).
The different identified obsidian outcrops (Fig. 1) are located on
the southern (Sırca deresi and Büyük Göllü Tepe), western (Bozköy
Ilbiz, Bozköy Muneninyeri, Bozköy Boztepe, Kayırlı village, Gösterli),
northern (Birtlikeler and Ekinlik) and eastern slopes of the Göllüdag
(Eriklidere, Kaletepe deresi 2, Kaletepe deresi 3 downstream, lower
upstream and upper upstream-, Kömürcü) of the Göllüdag (BalkanAtlı et al., 2008). The obsidians outcrop within rhyolithic and perlitic
extrusions and intrusions as well as bombs within the pyroclastic
deposits. The obsidian outcrops associated with rhyolithic and
perlitic lava provide a wide range of grey shades and opacities while
those associated with pyroclastites display transparent slightly grey
glasses.
Archaeological evidences of knapping activities have been
recovered on the obsidian outcrops of Eriklidere, Kaletepe,
Birtlikeler, Ekinlik, Bozköy Ilbiz and Bozköy Boztepe (Balkan-Atlı
et al., 1998; Balkan-Atlı et al., 1999). Among them well preserved
workshops have been identified and sounded or excavated as
Kaletepe, locus P (EPPNB), and locus M (PN), but the first traces of
obsidian exploitation date from the Early and Middle Palaeolithic
periods (Slimak et al., 2008).
Previous works done either by EDXRF (Keller and Seifried, 1990),
neutron activation analysis (Yellin, 1995), ICP-AES and ICP-MS
(Bellot-Gurlet, 1998) or LA-ICP-MS (Gratuze, 1999) have shown
that among the different obsidian outcrops occurring on the
Göllüdag massif, some could be easily differentiated by chemical

2. Methods
In order to check the possibilities or the impossibilities to
discriminate between the most important outcrops where knapping
workshops have been identified, new campaigns of sampling were
undertaken in 2000e2002 by the Franco-Turkish archaeological
mission (Balkan-Atlı et al., 2008). Also intensive field work has been
undertaken with the aim of understanding volcanic activity and the
morphological evolution of the Göllüdag complex (Mouralis, 2003).
Fig. 1 shows that three outcrops have not been sampled in the
framework of this study: i) Kayırlı-village, ii) south of Bozdag and iii)
north of Paleo-Büyük Göllü Tepe. The obsidians from Kayırlı-village
have been previously analysed by Gratuze (1999), but their precise
location was not known. The Kayırlı-village outcrop is a pyroclastic
flow that has probably destroyed a previous dome. The obsidian
forms a small part of this pyroclastite and has perlitic facies. It has
probably not been used for knapping and has not been sampled
again for this new study. The obsidians located south of Bozdag and
north of Paleo-Büyük Tepe are associated with perlite and rhyolite
and show very rare pure obsidian outcrops.
Only geologic obsidian samples originating from well identified
outcrops have been collected and analysed, in order to sample the
main known outcrops associated with the different domes.
The relationship between ancient (Gratuze, 1999) and recent
studied obsidian outcrops is given in Table 1, a more extensive
correlation between the different outcrops names used by different
authors could be found in the work done by Chataigner (Chataigner,
1998). The samples as well as the outcrops and the chemical groups
are located in Fig. 1. The names used in the map and in the text are
those of the 1:25,000 topographic maps. Based on our previous
results, particular attention has been given to elements such as
zirconium, yttrium, niobium, barium, strontium, boron, lithium and
titanium that appear to be more powerful in establishing a fine
discrimination between outcrops with similar chemical compositions.
A whole of 108 analyses have been carried out on the obsidian
taken from the different sampling points (the full list is given in
Annexe, for each sampling point two or more analyses were carried
out; mean compositions and standard deviations are given in
Table 2). The analytical method used for this study was laser
ablation inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (LA-ICPMS). The retained operating procedure has already been described
(Gratuze, 1999; Gratuze et al., 2001) and will not be repeated here.
Although the accuracy and the precision of LA-ICP-MS are not as
good as those obtained using ICP-MS in its liquid mode, LA-ICP-MS
offers the possibility of fast multi-elements determination without
damaging the sample (sampling remains invisible to the naked
eye). For archaeological studies it thus allows to study a large
number of artefacts and then to base the conclusions on a statistical
population of artefacts rather than on a few selected objects.

3.1. The obsidian outcrops inside the Göllüdag volcanic complex
The Göllüdag massif is a rhyolitic complex that is Lower and
Middle Pleistocene in age. The rhyolitic domes are extruded inside
a highly probable caldera and form a cumulodome by coalescence
of the extruded domes. The obsidian outcrops are associated with
these extrusions. The most elevated of these domes is the Büyük
Göllü Tepe, 2172 m in altitude. Ten domes have been recognised
(Fig. 1), but some of them (Kabak Tepe and Büyük Göllü Tepesi)
contain fossilised previous domes (further named Palaeo-Kabak
Tepe and Palaeo-Büyük Göllü Tepe, Fig. 2) as well as caldera
structures.
Five field evidences allow an understanding of the relationships
between obsidian outcrops and the domes extruded during this
“post-caldera” stage.
(i) The obsidian outcrops draw discontinuous circular shapes
around most of the domes and may occur within phreatomagmatic deposits. The Küçük Göllü Tepe is not associated
with any obsidian outcrops: this elongated dome-coulée may
have fossilised previous obsidian.
(ii) The obsidian never outcrops as pure lava flow. The outcrops are
generally associated with the rhyolitic and perlitic extrusion,
whereas, in some cases, the sub-vertical foliation plans suggest
intrusion emplacement. The Eriklidere obsidian outcrops with
their lateral component seem to be an exception.
(iii) The obsidian outcrops often show transition between the
massive “heart” of the outcrops and the surrounding perlitic
lava. We notice transitional facies between obsidian and
perlite either shown by alternating obsidian and perlite centimetric to millimetric foliation or by enrichment in perlitic
spherolites.
(iv) Some obsidian outcrops are disconnected from the dome,
because of later volcanic activity as well as later erosion that
have either fossilised or disturbed previous structures.
(v) Some obsidian blocks (bombs) occur in the pyroclastites
indicating an explosive volcanic activity after emplacement of
the obsidian.
According to these field observations, Mouralis (Mouralis
et al., 2002; Mouralis, 2003) has proposed a model that
describes the extrusions of the domes including four volcanic
stages (Fig. 3):
(i) Tuff ring may be seen around some of the domes (e.g.: Kabak
Tepe, Bozdag) indicating a phreatomagmatic activity that has
been then fossilised by the rhyolitic extrusion.
(ii) In the same time, obsidian and perlitic ring-dykes have been
intruded, probably using the fractures limiting the diatrem of
the maar. These ring dikes have thus a general circular shape
and are associated to most of the domes.
(iii) The terminal dome is extruded inside the previous structures.
Some of the domes have simple morphologies, but many of
them show multiple lobes.

3.2. Chemical results: 7 main different groups of obsidians
The analytical results obtained on this new collection of
geological samples show that using simple binary diagrams, the
obsidian from the Göllüdag can be easily splited into four main
chemical groups as shown on Figs. 4 and 5. The obsidian originating
respectively from Bozköy Ilbiz-Muneninyeri, Gösterli and Büyük
Göllü Tepe form the three first groups (Göllüdag 1e3). Göllüdag 1 is
equivalent to our ancient group West Göllüdag 1 while Göllüdag 2
corresponds to West Göllüdag 2. The new Göllüdag 2 chemical
group has the same signature than the former Kayırlı-village,
previously analysed by Gratuze (1999) whose samples were not
precisely located. This new Göllüdag 2 chemical group has the
advantage of having been precisely located and sampled on a well
defined outcrop.
In the fourth chemical group, we find all the other obsidian
outcrops: Birtlikeler, Ekinlik, Eriklidere, Sırca Deresi, Bozköy
Boztepe and the different outcrops of Kaletepe.
In fact although the chemical compositions of these outcrops are
close, we observe, mainly for strontium and barium, a gradual
evolution of their composition, and thus chemical distinction of
some of these obsidian outcrops appears to be possible. In order to
obtain an easy graphical representation of the results, we choose to
run principal component analysis and to plot the analytical data on
an X/Y graph where the two axes are expressed as a linear
combination of different chemical elements which correspond to
the factor 1 and 2 of the PCA. It is thus possible to visualise all the
different outcrops on this single complex binary diagram (Figs. 6
and 7). The large fourth group could then be divided into four
smaller groups, one of them being divided into two subgroups.
Hence, the chemical data allow us to identify seven main compositional groups among the thirteen sampled outcrops. Three chemical
groups refer to single outcrops while in the four others groups we find
the eight remaining outcrops (Table 3).

to some of the obsidian found in the Birtlikeler ravine, while the

4. Discussion
4.1. The obsidian outcrops: ring-dikes associated
with rhyolitic domes
This paper has detailed and explained the variability of Göllüdag
obsidian resources that was still unsolved recently despite
a presentation of our preliminary results in 2003 (Gratuze et al.,
2005) and further questions asked by Poupeau et al. (2005). The
geochemical results presented here are in a good accordance with
the field observations and with the volcanic evolution of the
Göllüdag complex. Previous research has shown the high spatial
complexity of the outcrops. It was impossible to understand the
spatial distribution of the obsidian outcrops without understanding
the volcanic structures associating the obsidian outcrops and the
domes. Fig. 1 and Table 3 shows that most of the domes are associated with one or more obsidian outcrops that have their own
chemical signatures: Göllüdag 1, 2, 4b and 6 are respectively
associated with Ilbiz Tepe, Kalebası Tepe, Kayıskıran and Boztepe.
Göllüdag 7 is associated with Palaeo-Büyük Göllü Tepe, whereas
Göllüdag 3 is associated with the newest Büyük Göllü Tepe that cut
and fill previous one (Fig. 2). A similar structures is shown in the
case of Kabak Tepe : Göllüdag 5 is associated with Palaeo-Kabak
Tepe whereas Göllüdag 4a is associated with the newest Kabak
Tepe. Our observations explain that some outcrops that are closed
in the field (i.e. Birtlikeler and Ekinlik) have different geochemical
signatures whereas some outcrops that are more distant (e.g. Eriklidere and Kaletepe 2) belong to the same obsidian structure and
thus have the same geochemical signature.
It is noticed that the obsidians sampled in the Kaletepe Deresi 3
“Upper upstream” (KT102e104) belong to Göllüdag 4b (“Ekinlik”).
However, following our observations (Fig. 1), these samples should
belong to the chemical group Göllüdag 4a (“Birtlikeler”, Kabak
Tepe). As previously noticed, there is very low chemical difference
between these two groups. The component analyses (Figs. 6 and 7)
as well as the Sr/Ba ratio (Figs. 4 and 5) show that Göllüdag 4a
and Göllüdag 4b form a continuum. Such a continuum may be explained by volcanic eruptions very close in time or even contemporaneous so that no differentiation occurs in the magmatic
chamber.
At least, we notice that, with the exception of these three
samples, all of the other analytical results are in good accordance
with our field observations.
4.2. Morphological evolution of the Gölludag complex and
accessibility of the obsidian outcrops
All these obsidian outcrops where not used in the same time by
prehistoric people, depending of the quality as well as the accessibility of the outcrops. However, we have still rare information
about the morphological evolution of the Göllüdag complex after
the end of volcanic activity. The detritic accumulations from
reworking Göllüdag material (pumices, rhyolite, obsidian) in the
plains all around the massif, eastward (Derinkuyu plain), northward (Kitreli plain) and southward (Ciftlik plain) as well as the
alluvial cone in the eastern part of the massif evidence some
erosion phase that have still not been dated.
In the Acigöl complex, 80 km northward of Göllüdag, Mouralis
(2003) has shown two major morphological evolutions during the
Upper Pleistocene and Late Glacial. The first occurs between 100 ka
and 30 ka and is responsible of large accumulation of detritic
formations (alluviums and colluviums) constructing some glaciscones. The second occurs after 30 ka with the erosion of the
previous morphologies. Even if this chronology is still imprecise, it
shows at least two major erosion stages occurring during the Upper
Pleistocene. The second one should explain the scarcity of Upper
Palaeolithic sites around the Acigöl and Gölludag massifs.
4.3. Archaeological application and new issues
In order to test our model from an archaeological point of view,
we have reconsidered the results previously obtained for one
hundred and three artefacts from the pre-pottery Neolithic site of
Shillourokambos (Cyprus). These artefacts were analysed a few
years ago using both fast neutron activation analysis and LA-ICP-MS
(Briois et al., 1997; Gratuze, 1999). At that time, we were not able to
fully distinguish between the areas of Birtlikeler-Ekinlik and Kaletepe (East Göllü-Dag sub-group 1 and 2), and the geological source
of the artefacts was just defined as East Göllüdag without anymore
precision. Using the new data and analyses, it is now possible to
separate the artefacts into three main groups (Fig. 8). Sixty two
artefacts are similar to the obsidian originating from KaletepeEriklidere (Göllüdag 5), twenty-five artefacts have the same
composition than the obsidian found at Birtlikeler (Göllüdag 4a),
and sixteen artefacts have an intermediate composition which is
related to Ekinlik (Göllüdag 4b). As during the Neolithic period, the
outcrop located at Kaletepe deresi 3 upper upstream was not
accessible; it means that these obsidians from Göllüdag 4a and 4b
originate from the Birtlikeler and Ekinlik ravines. Thus ca. 60% of
the obsidian tools recovered at Shillourokambos originates from
the surroundings of Kaletepe/Eriklidere and ca. 40% from the
Birtlikeler-Ekinlik area. We shall notice here that the artefacts from

4.4. New issues can be raised thanks to these results
As far as we can observe today, the different obsidian sources
that have been identified and analysed all around the Göllüdag are
not interchangeable. From a knapping perspective, major differences are due (i) to the dimensions and shapes of the available
blocks and (ii) to the intrinsic quality of the glass (e.g. fragility,
presence of perlitic inclusions, etc.). Among the currently known
sources, two of them appear to have been mainly exploited during
the PPN period on the basis of surveys and excavations conducted
since 1993: they are dark-grey obsidians from Göllüdag 5 ryolithic
lava and transparent ones from Göllüdag 4. Both have been
exploited for craft productions during the Early PPNB, i.e. prismatic
pressure bladelets diffused in the whole Near-East and points
obtained from highly specialised naviform cores of a Levantine
tradition (Balkan-Atlı and Der-Apprahamian, 1998; Binder and
Balkan-Atlı, 2001; Binder, 2002). Surprising is the lack of field
evidence for an exploitation at that time of the transparent obsidian
outcrops around Eriklidere (Göllüdag 5).
For both types of craft productions, Göllüdag 5 dark-grey and
Göllüdag 4 transparent obsidians do not offer the same interests.
Göllüdag 5 provides large blocks in which standardised pre-cores
for pressure or bipolar blade technique can be easily inscribed.
On the other hand, Göllüdag 4 bombs of transparent glass exhibit
a concentric layered structure, splitting in convexo-concave prisms
in which shaping out pre-cores appears much more hazardous.
Concerning the mechanic qualities of the glass itself, one can
notice that Göllüdag 4 transparent obsidian is generally free of
perlites while the Göllüdag 5 dark-grey obsidian sometimes contains
a few. But the major advantage of Göllüdag 5 dark-grey, compared to
Göllüdag 4 transparent, is its lower fragility that gives more flexibility
for the use and combination of different knapping techniques: pressure as well as direct or indirect percussion.
In a techno-economic perspective, we can assume a trend for
craftsmen to use first Göllüdag 5 dark-grey for making blade using
complex methods. Thus, the detailed analysis of obsidians discovered
at long distance consumption sites, could be an indicator of the
evolution of resource availability and could then explain the evolution
of the strategies of the craftsmen them-selves. Could a depletion of the
best Cappadocian sources from Göllüdag 5 explain the decrease of PPN
craft activities at that place and a major change in resource takeover?
Actually at the very end of the 8th millennium cal BCE and
during the Middle and Late PPNB, preliminary analyses from Gratuze (unpublished) indicate that Göllüdag 4 was the main resource
exploited at As ̧ ıklı Höyük and Musular, which lithic traditions
proceed from a syncretism between Levant PPNB and local Epipalaeolithic; these obsidians were then used for simpler blade
productions, most of them produced directly at the consumption
sites (Abbès et al., 1999; Binder, 2002).
At Çatalhöyük, recent analyses identified East-Göllüdag
outcrops as the origin of a part of the earliest PN industries (levels
IX to VII-VI) (Carter et al., 2006, Carter and Shackley, 2007). EastGöllüdag obsidians were actually employed for knapping irregular
small blades and for bifacial productions. Some of the latest (e.g.
OB63 and OB64) have high Ba values that could be correlated with
Göllüdag 5, and even Kaletepe M workshop (Dere 2 upstream)
where pyroclastites provide slabs at the origin of a massive
production of bifacial pieces. Unfortunately we are not able to
compare these results with our data since the measurement of
one of the most distinctive elements (Ba), for differentiating
between the sources of East- Göllüdag, is not presented in this
paper.
5. Conclusion
According to geomorphological, chemical and archaeological
evidences, our study allows to distinguish around the Göllüdag massif
7 main obsidian groups, some of them including many outcrops. This
work allows thus a more accurate sourcing of the obsidian artefacts:
what was previously named “Göllüdag-East”, is currently divided in 3
different chemical groups. Moreover, the comparison with chemical
analyses of artefacts shows that the major source during the Neolithic
times are located eastward (Göllüdag 5) and northward (Göllüdag 4)
of Kabak Tepe. Our results show however that a new sampling
campaign in the Birtlikeler-Ekinlik area is needed to clarify the relationships between these two zones and to validate or unvalidate the
splitting of group 4 into two subgroups.

Annexe 1. Brief description of the Göllüdag outcrops and raw
materials
Sampling of obsidian sources have been done in order to procure
enough material to constitute three reference series (A, B, C).
 Series A have been analysed at the UMR5060 (IRAMAT, CNRS
and Orleans University) for this paper.
 Series B have provided thin sections for future Raman spectrometry studies at the UMR7075 (LADIR CNRS and Paris VI
University).
 From series C, two sets have been separated as reference
collections for archaeologists: C1 in Turkey at the Department of
Prehistory of Istanbul University; C2 in France at the UMR6130
(CEPAM eCNRS and Nice Sophia-Antipolis University).
Göllüdag 1 (Ilbiz Tepe dome) At Bozköy, Ilbiz site provides slabs
of transparent and slightly banded obsidian. Two sample groups
have been collected: BO1.1 is an in situ obsidian deposit associated
with perlite, cut by the road and covered by a layer of workshop and
quarrying wastes including long pickaxes made of rhyolite; BO1.2
consists of big rough pieces from the surface.
Still at Bozköy, Muneninyeri site shows veins of obsidian included
within perlite lava that contain many spherolites. Two different
aspects have been sampled: BO2.1 obsidian is opaque black- brown,
found as fractured blocks of 20e30 cm of module; BO2.2 is a more
glassy one and contains spherolithes. Lithic wastes and tools (e.g.
arrowhead) have been discovered upward this outcrop.
Göllüdag 2 (Kalebas ̧ ı Tepe dome) After our survey, in situ
obsidians appeared very rare within this impressive edifice destroyed
by a pelean episode and further erosion. Small nodules of a few cm
have been collected in the rhyolithic lava flow overhanging Gösterli
dere (GÖ1.1, not analysed here). Most of the samples are pebbles of
10e20 cm of module, coming from the alluvial deposits of the dere;
GÖ2.1 is a black to greenish banded obsidian; GÖ2.2 is much glassy
and homogoneous but contains big spherolithes. No knapping activity
has been found in the surroundings.
Göllüdag 3 (Büyük Göllü Tepe dome) A single big block of
a light grey and metalescent obsidian (BG1.1) have been sampled by
Dr. F. S ̧ aroglu at ca. 2000 m high on the southern slope of the Büyük
göllü tepesi.
Göllüdag 4 (Kabak Tepe newest and Kayıskıran Tepe (?)
domes) South-west to Kayırlı village, the hills of Birtlikeler and
Ekinlik are drained by many ravines (or dere) that reveal rhyolitic
lava flows and pyroclastites. The top and the slopes of Bitlikeler hill
are covered of wastes coming from an important PPN Neolithic
workshop, unfortunately eroded. Four groups of samples of grey
and slightly banded obsidian have been collected as blocks within
the rhyolitic flow: KY1.1 et KY1.2 from the ravine between Birtlikeler and Ekinlik hills; KY1.3 in the rhyolitic slope of Birtlikeler
hill, covered with lithic wastes; KY1.5 in the rhyolitic slope of
Ekinlik hill. Three groups have been sampled inside the perlite
deposits: KY1.4 from the slope of Birtlikeler; KY.1.6 and KY1.7from
the Ekinlik ravines; the latter obsidian samples consist of big blocks
of transparent grayish obsidian with a natural concentric debit.
Obsidian with a similar composition, and then reported to the
same group of volcanic events, have been collected at KömürcüKaletepe e dere 3-upper upstream. There, the rhyolitic flow has
been discovered after an erosion of the Palaeolithic deposits during
the winter 2001e2002. Three samples of transparent and very
slightly banded obsidian have been collected: KT10.2, KT.10.3 and
KT10.4. Covered by Pleistocene colluviums and Palaeolithic deposits,
this outcrop was probably unknown from Neolithic people.
Göllüdag 5 (Palaeo-Kabak Tepe dome) Above Kömürcü village,
the area corresponding to the visible extension of Kaletepe workshops is intersected by two main ravines: Dere 2 and Dere 3. Two
types of deposits have been sampled. The rhyolitic flow provides
large blocks (reaching sometimes 30e40 cm) of homogeneous
dark-grey obsidian of an exceptional quality, with very rare small
perlites (reaching very exceptionally 1 cm of diameter). Is has been
sampled: at different levels in the Dere 3 lower upstream and
downstream on both sides of the Chalcolithic operation (BalkanAtlı et al., 1998) (KT1.1, KT1.2, KT1.3; KT.2.1, KT2.2, KT2.3; KT3.1;
KT4.1, KT4.2; KT5.1 and KT5.2); at the Dere 2 downstream, close to
the PPNB operation (Binder, Balkan-Atlı, 2001) (KT6.1 and KT7.1).
This is the type of obsidian almost exclusively knapped at the
Kaletepe PPNB workshop. At the Dere 2 upstream, pyroclastic
deposits (ashes and breccias) contain slabs of transparent obsidian
rich and even very rich in bubbles (KT8.1, KT8.2, KT8.3). Some of
them are perfectly suitable for bifacial shaping and were obviously
used at the nearby Pottery Neolithic workshop (Kaletepe M).
Surface finds also indicate the use of these slabs for Middle Palaeolithic Levallois productions.
The ravine of Eriklidere, situated at the north-west of Kömürcü
village, intersects two types of deposits providing very large
amounts of obsidian previously used as standard references for
defining “Kömürcü obsidian”. Obsidian from the upstream rhyolites
deposits (ED1.1. and ED1.2) forms a huge flow, extremely rich of big
perlitic spherolits (over than 2 cm diameter), only small blocks of
transparent obsidian can be saved from the spherolitic gangue.
Downstream pyroclastites display blocks of transparent grayish
obsidian with a natural concentric debit suitable for knapping
(ED2.1 et ED3.1).
Göllüdag 6 (Boz Tepe dome) Two samples (BO3.1 and BO3 .3) of
a dark-grey obsidian have been collected from large blocks within
eroded perlites. This raw material seems of a poor quality, due to
many perlitic spherolites and cracks. Nevertheless the same area
provided some Middle Paleolithic artifacts (i.e. Levallois core).
Göllüdag 7 (Palaeo-Büyük Göllüdag dome) Sırça deresi
outcrops close to Bozköy have been explored by companies
exploiting perlites; that let some uncertainties about the field
conditions. Obsidian samples coming from in situ perlites are black,
with moiré aspects (SD1.1), or slightly translucent (SD1.2). Samples
in the post-depositional context of the perlite quarries are marbled,

The use of these obsidian sources, as little blocks and pebbles from
secondary deposits, is probably attested de visu at the ÇiftlikTepecik, a close Pottery Neolithic and Chalcolithic settlement
(Biçakcı et al., 2007).
