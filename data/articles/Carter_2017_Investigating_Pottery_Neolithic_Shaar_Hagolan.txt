A B S T R A C T
This paper details the characterisation of 34 obsidian artefacts from Sha'ar Hagolan in the Jordan Valley, a major
Pottery Neolithic Site of the southern Levantine Yarmukian culture (6400–6000 cal BCE). Employing an integrated approach that melds sourcing data from EDXRF spectroscopy with the artefacts’ techno-typological
characteristics, we contrast Sha'ar Hagolan's lithic traditions with those of the Pre-Pottery Neolithic southern
Levantine sites in the context of alleged socio-economic disruptions in the Pottery Neolithic. The results indicate
that community's obsidian consumption habits largely followed deep-time regional traditions (with only the
slightest decrease in relative quantities), i.e. the use of Cappadocian raw materials (Göllü Dağ and Nenezi Dağ) to
make pressure blades, and occasional projectiles, with only a small proportion of eastern Anatolian products
(Nemrut Dağ). While the Sha'ar Hagolan material seems to embody continuity of southern Levantine cultural
tradition, other broadly contemporary assemblages attest to the initiation of new procurement networks, and
novel modes of consumption that reflect the increasing degree of cultural heterogeneity of the period. Finally,
the distribution of obsidian across the site does not support the idea that social distinction at Sha'ar Hagolan was
part-based on the preferential access to these exotic resources.
1. Introduction
This paper details the elemental characterisation and sourcing of the
obsidian used to make 34 obsidian artefacts from the 8th–6th millennium cal BC Neolithic site of Sha'ar Hagolan in the southern Levant
(Fig. 1). Obsidian was an exceedingly rare resource for this community,
comprising a mere 0.01% of its chipped stone assemblage. Given that a
mere 38 obsidian artefacts were generated from the excavation of a site
that housed several thousand inhabitants over a period of ~400 years,
most people at Sha'ar Hagolan would never have handled this material
(the original publication details 39 pieces but one was shown to be
made of flint [see below]). Entire generations may have passed during
which no new obsidian tools were imported, or made at the site. Obsidian's rarity at Sha'ar Hagolan can be part-explained by the fact that it
was an exotic resource, the nearest sources located some 630 km linear
distance to the north in central Anatolia (Fig. 1), its procurement almost
certainly involving intermediary exchange rather than expeditions to
the quarries. As such, the ownership, transformation and use of such
media may have related more to issues of social capital, than utilitarian
concerns (cf. Helms, 1988, 1993).
Undertaking this sourcing project, four primary research aims were
in mind. Firstly, we were interested in situating our analyses in a longterm view of obsidian consumption in the region as a means of engaging with prior claims that the Pottery Neolithic [PN] was a period of
socio-economic disruption and “retrogression” (Kenyon, 1960: 67–78).
In short, does the Sha'ar Hagolan data support the idea of a significant
diminishment and/or reconfiguration of obsidian use by southern Levantine populations? By extent, we would be redressing a major research bias, where obsidian sourcing studies have tended to focus on
Epi-Palaeolithic (hunter-gatherer) and earliest Neolithic (farming) assemblages as a means of contributing to debates surrounding ‘neolithisation’ (e.g. Carter et al., 2013; Delerue, 2007, inter alia). Secondly,
we wished to consider the claim that social distinction at Sha'ar Hagolan was partly constituted through preferential access to non-local
goods (Garfinkel et al., 2012: 119); i.e. did the obsidian come from the
larger and more complex buildings in association with other exotica?

Fourthly, we wished to contextualise Sha'ar Hagolan's raw material

of people whose common traditions were underpinned by close social
relations, articulated via spousal exchange, trade partnerships, and
other forms of binding alliances. The latter concern relates to our research group's larger interest in reconstructing socio-economic interaction from the ground-up via a form of ‘thick description’ characterisation study (e.g. Carter et al., 2005, 2013). Ultimately such an
approach enables us to examine how populations became bound together through the development of shared traditions, working under
the premise that what we are revealing about raw material and technical choices ultimately part-constituted these peoples' very identity
(Dietler and Herbich, 1998: 246–47; Dobres, 2000).
2. Background to Sha'ar Hagolan and the Yarmukian culture
The site of Sha'ar Hagolan is located on the river Yarmuk, a tributary of the great Jordan River in northern Israel (central Jordan Valley),
1.5 km south of the Sea of Galilee (Figs. 1–2). The site was first excavated from 1949 to 1952 (Stekelis, 1951, 1972), followed by a further
11 seasons from 1989 to 1990, and 1996–2004 (Garfinkel and Miller,
2002; Garfinkel et al., 2012). Sha'ar Hagolan comprises the type site for
the PN Yarmukian culture, i.e. the first pottery producing communities
of the southern Levantine Mediterranean belt (Israel, Jordan, Lebanon,
and Palestine [Garfinkel, 1993, 2002]). Before the excavations at Sha'ar
Hagolan, the PN of the southern Levant was largely viewed as a period

and contemporary cultures to the north. The PPNB has been characterised as a period of pan-Levantine cultural traditions, with large
villages of 8 ha (Bar-Yosef and Belfer-Cohen, 1989), whereas the PN
world was a “mosaic of regional cultures”, with semi-nomadic populations and limited architectural investment (Garfinkel and Miller,

by the so-called Yarmukian, Jericho IX, and slightly later Wadi Raba
cultures (Garfinkel, 1993; Gopher and Gophna, 1993). This period of
cultural “retrogression” (Kenyon, 1960: 67–78) is claimed to have been
partly-due to a shift from an economy based on agriculture to a greater
reliance on pastoralism in the first half of the 7th millennium BC (BarYosef, 1992: 31–38; Rollefson, 1989: 170; Wasse, 2002).
The Yarmukian is dated between 6400 and 5800 cal BCE
(~ 7500–7000 BP), by 23 radiometric determinations, 17 of which were
derived from Sha'ar Hagolan itself (Garfinkel, 1999; Garfinkel et al.,
2012: 103–106, Fig. 6, Table 1; Gopher and Gophna, 1993: 307–317).
Typical for the site and culture were ceramics with herring-bone decoration, coarsely denticulated flint sickle-blades, and numerous schematic anthropomorphic figurines (Stekelis, 1951). While the southern
Levantine Pottery Neolithic [PN] was largely perceived to be a period of
diminished social complexity and settlement size, Sha'ar Hagolan constituted a large (c. 20 ha), densely populated settlement, one of the
major Near Eastern sites of the period (Garfinkel et al., 2012: 97–101).
In turn, while Yarmukian sites had been characterised as architecturally
poor, consisting of part-sunken circular huts, and/or rudimentary round
and rectangular structures (Garfinkel, 1993: 127–129), Sha'ar Hagolan
had monumental courtyard buildings (Garfinkel and Ben-Shlomo,

3. Research questions and methodology
This study's basic aim is to source the obsidian used to make the
Sha'ar Hagolan artefacts. This is achieved by matching elemental fingerprints of artefact raw materials with geological samples from known
volcanic sources, the chemical analyses undertaken using an energydispersive X-ray fluorescence spectrometer [EDXRF]. These data when
integrated with other aspects of the finds' character, will enable us to
engage with a series of larger debates about the nature of the community, and its location within local, regional, and supra-regional
socio-economic dynamics. These research questions can be described as
follows:
Firstly, given the claimed decline in socio-economic complexity, and
the reconfiguration of lifeways from the PPNB to PN, do we simultaneously view significant changes in the level and nature of obsidian
consumption amongst southern Levantine communities? In a related
vein, is there an increased regional diversity with regard to the raw
materials circulating and the means by which they were worked in the
context of the claimed shift from a pan-Levantine tradition of the PPNB
to the ‘mosaic’ of regional cultures in the PN? This first question necessitates a long-term regional perspective on obsidian consumption,
drawing on the major studies of Chataigner (1998), and Delerue (2007)
amongst others. We also employ a social network analysis [SNA] approach based upon a recent study by Batist (2014).
Secondly, to what extent is the argument that foreign goods were
preferentially consumed by the residents of larger buildings borne out
by a detailed contextual analysis of the Sha'ar Hagolan obsidian?
Thirdly, given the apparently distinct nature of Sha'ar Hagolan in its
wider PN southern Levantine context, can one view distinctions in
obsidian consumption amongst other Yarmukian culture populations?
Fourthly, how does the Sha'ar Hagolan data fit into more general patterns of supra-regional obsidian circulation at this time? Here we consider which other (broadly) contemporary communities were accessing
the same raw materials and the means by which they were working
them. The aim would be to situate Sha'ar Hagolan within a particular
tradition of consumption (or ‘community of practice’) as a means elucidating at a more detailed resolution the socio-economic networks
these people were participating within. Conversely, can we document
parallel regional traditions that the Sha'ar Hagolan community did not
engage in?
Methodologically we approach these questions through an integrated characterisation study, a ‘thick description’ method (Geertz,
1973: 3–30; Hodder, 1999: 66–79) that situates metrical data, technotypological information and raw material sourcing within a chaîne
opératoire analytical framework (e.g. Carter et al., 2013). Such an approach generates a rich evidential basis for documenting localised
traditions of practice and their changes through time (when contrasted
with earlier assemblages from the same region) and by extent a means
of interrogating inter-community relations (cf. Lightfoot et al., 1998).
4. The Sha'ar Hagolan chipped stone assemblage
Excavations at Sha'ar Hagolan generated almost one million pieces
of chipped stone, of which approximately a third have been studied in
detail (Garfinkel et al., 2012: 120). The assemblage is dominated by
artefacts made from flint pebbles, locally available in abundance from
the adjacent Yarmuk River, together with lesser quantities of flint nodules from a different source, plus handfuls of implements made of
limestone and obsidian (Alperson and Garfinkel, 2002: 146–147). Obsidian artefacts constitute a minute proportion of the chipped stone
assemblage. While the final figures are not available, the 2002 report
details 34,583 artefacts (99.8% flint), of which 10 were obsidian, a
mere 0.03% of the total from the 1989–90 and 1996 seasons (Alperson
and Garfinkel, 2002: 146–147, Table 10.1). We are thus dealing with 39
artefacts recovered from an area that housed several thousand inhabitants over a period of ~ 1200 years, which would equate to approximately 1–1.6 pieces of obsidian entering the site per generation
(based on a 25–30 year estimate [see Coale et al., 1983]). It thus becomes clear that obsidian artefacts would have been very rare to the
people of Sha'ar Hagolan, a rarity that no doubt part-constituted their
significance to those who were able to access such media (see Helms,
1988). On the face of it, such tiny quantities of obsidian are perhaps
unsurprising given that the closest sources lay some 630 km (as-thecrow-flies) away in the Cappadocian region of central Anatolia (Fig. 1).
That said, as we discuss below, larger relative amounts of obsidian were
being procured both by contemporary, and earlier communities of the
same region, indicating that (linear) distance from source cannot be
viewed as the sole influence on the raw materials' distribution.
Technologically, the local flint was worked by four knapping traditions (Garfinkel et al., 2012: 120), mainly the manufacture of ad hoc
flakes, together with lesser quantities of bifacial tools, plus unipolar,
and opposed platform blades (Barzilai and Garfinkel, 2006). Amongst
the modified tools, denticulated prismatic blades with glossed edges
(‘sickles’) were dominant. There were also scrapers, awls/borers, notched/denticulated implements (without gloss), truncated pieces,
burins, a range of short blade-based tanged projectiles (‘arrowheads’),
and bifacial axes (Alperson and Garfinkel, 2002: 162–174,
Figs. 10.13–10.25).
The obsidian at Sha'ar Hagolan was worked using at least two distinct knapping traditions. Dominant are pressure-flaked blades from
unipolar cores, followed by a few laminar products from percussionbased opposed platform cores. Obsidian tools were intensively used,
with over three-quarters of the assemblage having macroscopic usewear (n = 27/79%). In turn, almost a quarter of the blanks had been
deliberately modified into tools sensu strictu (n = 8/34, 24%), a significantly higher proportion that witnessed in the flint component of
the chipped stone (3% [Alperson and Garfinkel, 2002: Table 10.1]). The
obsidian implements included two end-scrapers, two (possibly three)
broken projectiles, and two notched blanks. The obsidian is thus more
intensively used and worked than the flint, but less techno-typologically
varied (with no denticulates, or awls), which might suggest a more
restricted functional role for obsidian tools. That said, the existence of
points and scrapers in both obsidian and flint might argue against raw
material specific uses; only a use-wear analysis could clarify these issues.

In this study, we chemically characterised 34 of the 38 Sha'ar

50 kV in 1 kV increments (current range, 0–1.98 mA in 0.02 mA increments), and an Edwards RV8 vacuum pump for the analysis of elements below titanium (Ti). Data is acquired with a pulse processor and
analogue to digital converter.
The analytical protocols and methods follow those devised by M.S.
Shackley (2005, appendix; Poupeau et al., 2010: 2711). In the study, we
ran the artefacts under two analytical conditions. The pieces are first
run under a Mid Zb analysis condition with the X-ray tube operated at
30 kV using a 0.05 mm (medium) Pd filter in an air path for 200 s livetime to generate X-ray intensity Kα-line data for elements titanium
(Ti), manganese (Mn) and iron (Fe), zinc (Zn), gallium (Ga), rubidium
(Rb), strontium (Sr), yttrium (Y), zirconium (Zr), niobium (Nb), lead
(Pb) and thorium (Th). The second is a High Zb analysis condition with
the X-ray tube operated at 50 kV using a 0.63 mm (thick) Cu filter in an
air path to detect the element barium (Ba).
Trace element intensities were converted to concentration estimates
by employing a least-squares calibration line ratioed to the Compton
scatter established for each element from the analysis of international
rock standards. These comprise AGV-2 (andesite), BCR-2 (basalt),
BHVO-2 (hawaiite), BIR-1a (basalt), GSP-2 (granodiorite), QLO-1
(quartz latite), RGM-2 (rhyolite), SDC-1 (mica schist), STM-2 (syenite),
TLM-1 (tonalite), and W-2a (diabase) from the US Geological Service
[USGS], plus JR-1 and JR-2 (both obsidian) from the Geological Survey
of Japan. The USGS standard RGM-2 is analysed during each sample
run to check machine calibration and accuracy, with a maximum of 19
artefacts, plus standard per analysis. The data is then translated directly
into Excel for Windows software for manipulation, and interrogation.
Once in Excel form, data is normalised to the standard reference
sample, RGM-2, before its interrogation and plotting. This process
consists of determining the relative error in the standard measurement
and applying this difference to the individual artefacts analysed for
each tray processed by the instrument. This procedure ensures source
and artefact data results are consistently based on the reference, thus
providing a more accurate match between chemical fingerprints.
6. Results: raw material sources
The elemental concentrations of the 34 artefacts after normalisation
to the RGM-2 standard are presented in Table 1. Source assignment was
achieved through comparing the chemical signatures of the artefact
with those of source samples run by the lab under the same analytical
conditions. In choosing which geological products to compare with the
Sha'ar Hagolan data, we considered a number of factors. Firstly, our
work does not exist in isolation, with a series of southern Levantine
characterisation studies over the past 50 years detailing how through
time the region's populations have produced and/or procured tools
made of obsidian from central, eastern and north-eastern Anatolian
sources (Chataigner, 1998; Delerue, 2007). These include products of
the following volcanic complexes (Fig. 1): Acıgöl, Göllü Dağ and Nenezi
Dağ (Cappadocia), Bingöl, Gürgürbaba Tepe (Meydan Dağ), and
Nemrut Dağ (Lake Van region), plus Sarakımış (north-eastern Turkey).
We also included raw materials from the Armenian sources of Gutansar
and Syunik as these raw materials have recently been documented from
another Neolithic – Chalcolithic southern Levantine site by our lab.
In an initial bivariate plot of zirconium vs. strontium concentrations
the artefacts are clearly discriminated into three groups (Fig. 4). The
largest cluster (n = 17, 50%) of artefacts appear to match the elemental
values for geological samples from the southern Cappadocian source of
Nenezi Dağ. This assignation is confirmed by a close-up view of the
same data-plot, exclusively depicting this group of artefacts and the
Nenezi Dağ source data encircled with a 95% confidence ellipse (Fig. 5).
The next major grouping of artefacts (n = 15, 44%) cannot be
completely discriminated using the Zr vs. Sr graph, with the data points
broadly coinciding with source materials from Göllü Dağ Suphan Dağ,
and Syunik. Full discrimination can however be achieved via a multivariate plot of Zr vs. Rb/Zr vs. Nb (Fig. 6), which serves to illustrate that
these Sha'ar Hagolan artefacts were made of obsidian from the southern
Cappadocian source of Göllü Dağ, a provenance that can be further

7. Obsidian consumption at Sha'ar Hagolan
Most of the 17 artefacts of Nenezi Dağ obsidian relate to the manufacture of pressure flaked blades/bladelets from unipolar cores
(Table 2). Evidence for the on-site performance of this craft is attested
by two exhausted cores (SH14, SH4), one of which appears to have been
made on a thick-flake (Fig. 10.1, 5). There were also nine fragmentary
blades, including two with remnant cortex (SH11, SH13), three true
centre/prismatic blades (SH6, SH9, SH28), two core rejuvenation flakes
(one removing the upper face/platform [SH26]), two flakes, and a chip
(Table 2). There was also a slightly wider blade that may have derived
from a percussion knapped opposed platform core (SH23), a quite
distinct tradition (Fig. 10). Almost two-thirds of these blanks had
macroscopic use-wear (n = 11/65%); three of them had also been retouched, with a notched part-cortical blade (SH11), and two points. The
first projectile is made on the distal end of a pressure bladelet (SH10); it

(Aurenche et al., 2001; Hours et al., 1994), within which Sha'ar Hagolan represents a Period 5–6 site.

8.1. A diachronic social network analysis perspective
Our means of considering the consumption of obsidian at Sha'ar
Hagolan in a long-term context - and as a possible index of later
Neolithic socio-economic reconfigurations – is through the application
of a social network analysis (SNA). SNA is a methodology that highlights the structured relations amongst interconnected entities by rendering data comparable. This approach has been employed by archaeologists to examine inter-site relationships based on the dispersion of
certain forms of material culture as manifestations of socio-economic
links (see Freund and Batist, 2014; Golitko et al., 2012, Golitko and
Feinman, 2014; Knappett, 2011; Mills et al., 2013 inter alia).
In this case, the results of all obsidian sourcing data from Neolithic
Levantine sites situated between the Mediterranean coast and the
middle Euphrates were compiled and represented as nodes, which are
related by edges that represent values of similarity derived from their
quantitative comparison. More specifically, the proportions of raw
material that make up obsidian assemblages were compared using the
Brainerd-Robinson formula to obtain coefficients of similarity pertaining to each pair, which were then considered as edges in a network
(Peeples, 2011). The edges were filtered so that only strong ties with
coefficients of similarity equal to, or above, a minimum threshold value
of 50% were included in the analysis. This high minimum value and the
decision to account for variable degrees of similarity above it were
chosen to support the notion that the inhabitants of sites with similar
proportions of raw material variants were more likely to have been in
contact with one another.
Networks were constructed using obsidian sourcing data from sites
in the Levant and neighbouring regions spanning ASPRO periods 3 to 6,
to provide the Sha'ar Hagolan data with a long-term diachronic perspective (Fig. 12). A clustering algorithm was then used to detect
groups of nodes that are more connected to each other than they are to
others in the network (following Newman and Girvan, 2004). Members
of the same cluster are thus defined as communities who were in the
habit of consuming the same range of raw materials in similar proportions at broadly the same time, and by extent were likely participants in the same socio-economic interaction networks (trading partners, kin relations, etc.).
By focusing on raw material alone we appreciate that this analysis is
lacking nuance, however, this is a problem faced by anyone wishing to
interrogate the region's sourcing data, as so little of it is accompanied by
the artefacts techno-typological characteristics, and contextual information (Carter et al., 2006: 894–895). It is this more holistic notion
of obsidian characterisation data we would ideally like to work with, as
the specific modes by which raw materials were worked – consumption
traditions - would arguably provide a much more powerful insight into
community interaction (cf. Briois et al., 1997; Carter et al., 2013;
Orange et al., 2013).

phase. Here the data assigns most sites to membership of a rather
homogenous cluster (Fig. 12), with the communities of the Levant,
Middle Euphrates, and Cyprus primarily procuring obsidian from the
southern Cappadocian source of Göllü Dağ (Batist, 2014; Delerue,

suggesting – in part – a significant reconfiguration of deep-time procurement traditions, and supra-regional exchange relations (Fig. 12).
Continuing from the preceding periods, we view a cluster of Levantine
sites that primarily, if not exclusively procured obsidian from the
Cappadocian source of Göllü Dağ. In contrast, the communities of the
Middle Euphrates now employed not only south-eastern Anatolian
products – as they had for the preceding millennia – but also obsidian
from Cappadocia, supra-regional interactions that produce a second
network in our analysis. The isolation of nodes representing El-Khiam
and Munhata is due to two factors: (i) the fact that they were using
Acıgöl and Nenezi Dağ obsidian rather than Göllü Dağ, the principal
Cappadocian raw material used by their southern Levantine contemporaries, (ii) the small sample sizes involved, with one artefact
sourced from each site. The first appearance of obsidian from the
northern Cappadocian source of Acıgöl is noteworthy here, as the
network reconfiguration we witness in Period 5 is the product of not
only the emergence of new supra-regional exchange relations but also
the introduction of new raw materials into these ‘systems’. Finally,
Qdeir appears as a distinct node due to its obsidian assemblage being
comprised of a notably diverse array of raw materials from both Cappadocian and eastern Anatolian sources.
During Period 6, the clusters defined by our analyses are broadly
consistent with the interaction complexity of Period 5 (Fig. 12). Thus,
we continue to witness a Levantine reliance on Cappadocian products,
together with their occasional procurement of eastern Anatolian obsidian – as at Sha'ar Hagolan – while Middle Euphrates' populations still
accessed both central and eastern Anatolian products. At the same time,
we witness more raw materials being put into circulation. These include
the introduction of the so-called ‘Group 3d’ obsidian of unknown origin
(Renfrew et al., 1966: 33), and products of the Gürgürbaba Tepe (also
known as Meydan Dağ) source on the north coast of Lake Van (Fig. 1)
which were used by populations throughout Mesopotamia, and the
Levant. While the obsidian at Sha'ar Hagolan is still principally Cappadocian in origin, the appearance of eastern materials there and at
other sites in the southern Levant is indicative of these populations'
engagement within broader exchange systems, a range of connections
that does not seem to have been made by those living in the northern
Levant. It remains unclear as to whether the small amount of Nemrut
Dağ obsidian at Sha'ar Hagolan indicates the community's direct relations with eastern populations (via Transjordan) or a connection
mediated by other southern Levantine centres.
8.2. Obsidian consumption traditions in Period 6 (Pottery Neolithic)
From the long-term perspective, we now turn to a synchronic consideration of the Sha'ar Hagolan material. More specifically, were the
people of this community procuring and using East Göllü Dağ, Nenezi
Dağ, and Nemrut Dağ obsidian in a similar manner to that witnessed at
other Yarmukian/Period 6 sites?
Where such information is available, it appears that these raw materials were used primarily for the manufacture of pressure blades
throughout Anatolia, the Levant, and Mesopotamia, as indeed were
most Anatolian obsidian at this time (Chataigner, 1998). That said, it is
not always clear as to whether the knapping techniques employed by all
of these communities were directly comparable to those attested at

studies of the Hagoshrim and Nahal Zehora material are helpful
(Fig. 13) as they provide clear evidence for a common pressure tradition
being employed in the later PN of the southern Levant (Delerue et al.,
2012; Schechter et al., 2013). Both sites have produced pressure
blades/bladelets of East Göllü Dağ, and Nemrut Dağ obsidian that are
not only of a similar size to those from Sha'ar Hagolan, but also show
common technical traits, namely the delicate linear/downward flaking
on the proximal face, a feature seen on SH21 (Fig. 11) that relates to the
preparation of the core's lip/overhang removal (Delerue et al., 2012:
1101, Fig. 25.1; Schechter et al., 2013: Fig. 4, e-f, h).
That we can draw direct comparisons between the knapping traditions embodied within these obsidian assemblages is perhaps not surprising given their relative proximity (c. 60 km linear distance), and
ease of water-borne connection (Fig. 13). But do these commonalities
necessarily signify a ‘community of practice’, i.e. the sharing of technical know-how between these populations via close socio-economic
interactions, such as trading partnerships, and inter-marriage? Alternatively, given the larger relative, and absolute quantities of obsidian at
Hagoshrim (stratum V [Fig. 13, Table 3]), and the clearer evidence for
on-site manufacture (blade cores), we might instead be dealing with an
unequal power-dynamic, where the communities of Sha'ar Hagolan and
Nahal Zehora were dependent on their northern contemporaries for
access to ready-made obsidian tools (Delerue et al., 2012: 1108; Gopher
et al., 1998: 647; Schechter et al., 2013: 523, Table 2, Fig. 7). We return
to this hypothesis below.
Further to the north at Tell Aray 2 in the Amuq Valley (Fig. 13), we
again view East Göllü Dağ, Nenezi Dağ, and Nemrut Dağ obsidians
being used to make pressure blades/bladelets. Despite the fact the
community was much closer to the sources, they too were in the habit
of procuring finished end-products, rather than working the raw materials themselves (Maeda, 2003: 179–180). Continuing northwards
into central Anatolia, broad similarities can also be made between the
traditions of obsidian consumption at Sha'ar Hagolan, and the Late
Neolithic - Early Chalcolithic sites of Çatalhöyük East and West. These
include their equal use of East Göllü Dağ, and Nenezi Dağ raw materials
(Özdöl-Kutlu et al., 2015: 188–191); the dominance of pressure blade
technologies; and the occasional manufacture of small, tanged, and
trapezoidal projectiles (Ostaptchouk, 2016). The use of these southern
Cappadocian raw materials to make pressure blades/bladelets is also
documented at sites in southwestern Anatolia such as Hacılar, Höyücek,
and Kuraçay Höyük, though in Cappadocia itself the knapping
traditions are quite distinct, with flake-based, and percussion blade
tools dominant at Köşkhöyük and Tepecik-Çiftlik (Özdöl-Kutlu et al.,
2015: 189–191).
Turning to Upper Mesopotamia, and eastern wing of the Fertile
Crescent, the use of obsidian in Period 6 (including that from Nemrut
Dağ) has many similarities to what we see at Sha'ar Hagolan. At the
slightly earlier site of Tell Seker al-Aheimar (7100–6500 cal BCE), and
broadly contemporary Hakemi Use (6100–5950 cal BCE), obsidian was
imported in the form of preformed pressure bladelet cores and/or endproducts, the latter again having the distinctive linear removal of the
platform overhang (Kadowaki et al., 2013: 148–149, Fig. 12.2; Kartal,
2007: 99–104, Figs. 3–4). While similarities in knapping traditions are
apparent, the range of tool types is for the most part distinct from the
southern Levantine implements (e.g. Maeda, 2013: Figs. 23.2–23.3).
9. Conclusions
We return here to the questions that drove our research project,
commencing with ‘to what extent does the nature of obsidian consumption at Sha'ar Hagolan support the idea of a significant diminishment and/or reconfiguration of obsidian use by southern Levantine
populations in the PN/Yarmukian?’. While the relative proportion of
obsidian within PN/Yarmukian chipped stone assemblages does indeed
reflect a diminished circulation of this exotic material amongst (most)
southern Levantine populations (Garfinkel, 1993: 132), a long-term
perspective shows clearly that this was a gradual, not radical decrease
(Table 3, Fig. 13). During the PPNA – early PPNB (periods 2–3) the
average relative proportion of obsidian was 0.52% (based on data from
four sites), falling to 0.16% (two sites) by the middle - late PPNB
(periods 3–4/5), while at Sha'ar Hagolan in Period 6 it is 0.01%
(Table 3). In turn, there are broadly contemporary sites such as Hagoshrim, where we witness not only significantly more obsidian proportionally than at Sha'ar Hagolan, but also more than all southern
Levantine sites of any period from the earliest Neolithic to Chalcolithic
(Fig. 13).
As to the range of raw materials being procured by the inhabitants
of Sha'ar Hagolan as an index of socio-economic reconfiguration, our
SNA suggests that it is during the previous Period 5 that significant
changes in exchange networks and obsidian source use are witnessed
(Fig. 12). That said, these PN obsidian assemblages do provide further
evidence for the proclaimed regionalism that emerges at this time in the
southern Levant. The number of Period 6 clusters in our SNA reflect the
variety of raw materials in circulation, and the different suites of raw
materials these communities were procuring. Here again, we see a stark
difference between Sha'ar Hagolan with its two southern Cappadocian

Fig. 353 [though some of these may come from the Period 7 assemblage

Hagolan was dependent on Hagoshrim for access to all of their obsidian,
i.e. they provide a counter-point to our techno-typological considerations of the assemblage. If this better-connected community was responsible for supplying Sha'ar Hagolan – then the latter assemblage
should, in theory, represent a derivation of the former data-set; this is
patently not the case. Perhaps most notably, while 50% of the Sha'ar
Hagolan artefacts were made of Nenezi Dağ obsidian (n = 17), none of
the 28 pieces characterised from Hagoshrim originated from this
source. At Hagoshrim the range of raw materials, and the types of obsidian tools, including such distinctive items as corner–thinned blades
(from Periods 5 and 6), indicate clearly the connections to Upper Mesopotamian traditions at this time. This relationship becomes even
clearer by the late PN Wadi Raba phase (Period 7) where obsidian ornaments and vessels evidence relationships with the Halaf culture
(Schechter et al., 2013, 2016). In contrast, the Sha'ar Hagolan assemblage attests to far closer links with deeper-time Levantine networks
underpinned by the circulation of Cappadocian raw materials, and the
manufacture of such weapons as the Amuq point, a projectile known
throughout the Levant, and into central Anatolia (Gopher, 1994: 39,
209–210, Fig. 4.8). One would be unwise, however, to take these two
statements to infer completely exclusive socio-economic networks. Indeed, the technical commonalities of these southern Levantine populations' pressure bladelets clearly suggest common knapping traditions
throughout the region (and beyond), practices that would have come to
be shared through close inter-community interaction.
Finally, our data provides little to support the hypothesis that social
distinction at Sha'ar Hagolan was part-based on members of the community enjoying preferential access to non-local goods (Garfinkel et al.,
2012: 119). There is certainly no clear concentration of artefacts in any
one area, though some intra-site distinction in consumption can be
noted, namely the evidence for tool-production in Building I, and the
storing of weapons (projectiles) in Building II (Table 2).
In sum, our study has demonstrated the following. (1) while the PN
is known as a period of socio-economic disruption the modes of obsidian consumption at Sha'ar Hagolan reflect deep-time southern
Levantine traditions, i.e. a continuity of practice. (2) differences can be
viewed in the procurement and use of obsidian amongst southern
Levantine PN populations, with a wider array of raw materials attested
at sites such as Hagoshrim, data that support the more general notion of
intra- and supra-regional cultural segregation at this time (the ‘mosaic’
model), in contrast to the greater homogeneity of the preceding PPN
periods. (3) the data do not clearly support the thesis that social distinction at Sha'ar Hagolan was part-constituted via preferential access
to exotic goods, though some intra-community differences in consumption can be noted.
