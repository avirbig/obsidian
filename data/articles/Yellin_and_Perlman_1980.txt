### **The Provenience of Obsidian from Neolithic Sites in Israel***

A comprehensive program is now under way for determining the points of origin of obsidian artifacts and flakes found in Israel. The present paper is confined to material obtained from six Neolithic sites; obsidians dated to later periods will not be discussed here (see explanation below).

The chemical compositions of the obsidians were determined by neutron activation analysis (NAA), from which abundance values were obtained for more than twenty chemical elements. The system for carrying out this analysis is a modification of one described by Perlman and Asaro.1 The chemical profile serves as a 'fingerprint' which can be compared, and if possible matched, with profiles of obsidians drawn from different obsidian sources.

The region closest to Israel where obsidian flows are known to exist is Anatolia; the collection of source materials consequently began there.2 Anatolia has a considerable number of sources, and since not all of them have been sampled, one would expect to find compositions among the specimens of archaeological obsidians which cannot yet be matched with their sources. The principal conclusion reached in this paper, however, results from source-material analyses which have been carried out.

#### **THE NEOLITHIC SITES**

Fig. 1 shows the archaeological sites from which the obsidians came. Table 1 presents a summary of the numbers of pieces analysed, the archaeological period to which the materials are attributed and the names of the archaeologists who furnished the specimens. Since the dating of these materials is of importance with regard to trade patterns in different periods, mention will be made here of possible ambiguities connected with some of the materials.

The site of Beisamoun lay under the western edge of Lake Huleh until 1950, when the lake was almost completely drained in order to reclaim land for agricultural purposes. As part of these operations, dikes were constructed around part of the exposed surface to create ponds for fish breeding. In the following years, some of these ponds were temporarily drained, again exposing the surface. Though the land was greatly disturbed by these projects, they permitted surface collections of lithic materials from the ancient site3 and brought to light architectural remains. Short seasons of archaeological excavations were also conducted. The details behind this brief résumé and a summary of the finds at Beisamoun have recently been published.4

The excavations and the examination of the surface finds revealed that habitation in this area was predominantly Pre-pottery Neolithic B, dated to the mid-seventh millennium. While nothing earlier came to light, there were also surface finds belonging to the Pottery Neolithic and Chalcolithic periods and the Early Bronze Age. We wish to stress that although one may quite reasonably assign the obsidians as a whole to a PN context, some of the surface obsidians might belong to a later period.

An explanation is also necessary for the obsidians from Tel Mevorakh. This mound is not a Neolithic site, but stone implements and a few pieces of obsidian were found in a cut through the Middle Bronze rampart.5 The material in the upper part of the rampart, where the lithic materials appeared, had been brought from outside the immediate area. A subsequent examination of the stone tools showed them to belong to the Pottery Neolithic period.6 Unless evidence to the contrary turns up, it may be assumed that the obsidians are similarly dated.

#### **RESULTS AND DISCUSSION**

Table 2 presents a partial list of data for obsidian groups from two archaeological sites in Israel and from two obsidian sources in south-central Anatolia. The four Pre-pottery Neolithic sites (Table 1) include Beisamoun in northern Israel and Nahal Lavan in the Negev. The latter is the southernmost site at which obsidian has been found up to the present. The obsidian flow at Güllü Dag is located at the village of Kömürcü, about 40 km. west-of-south from Nevşehir. The second flow, termed Nenezi Dag, lies just 14 km. north-west of Güllü Dag, close to the village of Bekarlar. In this table, only 12 chemical elements are listed out of the much larger number that were measured. Those omitted only reinforce the conclusions that were drawn.

The numbers shown for the respective elements under Beisamoun are the mean values (M) for a group of 24 obsidians and the root-mean-square-deviation (σ) for the group. Each value of σ is an index for the spread encountered among the 24 individual abundances for which M is the mean value. The terms in the other columns have the same meaning, though it should be kept in mind that the last two columns refer to random collected obsidian specimens from the respective sources.

An examination of the data, element by element, shows that there is a very close correspondence between the groups from Beisamoun and Nahal Lavan and that both match the source group from Güllü Dag. The data for the Nenezi Dag source are presented in order to highlight the contrast in the compositions of two sources that happen to be only 14 km. apart. It should be noted that all other sources that have been examined are equally well, or even more easily, differentiated.

In order to acquire some perspective on the ease of distinguishing between the sources, one should note the values of iron, given as 0.607 ± 0.017 and 0.884 ± 0.010. Superficially, the mean values 0.607 and 0.884 do not appear to vary greatly, but the difference between them (0.277) must be compared with the spreads in the two groups. Thus, the statistical odds that a member of one group can be distinguished from the other, on the basis of this one element alone, are astronomical.

As shown in Table 1, 27 pieces of obsidian from Beisamoun were analysed, but the results for only 24 of them appear in Table 2. The three that were omitted will be discussed below, but we will first briefly describe the results from the other sites listed in Table I and from the Chalcolithic sites that are not included in this report.

In addition to the 24 Beisamoun pieces (including the six excavated by Lechevallier), all the specimens from the PPN sites, Nahal Lavan, Mikhmoret and Netiv ha-Gedud, also came from Güllü Dag. It is hardly possible that only Güllü Dag contained an abundant supply of obsidian of'export quality' because obsidians from the Chalcolithic sites present quite a different picture. At these sites, 12 different compositions of obsidian were revealed, including material from Güllü Dag. The settlements at Hazorea alone, for example, yielded obsidians of seven different compositions.

From the above, it is evident that if we exclude the three pieces from Beisamoun, a clear break can be observed between the Pre-pottery Neolithic sites and the others. Whereas the PPN materials seem to have derived from a single source, Güllü Dag, of the three Beisamoun pieces, one came from neighbouring Nenezi Dag, one from the Lake Van region, and the third from an unknown source. As was already noted, there is also a possibility that material of post-PPN periods occurs at Beisamoun, and it is therefore not unlikely that these three pieces are not of PPN date.

Two groups of obsidian in Table 1 came from Pottery Neolithic contexts: three samples from Tel Mevorakh and three from Giv'at ha-Parsa. At Tel Mevorakh, one piece came from Nenezi Dag and the other two from the Lake Van region. At Giv'at ha-Parsa, all three came from Güllü Dag. This finding is not at variance with the above mentioned 'break', because Güllü Dag continued to be a source of obsidian into the Chalcolithic period.

Although plausible arguments have been presented supporting the theory that there was a single obsidian source during the PPN periods, this has by no means been proved. We have no direct evidence that the Beisamoun specimens not from Güllü Dag are later than PPNB, and it may well turn out that well-attested PPN materials not yet analysed will have proveniences different from Güllü Dag.

Finally, an explanation is in order as to why only a brief mention is made of the results from Chalcolithic sites and no data are given. The analyses of materials from these sites revealed the existence of 12 different compositions which almost certainly indicate the same number of different sources. Since up to now we have succeeded in obtaining source materials to identify conclusively only six of these, it was not considered worthwhile to present results based on material whose origins cannot be pinpointed. Of the finds covered in the present report, only one piece (from Beisamoun) comes from an unknown source.

Despite the uncertainty in asserting that there was a single obsidian source during the PPN periods, it is still rather puzzling that for over 1500 years, so much of the obsidian did originate from the very same source. It should furthermore be noted that other sources of obsidian are located at no great distance from Güllü Dag. Perhaps we shall obtain a clue to this phenomenon when analyses are made on obsidian of the same period excavated from archaeological sites closer to the sources; that is, on PPN material from Anatolia. Unfortunately, existing analytical work relevant to this subject has been carried out by methods that are inadequate for making fine distinctions, and we have too few specimens of source material and archaeological samples to reveal a comprehensive picture.15

Turning now to the question of the nature of the obsidian 'trade'16, as reflected in the sites under discussion, there is no evidence indicating that obsidian tools were fashioned at these PPN sites in Israel. No cores have been found, and no reports have been received of the discovery of the copious minute chips that provide a tell-tale sign of obsidian working. Larger flakes found in surface collections or by coarse sieving perhaps arose from the breakage of implements. Until evidence to the contrary appears, we must therefore not consider the obsidian trade in this period as a brisk traffic in the raw material but rather as a modest trade involving the finished tools.17

How modest this traffic was is difficult to assess. The 62 pieces that we studied must constitute only a very small fraction of the imports. Many times this number have already come to light, and this must be multiplied by an unknown factor representing those not yet uncovered in the habitation sites, those from sites not yet discovered, and those which were dispersed in the surrounding countryside where they will never be encountered except by chance. That this trade covered a long time span cannot be disputed. However, much more extensive and meticulous stratigraphie work must be carried out before we can establish whether this trade was intermittent or sustained.
