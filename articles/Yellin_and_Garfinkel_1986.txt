FROM A PRE-POTTERY
NEOLITHIC В SITE
AT YIFTAHEL, ISRAEL
J. YELL1N and Y. GARFINKEL
ABSTRACT. - The PPNB site of Yiftahel in Lower Galilee, has yielded 8 pieces of obsidian. These obsidians were analysed by
instrumental neutron activation analysis. Their provenience seems to be the Göllu Dag area attesting cultural contacts between the
Lower Galilee and central Anatolia.
RÉSUMÉ. - Le site PPNB de Yiftahel en Basse Galilée a livré 8 pièces en obsidienne. Ces obsidiennes furent analysées par activation
neutronique. Leur provenance semble être le Göllu Dag attestant des contacts culturels entre la Basse-Galilée et VAnatolie Centrale.
INTRODUCTION
The site of Yiftahel in Lower Galilee, Israel map
réf. 172/240, was excavated in a large salvage project
carried out by the Israel Department of Antiquities
and Museum in the years 1982-1985. The remains
uncovered in this project are mainly from the Early
Bronze I and the Neolithic periods (1). The Pre-
Pottery Neolithic В (PPNB) settlement of Yiftahel
falls into the category of large Neolithic villages
unearthed in the Mediterranean areas of the Levant.
While C-14 dates are not yet available, the site is
dated on the archaeological evidence to the 7th
Millennium B.C. Well known sites from this period
in the Levant are : Jericho, Ain Ghazal, Munhata,
Tell Ramad, Abou-Gosh, Beisamoun and others (2).
The Neolithic villages of the Mediterranean
Levant in the 7th millennium B.C., and Yiftahel
among them, have a high degree of unity in the
material culture : rectangular architecture, plaster
floors, and a similar technology and typology of flint
knapping. From the economic point of view, how-
ever, the site of Yiftahel is outstanding among the
Neolithic villages. The economic basis of its inhabi-
tants, as reflected by the botanic and faunal remains,
was limited and focused on the cultivation of two
kinds of legumes and on gazelle hunting. From over
1 000 identifiable animal bones, gazelles constituted
ca. 75 percent (3). In the other PPNB sites mention-
ed above, sheep and goats are dominant, and their
presence has been explained as resulting from
domestication (4). In Yiftahel, we do not have
animal husbandry. The seeds sample consists of two
kinds of legumes : Lentil ( Lens culinaris Medik), and
Horsebean (Vicia Faba). These Horsebean "push
back the known use of this vetch by about
2 000 years" (5). Cereals which are very common in
Neolithic sites were not found in our site.
Only a small portion of the entire range of
activities connected with cultivation and husbandry
prevalent in Neolithic sites was practiced at Yiftahel.
It is therefore particularly important to understand
the character and strength of the relationship bet-
ween the Neolithic community of Yiftahel and the
neighboring Neolithic sites. Was this community
isolated and cut-off, or did it keep up inter-relations
with other Neolithic villages ?
How can these interrelations be investigated ?
Possibly, the only criterion for these are exotic
objects delivered to the inhabitants from great
distances and reflect exchange systems. In the PPNB
period, such objects were obsidian, green stones, and
seashells. For this reason the study of the obsidian
sources and its distribution in the various sites (6) is
of outstanding importance.

III. - OBSIDIAN ANALYSIS
The Yiftahel obsidians were analysed by instru-
mental neutron activation analysis. The system of
analysis employed is based on that reported by
Perlman and Asaro (8) with suitable modifications
as necessitated by differences in instrumentation and
some operational procedures. The Standard Pottery
of Perlman and Asaro was employed. Some details
of the system of analysis was reported elsewhere (9).
Previously, obsidian analysis from the following
PPN sites have been reported: Beisamoun (10),
Nahal Lavan; Netiv ha-Gdud, Mikhmoret, Givat
ha- Parsa and Tel Mevorahk (1 1). The location of
these sites is shown in the map (fig. 1). In table 1 we
give the analysis of seven obsidians. One obsidian

analysis. We give only the rare earth elements we

Nenezi Dag (NNZD), and Zarneki Tepe (ZNKT).
These compositions were previously reported (12).

In a previous report on the sources of obsidian
form neolithic sites in Israel (15), it was noted that
the majority of obsidians originated in Göllu Dag.
Three exceptions from Beisamun were noted with
one piece coming from Nenezi Dag, 15 km north-
west of Göllu Dag, one piece coming from the Lake
Van region in Eastern Turkey, and one piece from an
unknown source. At Tel Mevorak too, one piece was
traced to Nenezi Dag and two pieces to the Nemrut
Dag region in Eastern Turkey. That the preponde-
rance of material came from Göllu Dag is puzzling,
since other sources of obsidian exist in close proxi-
mity of Göllu Dag, e.g. Nenezi Dag, and their
obsidian does not appear to be of inferior quality. At
Yiftahel we have encountered yet another unknown
source of neolithic obsidian although this source
may yet turn out to be Göllu Dag.

IV. _ CULTURAL CONTACT
At Yiftahel, as in other neolithic sites in Israel,
there is no evidence of an obsidian industry. No
obsidian cores have been found nor have copious
quantities of minute chips turned up as would be
expected if obsidian had been worked at the site.

number have now come to light it is still too small
to suggest any brisk trade even allowing for sites that
have not been excavated or discovered. The occur-
rence of obsidian is minuscule, just a few pieces per
site. How obsidian got to Yiftahel, whether by some
modest trade, migration of people or some exchange
network, is a matter of speculation. In itself, the
discovery of obsidian provides direct evidence that
Yiftahel was not an isolated habitation. Analysis of
the obsidian shows the source to be central Anatolia
and possibly Göllu Dag to which the preponderance
of neolithic obsidian artifact from other neolithic
sites has been traced. In the larger scheme, Yiftahel
provides us with yet another point in the distribution
of obsidian in neolithic times. Much meticulous
work needs to be done to clarify the geographic
distribution of archaeological obsidian, before
conclusions can be drawn about the manner in
which the obsidian arrived and whether the move-
ment of obsidian was sporadic or sustained over
prolonged periods. On the one hand it is clear that
more extensive chemical mapping of the sources is
needed and that obsidian from many sites needs to
be explored. On the other hand stratigraphie work is
needed to establish the time periods at which the
obsidian arrived.
