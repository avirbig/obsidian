This paper details the use of obsidian sourcing to reconstruct networks of interaction (or ‘communities of
practice’) amongst populations of south-eastern Anatolia and the Near East in the context of ‘Neolithisation’ during the late 11theearly 10th millennia BC. EDXRF was used to elementally characterise 120
artefacts of Epi-Palaeolithic e Pre-Pottery Neolithic A date from Körtik Tepe in south-eastern Anatolia.
Four eastern Anatolian sources are represented, mainly Bingöl A/B and Nemrut Dag, plus the first
evidence for the use of Mus ̧ obsidian. When the source data is integrated with the artefacts’ technotypological attributes it is possible to locate the assemblage within an Upper Tigris tradition (with
some interesting local differences), which stands in stark contrast to contemporary practices in northern
Mesopotamia and the Levant. These local and regional distinctions support recent views of the Neolithic
being much more heterogeneous, with a ‘mosaic’ of community-specific/local traditions of subsistence
practices, raw material choices and lithic technologies during the Younger DryaseEarly Holocene.

1. Introduction
Situated on the upper reaches of the Tigris River in southeastern
Turkey, Körtik Tepe is one of the first sedentary settlements in the
Anatolian part of the Fertile Crescent (Fig. 1). Excavations have
revealed a deeply stratified settlement spanning the later 11th to
early 10th millennium BC, with six occupation strata of Pre-Pottery
Neolithic A [PPNA] date above a series of Epi-Palaeolithic deposits
(Özkaya, 2009; Özkaya and Cos ̧ kun, 2009, 2011). Work at the site is
thus contributing to our knowledge of the region in the Younger
DryaseEarly Holocene, and represents a focus of attention for those
seeking to understand the various processes we associate with the
origins of ‘the Neolithic’, however one interprets that term (cf.
Binford, 1968; Cauvin, 2000; Belfer-Cohen and Goring-Morris, 2011).
This paper details the elemental characterisation of 120 obsidian
artefacts from Körtik Tepe, a study that was undertaken with
a number of questions in mind. Aside from the relatively
straightforward issues of sources represented and the means by
which these raw materials were procured and consumed through
time, our project forms part of a broader project employing
obsidian characterisation as a means of defining local and supraregional cultural practices in the context of ‘Neolithisation’, i.e.
the coming-into-being of early agricultural lifeways. With recent
faunal and archaeobotanical studies indicating “a mosaic of
exploitation strategies” amongst the earliest Neolithic communities of the Fertile Crescent (Arbuckle and Özkaya, 2006: 114; see
also Asouti, 2010), we wished to see if obsidian procurement and
technical choices also reflected such local contingencies, or
conversely provided evidence for common modes of consumption
across large areas. If the latter were true it could be argued that
gifting and exchange represented foundational social processes of
the Neolithic, practices that both connected people and provided
the networks through which new ideas and practices e such as
agriculture e could have circulated.

(Özkaya, 2009; Özkaya and Cos ̧ kun, 2009, 2011 inter alia).
As of 2011, 3425 m 2 (28%) of the site’s surface had been
exposed (Fig. 2). At the top of the mound is a small amount of
medieval activity, followed by a deep prehistoric sequence. The
integration of stratigraphic analysis, 14 C dates and the nature of
the finds has allowed at least six PPNA sub-phases to be distinguished within the 10th millennium BC, while the Epi-Palaeolithic
deposits push the occupation back into the late 11th millennium
BC (11,700e11,250 cal. B.P. [Benz et al., 2011, Benz et al., in press]).
These earliest deposits, thus far located in only trenches A80 and
A84 (Fig. 2), have yet to be fully exposed due to ground water
levels. The site is thus one of the earliest sedentary communities
in the Anatolian part of the Fertile Crescent, whose Younger
DryaseEarly Holocene occupation sequence offers an important
insight to the various processes that preceded full plant and
animal domestication and by extent the first farming economies
of the Near East.
Excavations have revealed 112 structures with stone foundations, whose round and oval plans are typical for the period in
southeastern Anatolia and the Levant, plus 73 small circular
constructions with pebble floors interpreted as food storage units
(Özkaya and Cos ̧ kun, 2011: 91, Fig. 7). Körtik Tepe also has by far the
richest funerary record of any PPNA site, with 723 inhumations,
many displaying post-mortem treatment such as plastering the
body or painting the skeleton, plus hundreds of bead necklaces and
stone vessels (some with human and animal engraved decorations),
plus numerous bone and stone tools (Özkaya, 2009).
While described as a primarily Neolithic site, evidence suggests
that the community relied largely, if not exclusively on wild
resources. Preliminary analysis of the faunal assemblage suggests
that the PPNA inhabitants hunted sheep, deer, gazelle, pig, cattle,
bird, and fished in the nearby river. The dominant species was wild
sheep and red deer, with the intensive exploitation of juveniles
considered to represent the beginnings of herd management, pre-
figuring true domestication (Arbuckle and Özkaya, 2006; Özkaya
and Cos ̧ kun, 2011: 101). These data have been juxtaposed with
those from nearby Hallan Çemi, where pigs and cattle dominated,
suggesting that subsistence practices were localised and variable
(Arbuckle and Özkaya, 2006: 114). Initial archaeobotanical studies
attest the exploitation of a broad spectrum of wild plants, including
a significant quantity of large-seeded grasses, plus a smaller
amount of wild progenitor species of barley and wheat, pistacia and
legumes (Riehl et al., 2011).
Körtik Tepe’s architecture, artefacts, iconography and burial
practices find parallels both locally at sites along the Batman River
and Upper Tigris, and within the Fertile Crescent more generally,
from Beidha and Jericho in the southern Levant to Qermez Dere
and Nemrik in Mesopotamia (Özkaya, 2009: 4 [Fig. 1]). This project
aimed to interrogate the nature of these local and supraregional connections through a comparative analysis of obsidian
consumption.
3. The Körtik Tepe obsidian study
While the chipped stone is dominated by local chert, it also
contains a significant quantity of obsidian, an exotic raw material
whose closest sources lay 140 km to the northeast (Fig. 1). The
assemblage comprises mainly unipolar blade and microblade
products, with large numbers of retouched tools, including
geometric microliths, arch-backed blades and thumbnail scrapers.
While projectiles are common they are Epi-Palaeolithic in tradition,
the assemblage lacking the type of points associated with classic
PPNA assemblages of the Levant and Zagros (Özkaya, 2009: 6;
Özkaya et al., 2009: 90e95). Kartal’s ongoing study of this material
leads him to conclude that the Körtik Tepe obsidian has most in
common with local Epi-Palaeolithic/PPNA traditions (Özkaya and
Cos ̧ kun, 2011: 95), not least the material from Hallan Çemi
further up the Batman River (Rosenberg, 1994, 2011a). General

Shanidar, Nemrik, and other late Zarzian sites further to the east in
the Zagros Mountains (Kozlowski, 1996).
Cores and knapping debris indicate that some obsidian was
worked on-site, with microblade production attested throughout
the settlement. Conversely, the larger blade tradition is represented
mainly by end-products (Özkaya et al., 2009: 91), suggesting that
these implements were procured ready-made from another
community, or a quarry-based workshop.
While previous reports noted that some of the obsidian was
‘transparent and greenish’, and thus almost certainly of eastern
Anatolian origin (being the only region where peralkaline volcanics
are found), precise source assignment was impossible without
chemical analysis (Özkaya et al., 2009: 91). This project aimed to
resolve this issue, as well as investigating: (1) raw material choices
through time; (2) potential differences in intra-site consumption;
(3) the relation of the Körtik Tepe results to other pertinent datasets as a means of investigating community interaction in the
context of Neolithisation.
4. Sampling
To answer the above questions Kartal and Cos ̧ kun sampled
artefacts from various trenches and strata, the material reflecting
the perceived range of raw materials, as defined through variance
in colour, translucency, inclusions, banding, texture, and cortex.
While 120 artefacts admittedly represent only a tiny proportion of
a large assemblage (>10,000 pieces), a recent study by Carter and
Grant confirmed that these artefacts’ techno-typological characteristics were representative of the whole, i.e. dominance of bladelets (and retouched versions thereof), with a minor blade
component. Obsidian itself comprises c. 15e20% of the total chipped stone assemblage at any one time.
With the selection process biased towards non museum-quality
finds, most of the artefacts comprise relatively undiagnostic flake
debris (Table 1). The bulk of this material came from PPNA strata,
with only two pieces from Epi-Palaeolithic levels at the base of
Trench A80 (Table 1). Amongst the PPNA artefacts it has only been
possible to assign specific levels to those pieces from trenches A80
and A84 which have received the most in-depth stratigraphic
analyses (Benz et al., 2011, in press).
5. Analytical procedures
The 120 artefacts were analysed whole and non-destructively at
the McMaster Archaeological XRF Laboratory [MAX Lab] by
a Thermo Scientific ARL Quant’X energy-dispersive x-ray fluorescence spectrometer [EDXRF], each piece having first been cleaned
in an ultrasonic tank with distilled water for ten minutes. The
analytical protocols and methods follow those devised by Shackley
(2005, appendix; Poupeau et al., 2010: 2711).
The spectrometer is equipped with a ultra-high flux peltier air
cooled Rh x-ray target with a 125 micron beryllium (Be) window,
an x-ray generator that operates from 4 to 50 kV/0.02 to 1.0 mA at
0.02 increments, and a 2001 min-1 Edwards vacuum pump for the
analysis of elements below titanium (Ti). Data is acquired with
a pulse processor and analog to digital converter. The major

7. Obsidian consumption at Körtik Tepe
If obsidian sourcing is to be used to elucidate common or
distinct traditions of consumption amongst PPNA populations, then
we need to move beyond the issue of raw materials alone. The data
need to be considered more holistically through a chaîne opératoire
analytical framework, i.e. to reconstruct the choices these people
made with regard to which obsidian they selected, the form(s) in
which these materials circulated, the modes by which they were
then worked, the form and scale of the implements they produced
and then to consider these relations through time and space (cf.
Briois et al., 1997; Carter et al., 2006).
The 15 Nemrut Dag artefacts, 12.5% of the analysed material,
represent most of a microblade reduction sequence, with two
exhausted cores (Fig. 6: 1), a rejuvenation flake (Fig. 6: 2), and an
end-product. The presence of cortical debris is significant as it
suggests that at least some of this obsidian was procured as true
unmodified raw material.
The 36 artefacts of Bingöl A obsidian, 30% of the analysed
material, similarly evidence on-site microblade manufacture (Fig. 6:
3e9), with two exhausted nuclei, end-products (Fig. 6: 3e4), plus
rejuvenation pieces from the core’s platform (Fig. 6: 5) and
face (Fig. 6: 5). The few modified pieces include a borer on a blade
(Fig. 6: 7), an arched backed piece (Fig. 6: 8), plus pieces with simple
linear retouch (Fig. 6: 9). This material also included debris
with cortex (Fig. 6: 6), suggesting the procurement of unworked
nodules.
In contrast, the Bingöl B (n ¼ 51, 43%) assemblage lacked cortical
blanks, inferring that this obsidian arrived as decorticated nodules
and/or preformed cores. These subtly distinct modes of circulation
are notable, as it is often assumed that the calc-alkaline and peralkaline products of the Bingöl massif were exploited in the same
manner due to their proximity to each other (cf. Khalidi et al., 2009:
883). Once on site, the Bingöl B obsidian was knapped in much the
same manner as that from Bingöl A, i.e. it was used to make unipolar bladelets using a percussive technique. There are cores (Fig. 6:
10), chunks, non-cortical flakes, and a series of rejuvenation pieces,
as well as end-products (Fig. 6: 11e20). Modified blanks include
another arched and backed piece (Fig. 6: 18), backed and notched
bladelets, two small borers (Fig. 6: 15), plus a few bladelets and
blade-like rejuvenation pieces with normal and inverse linear
retouch (Fig. 6: 12e18).
The one piece of obsidian assigned to the Mus ̧ source is a distal
section of a part-cortical bladelet, analogous in size and regularity
to the Bingöl and Nemrut Dag products (Fig. 6: 21).

8. The Körtik Tepe data in context
The past 50 years of Anatolian and Near Eastern characterisation
studies provide us with a rich evidential context within which to
locate the Körtik Tepe data (Cauvin and Chataigner, 1998;
Chataigner, 1998; Delerue, 2007). When one considers the EpiPalaeolithic and PPNA data en masse a few clear patterns emerge.
Firstly, despite the fact that there were numerous sources available
to these communities throughout Anatolia and Transcaucasia, only
five of them seem to have been exploited during these early
periods, namely Göllü Dag, and Nenezi Dag (Cappadocia), plus
Bingöl A, Bingöl B, and Nemrut Dag (Figs. 9 and 10). To this list we
can now add Mus ̧ as a sixth source. Secondly, there are clear spatial
trends in these raw materials’ consumption. Cappadocian obsidian
was procured by communities in central and Mediterranean Anatolia, northern Mesopotamia and the Levant as far south as the
Negev, while obsidian from the eastern Anatolian were accessed by
populations in eastern Anatolia and Mesopotamia (Carter et al.,
2011; Chataigner, 1998; Delerue, 2007). Only in northern Mesopotamia did communities use both central and eastern Anatolian
products, at sites such as Göbekli Tepe (T. Carter pers. obs.), Abu
Hureyra, Cheikh Hassan and Mureybet (Abbès et al., 2001; Cauvin,
1991; Moore, 2000: 182; Pernicka et al., 1997), plus Tell Aswad in
the Djezireh (Epstein 1977).
So what do these patterns indicate? It could be argued that
distance was the primary factor that influenced these raw materials’ distribution, i.e. those closest to Cappadocia used Göllü Dag
and Nenezi Dag products, while those closest to Lake Van exploited
Bingöl and Nemrut Dag obsidian, while those in between the two
volcanic regions were able to access both sets of raw materials. If
we focus on the 11the10th millennia BC data alone then the
‘distance-from-source’ argument seems quite reasonable. However
if one takes a longer-term perspective, then we eventually see these
pattern’s disruption. For instance, in the 7th millennium BC Bingöl
and Nemrut Dag products appear in central Anatolia (Çatalhöyük
[Carter et al., 2008]), while conversely Cappadocian obsidian now
rarely if ever made its way to SE Anatolia (e.g. Gürcütepe, Urfa
region [T. Carter, pers. obs.]). Given that there were no advances in
transportation in the intervening period, we believe these shifting
patterns of circulation reflect changes in peoples’ traditions. This for
us is the crux of the matter, i.e. that cultural choice represents the
basis for the circulation and distribution of these raw materials
during the Epi-Palaeolithic and PPNA (indeed any period). What
significance do we then read into the common practices that we can
map through space and time? What was the nature of social
interaction that led to the creation and maintenance of these
traditions and what was the basis of the boundaries that distinguished different realms of cultural practice, i.e. those communities
exclusively choosing Cappadocian obsidian, versus those whose
habit was to consume Van region products?

prove that ‘the exclusive consumption of Lake Van obsidian’ is

Situated 215 km to the south-west of Körtik Tepe is the PPNA e
early PPNB site of Göbekli Tepe on the northern edge of the Harran
Plain (Fig. 10); here Bingöl A/B and Nemrut Dag products comprise
just under half of the small obsidian assemblage (>99% of the
chipped stone is flint [Schmidt, 2000: 51]). The consumption of
these eastern products is quite distinct to that witnessed at Körtik
Tepe, the artefacts almost exclusively comprising debitage from
pressure-flaked blade technologies (T. Carter pers. obs.). Indeed,
this mode of working Van region obsidian seems to be the norm for
communities south of Körtik Tepe, as for example evidenced by
archaeometric/technical analyses of PPNA assemblages from
Cheikh Hassan and Mureybet in the Middle Euphrates, plus Tell
Aswad in the Djezireh (Abbès et al., 2001; Abbès and Sánchez
Priego, 2008: 234; Epstein 1977: Table 3, Figs. 5 and 7).
While products of the bladelet tradition we associate with
Körtik Tepe are occasionally reported from these northern
Mesopotamian/Middle Euphrates sites, they only form a minority
component of their assemblages. Moreover, it is not always clear as
to which raw materials they were made of, i.e. they might be
Cappadocian, rather than eastern Anatolian. Only from Göbekli
Tepe do we have detailed data, and here only 1% of the eastern
(peralkaline) raw materials were in the form of bladelets (T. Carter
pers. obs.).
In sum, it is apparent that by focusing on raw material alone
there is a risk of masking major differences in how these EpiPalaeolithic and PPNA communities consumed Van region
obsidian. The populations of the Batman River system worked
Bingöl and Nemrut Dag obsidian on-site to produce microblades,
whereas the same raw materials were used by contemporary
communities further to the south-west to make pressure-flaked
blades, a radically different technology. The boundary between
these two traditions is not entirely clear, though given that
pressure-flaked blade production is reported as absent from PPNA
Çayönü (Binder, 2007: 240), it is tempting to set up an opposition
between Upper Tigris region cultural-technical traditions and those
practiced by populations of the Urfa area and Upper/Middle
Euphrates (Figs. 9 and 10). One might thus interpret the larger
Kortik Tepe blades e for which we lack associated manufacturing
debris e as imports from southern communities. Indeed, while one
might talk of a cultural boundary between Epi-Palaeolithic and
PPNA communities of the northern Levant and Upper Tigris, it is
clear that these people were in contact with each other and did
engage in certain shared traditions, as evidenced in domestic
architecture, burial, and iconography (cf. Kozlowski and Aurenche,
2005).

Bingöl and Nemrut Dag obsidian one is tempted to argue that the
technical divide was structured by the fundamental distinctions in
these communities’ subsistence practices and by extent the kinds of
tools they needed. Bar-Yosef (1998: Fig. 10) has characterised these

“sedentary foragers”, in contrast to their contemporaries in the

we view in tool types and technology, we ultimately prefer to
interpret the data through a more cultural lens (analogous to that
outlined by Belfer-Cohen and Goring-Morris [2010: 10]), one that
views subsistence as part-constituting a way of being-in-the-world,
rather than driving human practice.
9. Conclusions
Our analysis has shown that in the late 11theearly 10th
millennium BC the people of Körtik Tepe primarily procured
obsidian from various outcrops on the Bingöl and Nemrut Dag
massifs. The data further suggest a subtle distinction in the manner
in which these resources circulated, with Bingöl A and Nemrut Dag
peralkaline obsidian brought to the site in the form of cortical
nodules, while Bingöl B (calc-alkaline) materials arrived as partworked cobbles and/or preformed cores. From here onward these
raw materials were worked in a similar manner, used to make small
percussion bladelets, some being modified into geometric tools.
There was also one part-cortical bladelet whose raw material was
sourced to the Mus ̧ region, the first evidence for this obsidian’s use;
its rarity suggests a sporadic exploitation en route to the major
sources of Bingöl and Nemrut Dag (Fig. 1).
We tentatively suggest chronological patterning in raw material
consumption, with Bingöl products dominant in the EpiPalaeolithic strata, while Nemrut Dag obsidian then appears in
PPNA horizons. Technologically and typologically the Körtik Tepe
obsidian assemblage fits within a local Batman River/Upper Tigris
Epi-Palaeolithic e PPNA tradition. Closely comparable assemblages
are known from nearby Hallan Çemi, Demirköy, Gusir Höyük, and
Hasankeyf Höyük (Fig. 1).
This paper has employed a more holistic characterisation study
to detail common traditions of consumption amongst late 11the
early 10th millennium BC communities of south-eastern Anatolia
and the Levant. Such an approach enables us to make clear
distinctions in how Bingöl and Nemrut Dag source materials were
being used by broadly contemporary populations. Some of these
differences can be mapped at the inter-regional level, namely the
bladelet tradition(s) of the Upper Tigris populations versus the
pressure-flaked blade tradition(s) of the northern Levant. In turn,
we can document more subtle local distinctions, as with the
differential use of eastern Anatolian obsidian at Körtik Tepe and
Hallan Çemi. Here we return to one of our project’s opening
questions as to whether obsidian exchange represented ‘the’
network through which the Neolithic worlds of the Near East were
connected and part-constituted. Our results would suggest not,
defining instead a series of inter-community distinctions e at both
the regional and local level e that provide further evidence to
support the vision of the Near Eastern Neolithic as more heterogeneous than appreciated previously, with subsistence and now
technical practices and raw material choices highly localised and
contingent (cf. Arbuckle and Özkaya, 2006: 114; Asouti, 2010;
Conolly et al., 2011; Goring-Morris and Belfer-Cohen, 2011).
The elucidation of shared modes of consumption, or ‘communities of practice’ (common traditions of raw material choice/
technical practice amongst contemporary populations [Knappett,
2011: 98e123]), represents a powerful means of using characterisation data. Following debates from the sociology of technology it
can be argued that common forms of technical practice/raw
material choice implies a significant level of on-the-ground interaction between populations. The complex web of choices that
comprise these PPNA lithic traditions were likely transmitted
‘vertically’ from parent to child within a community, and shared/
perpetuated amongst members of other populations through intermarriage and other deeply binding socio-economic relations (cf.
Gosselain, 2000; Gosselain and Livingstone Smith, 2005: 42e43).
Ultimately the practices and their material forms are viewed as
mutually constitutive, i.e. these traditions are not only reflections of
a community’s way-of-being, but also a fundamental aspect
(performance) of what it is to be a member of that social group
(Gosselain, 2000: 209; Knappett, 2011: 104).
In the case of the Upper Tigris region there are various practical factors that one might consider concerning how intercommunity connectivity was articulated. For instance, from the
viewpoint of Körtik Tepe (Fig. 1), anyone travelling to obsidian
sources would have travelled up the Batman River and by extent
via the villages of Hallan Çemi and Demirköy (at different
periods). Alternatively, these villagers may have been intermediaries in the transport and dissemination of Bingöl and Nemrut
Dag obsidian, with the inhabitants of Körtik Tepe procuring these
raw materials through establishing long-term exchange partners
and/or inter-marriage. On the larger scale, i.e. regarding links with
other communities up and downstream the Tigris at Çayönü,
Hasankeyf Höyük and Gusir Höyük, one can point to these
sedentary foragers’ subsistence practices, with hunting and
gathering likely involving their movement over relatively large
and overlapping territories.
While the pragmatics of raw material procurement and food
collection strategies can help explain the development and
maintenance of this regional network (or community of practice),
the ties that bound people together were avowedly social, with
connections and the sharing of know-how and practice articulated
at the inter-personal level via trade partnerships and spousal
exchange. Common ideology and other shared notions of being-inthe-world are other key factors that bound these populations
together (‘identity’ is of course the hitherto unspoken concept we
are dealing with here, though we resist any attempt to map such
terms as ‘ethnicity’ [cf. Shennan, 1989] upon these ‘communities of
practice’). Ultimately it is through these networks that new
ideas and practices would have come to be shared, be that
cereal cultivation, the spread of pressure-flaked blade production,
or those various other ways of life we have come to term ‘the
Neolithic’.
