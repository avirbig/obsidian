North and South – Variable Trajectories of the Neolithic in the
Levant
Anna Belfer-Cohen & Nigel Goring-Morris
For years, there has been an ongoing debate about which region played a more pivotal role during the various stages
of the Neolithization processes, the southern or the northern Levant. As more data accumulates through the larger
region of West Asia it becomes clear that evolutionary developments coexisted, portraying individual local rhythms and
varying interactions with neighbouring societies throughout the Levant (and beyond). Examples are provided through
selected material culture features of the Early Neolithic entities generally accepted as markers of the various stages
within the Neolithic sequence.
Introduction
With the beginning of archaeological research in the
Near East it was clearly the southern Levant that received
most of the attention, and the excavations at the unique site
of Jericho provided a chrono-stratigraphic sequence that
served (and indeed still serves) as the foundation for the basic
four-fold subdivision of the Near Eastern Neolithic (Kenyon
1956). In the famous debate between Kenyon (1957) and
Braidwood (1957) concerning the precise location within the
Fertile Crescent in which one should look for the beginnings
of the Neolithic, the radiocarbon evidence then indicated
the precedence of Jericho over Jarmo (Iraqi Kurdistan). Still,
it was obvious that, while Kenyon believed that most new
stages in the archaeological sequence represented incursions
from elsewhere rather than local developments, she voiced the
opinion of many others in recognizing the Natufian culture as
a precursor of Neolithic developments in the region. Indeed,
even quite recently the paradigm of the so-called ‘primacy
of the southern Levant’ continued to rankle (e.g. Watkins
2008). Through time, from the 1970s onwards, excavation
projects took place in the greater region of the northern
Fertile Crescent and new sites provided a plethora of solid
data from the Early Neolithic rivalling the archaeological
record of the southern Levant, e.g. the series ‘The Neolithic
in Turkey’ (Özdogan et al. 2011–2014). While research has
continued apace in the southern Levant, slowly but surely
the geographic origin of purported innovations has shifted
northwards towards the Middle Euphrates and Tigris regions.
Thus, while opinions still differ as regards the PPNA, the
south Levantine PPNB was considered to represent a cultural
‘package’ imported from the north, comprising all its essential
subsistence and material culture characteristics. The site of
Jerf el-Ahmar (Stordeur 2012) has played a crucial role in the
assumption of the PPNA–PPNB transition occurring in the
north (and see Edwards et al. 2004; Kuijt 2003).
Subsequently, new early Neolithic sites in the northern
Levant have been reported from the region further to the
east along the upper reaches of the Tigris, e.g. Körtik Tepe
and Gusir Tepe (Karul 2011; Özkaya and Coşkun 2011),
as well as the area between the Middle Euphrates and the
Mediterranean coast, e.g. Tell Qaramel (Mazurowski 2006;
Mazurowski and Kanjou 2012). There also have been
renewed and new investigations in Central Anatolia at
Boncuklu (Baird et al. 2012), Aşikli (Özbaşaran 2012) and
Çatalhöyük (Hodder 2012).
No less spectacular have been the series of investigations
in Cyprus over the past two decades that have pushed back
the systematic colonization of the island to the end of the
late Epipalaeolithic, including a rich Early Neolithic record
(Guilaine et al. 2011; McCartney 2011; 2010; Peltenburg et
al. 2001; Simmons 2001, 2007; Vigne et al. 2012).
At the same time, in the south-central Levant a plethora
of projects were initiated from the late 1970s onwards
– most notably east of the Rift Valley in Transjordan in
the Mediterranean zone, e.g. ‘Ain Ghazal (Rollefson 1983;
1986; 1998), Basta (Gebel et al. 2006b; Nissen et al. 2004;
and references therein), but also in the semi-arid and arid
peripheries in the eastern desert, Sinai and the Negev (e.g.
Betts et al. 2013; Fujii 2010; Garrard 1998; Goring-Morris
and Belfer-Cohen 2014; Henry 2005).
The Damascus basin plays a key geographical role in
connecting the southern and northern Levant, in the sense
of whether Tell Aswad (Stordeur et al. 2010) is located within
the south-central Levant, as we believe (Goring-Morris and
Belfer-Cohen 2011a), or whether it relates to the northern
Levant in terms of modern political boundaries.2 Gary Rollefson Festschrift
Additionally, and notwithstanding contrary views (see
above), the recent investigations at sites such as Motza
(Khalaily et al. 2007) and Kfar HaHoresh (Goring-Morris et
al. 2008) in the Mediterranean zone of the southern Levant
have demonstrated convincingly that there is evidence for
the in situ presence of the Early PPNB, a short ‘bridging’
phase, chronologically and developmentally, between the
end of the PPNA and the main PPNB phases, locally.
Still, even after it has transpired that within the Near
East local, regional variants of Neolithic evolutionary
developments coexisted, each with its own rhythm, pace and
varying interactions with neighbouring societies, questions
regarding ‘origins’ and ‘first occurrences’ of the main/
common Neolithic traits have remained open to debate,
both in the practical as well as within the theoretical realms
of ongoing research. We would like to present examples of
the current state of the south-north ‘dichotomy’, as reflected
by selected material culture characteristics of the Early
Neolithic entities; these are generally accepted as markers of
the various stages within the Neolithic sequence.
Selected Facets of Early Neolithic Material Culture
Since the realm of domestication, whether of plants or
animals, though to be the core of ‘Neolithization’ processes,
is too complex and wide a subject to be treated herein – and
see current overviews concerning issues relating to plant (e.g.
Asouti and Fuller 2013; Gopher, Lev Yadun & Abbo 2013)
and animal (Vigne et al. 2011; Zeder 2012) domestication
– we focus upon more discrete traits such as architectural
features, distinct lithic techno-typological variants, symbolic
motifs, and funerary practices.
Architectural features
The development of architectural features relates to both
residential as well as communal/public spheres, the latter
including monumental elements (e.g. Byrd 2005; Goring-
Morris and Belfer-Cohen 2008; Schmidt 2012).
In terms of Neolithic domestic structures there is a shift
from oval to quadrilateral architecture in both north and
south, considered, when first defined, as a major marker of
the Neolithization processes. Research has demonstrated
that there was continued use of oval structures throughout
the course of the Neolithic and later still, especially in more
peripheral areas, where adaptations were based on greater
mobility and different subsistence patterns, i.e. pastoralism
vs. agriculture (e.g. Fuiji 2013; Martin 1999). Moreover,
even in the region where that shift was observed, it differed
in magnitude and timing, regardless of whether it is a
southern or a northern site. Indeed, in some instances both
oval and quadrilateral structures co-existed within the same
site, e.g. at Jerf el-Ahmar in the north (Stordeur 2012), and
in the south at Early PPNB Motza and M/LPPNB Wadi
Abu Tulayha (Fujii 2009; Khalaily et al. 2007).
The initiation of public space and buildings relates to
increasing community sizes and sedentism as mechanisms
of social cohesion and solidarity in order to alleviate
increasing scalar stress (Dunbar 2004; Johnson 1982). In
terms of the history of research, Kenyon’s excavations at
Jericho revealing the PPNA tower, wall and silos were the
first discoveries of its kind. For decades the tower stood
alone and inevitably led to claims concerning the supposed
‘primacy’ of the south. This was sustained through the
discoveries of cultic structures at PPNB Beidha and later
on those at ‘Ain Ghazal (Kirkbride 1968; Rollefson 2000).
However, matters were turned on their head during the past
25 years by the discovery, seemingly ‘out of nowhere’, of
‘kiva-type’ communal structures at the northern PPNA Jerf
el-Ahmar and Tel ‘Abr 3 (Stordeur et al. 2000; Yartah 2005);
and, retroactively, at Mureybet, too – previously interpreted
as a residential structure (Cauvin 1994; Ibañez 2008). Of
obvious relevance are the spectacular monumental enclosures
at PPNA/B Göbekli Tepe and other similar sites in the area of
the middle Euphrates (Schmidt 2012). Still, the more recent
discovery of monumental communal structures in the south
at PPNA Wadi Faynan 16 and early PPNB Kfar HaHoresh
makes the dichotomous (south vs. north) overviews of early
Neolithic developments in public architecture obsolete and
misleading both as regards the data and their interpretation
(Finlayson et al. 2011; Goring-Morris 2008; Goring-Morris
et al. 2008).
Chipped stone techno-typology
Detailed techno-typological studies of Early Neolithic
chipped stone industries have revealed a somewhat fractured
picture concerning inventions, innovations and their
subsequent diffusion in the northern and southern regions
of the Levant.
The current evidence would appear to indicate that
the earliest lithic markers, namely the Khiam points and
Hagdud/Gilgal truncations originated in the south with the
transition from the final Natufian to the Khiamian (Enoch-
Shiloh and Bar-Yosef 1997; Nadel 1997). Shortly thereafter
similar forms appear also in the PPNA and ‘Round House
Horizon’ in the north (e.g. Peasnall 2000; Watkins et al.
1989). Additionally, the marked morphological similarity
of the Late Epipalaeolithic Harif point reported from sites
in the Negev and Sinai and the ‘Round House Horizon’
Nemrik point of the Middle Euphrates and Upper Tigris is
notable (Goring-Morris and Belfer-Cohen 2011b).
However, the consensus of opinion posits that subsequently
the primary direction of innovation and diffusion was from
the north to the south: this includes both the innovation
of the bidirectional (naviform) technique during the later
stages of the PPNA at Jerf el-Ahmar producing targeted
blade blanks, as well as arrowhead types such as the Helwan
point (Abbès 2003; Gopher 1989; Barzilai 2013).3Belfer-Cohen & Nigel Goring-Morris: North and South – Variable Trajectories of the Neolithic in the Levant
In the south, the picture is rather complex; there is the
abrupt appearance of the bidirectional naviform method,
well documented during the Early PPNB at certain
southern sites such as Motza and Kfar HaHoresh (Barzilai
2010). The raw material of the high quality naviform blades
from Early PPNB levels at both sites is clearly non-local,
but does not appear to derive from the Middle Euphrates
region, so perhaps hitherto undocumented, geographically
intermediate sources in the area between Gilead in northern
Transjordan and the Palmyra basin should be sought
(personal observations; Barzilai 2010; 2013; Khalaily et al.
2007).
With regards to the Helwan points in the south (Gopher
1989), some are fashioned on elongated bidirectional blade
blanks as at Mikhmoret, while in other coeval sites such as
Nahal Lavan 109 morphologically similar Helwan points
are fashioned on short blade/flake blanks deriving from
other reduction sequences; and, indeed, sometimes both
reduction sequences are found in the same assemblage, as at
Motza (Barzilai 2013). Also of interest is the rare appearance
of obsidian Helwan points, e.g. Nahal Lavan 109, Qumran
cave 24, Motza and Kfar HaHoresh (Burian et al. 1999;
Gopher, Lemorini et al. 2013; Goring-Morris et al. 2008;
Khalaily et al. 2007). As an aside, no other projectile point
types fashioned on obsidian have been reported from PPNA/
B contexts in the south.
As data accumulate and there is more information about
the Levantine lithic industries, it becomes quite clear that
the regional interactions observed regarding various aspects
of the chipped stone technology and typology were by
no means unidirectional; an interesting example of inter-
regional exchange of projectile points has been reported
from Tell Ain el-Kerkh in the north (Arimura 2013).
Symbols and decorative motifs
To illustrate our perspective detailed in the introduction,
we shall touch briefly upon one symbol only and one motif
– the ‘bull’ and the meander/chevron pattern, the association
of which with the ‘Neolithic’ is unequivocal.
The bull
Stating that the ‘revolution of symbols’ is a major
component of the ‘Neolithic revolution’, Jacques Cauvin
(2000) mentions the appearance of the ‘bull’ as a most
representative symbol of that revolution. Indeed, the ‘bull’,
present already in symbolic contexts at PPNA Jerf el-Ahmar
(Stordeur 2010) and PPNA/B Göbekli Tepe (Peters and
Schmidt 2004 ; Schmidt 2012) as well as at the Round
House Horizon site of Hallan Çemi (Rosenberg 1999)
continued to figure prominently in symbolic contexts in
northern regions, especially in central Anatolia, whether
at Middle PPNB Aşikli (Özbaşaran 2012) or Late PPNB
Çatalhöyük (Mellaart 1975; Hodder 2012).
However, recent investigations conclusively demonstrate
(and for detailed discussion see Goring-Morris and Belfer-
Cohen 2011b), that the motif of the bull within symbolic
contexts (burials and/or feasting) also featured in the
southern Levant from at least the Late/Final Natufian, e.g.
at Hilazon Tachtit cave (Munro and Grosman 2010), as well
as the PPNA Khiamian, e.g. Hatula (Goring-Morris and
Belfer-Cohen 2011b), and Early PPNB Kfar HaHoresh and
Motza (Horwitz Kolska and Goring-Morris 2004; Khalaily
et al. 2007; and see Twiss 2008). Here, too, we should recall
the presence of small figurative stone items, interpreted as
bucrania ‘horns’, which are found at Early Round House
Horizon Hallan Çemi (Rosenberg 2011) and Körtik
Tepe (Özkaya et al. 2013) in the north, as well as at Final
Natufian Nahal Ein Gev II in the southern Levant (Bar-
Yosef and Belfer-Cohen 2000). Accordingly, it is difficult
to pinpoint exactly when and where the aurochs/bucrania/
horns acquired their symbolic status.
The meander/chevron pattern
This pattern is a fairly simple but distinctive design that
has a widespread geographical and chronological distribution
throughout the Near East. It seems to first appear during the
Natufian at Eynan (Perrot 1966), Wadi Mataha (Gregg et al.
2011) and Upper Besor 6 (Goring-Morris 1998), continuing
thereafter. Of interest is its presence in PPNA contexts in the
southern Levant, on incised pebbles at Netiv Hagdud (Bar-
Yosef and Gopher 1997) and (as a mirror image) at Wadi
Faynan 16 (Finlayson et al. 2011); it was found also incised
on benches in the kiva-like structures at PPNA Wadi Faynan
16 in the south, and at PPNA Jerf el-Ahmar (Stordeur et
al. 2000), and Early PPNB Djadé (Coqueugniot in press)
on the Middle Euphrates, in the north. Needless to say, the
meander/chevron pattern subsequently became a common
theme, most especially with the appearance of pottery in the
Late Neolithic throughout the Levant.
Burial practices
Mortuary practices are an integral part of the human
social fabric, reflecting the nature and character of a
particular group. This is particularly so for Levantine
Neolithic societies, and it has been a common assumption
that changes and variability in burial practices indicate
the stage and degree of complexity of the Neolithization
processes of the given society. Practices such as skull removal
are considered as reflecting a long-standing tradition,
originating in the Epipalaeolithic Natufian, still retained
by many Neolithic communities, both in the south and
north of the Levant. Other, novel and distinctive behaviours
are thought to represent more ‘advanced’ stages of social
evolution pertaining to the ‘Neolithic Revolution’. We
detail several illustrations of such behaviours in mortuary
treatments in the northern and southern spheres of the4 Gary Rollefson Festschrift
Near East. For detailed discussion on such pertinent issues
as skull removal and plastered skulls, see e.g. Bonogofsky
2006; Goring-Morris and Belfer-Cohen 2011a, in press;
and references therein.
It is notable that the burial practices do not reflect a
linear evolution. Thus particular associations observed
in northern Early Neolithic sites, and not reported from
contemporaneous southern sites, can be found in southern
Epipalaeolithic sites. An example concerns the inclusion of
tortoise/turtle carapaces within graves, e.g. in the Natufian
sites of Eynan and Hilazon Tachtit Cave in the south
(Grosman et al. 2008; Perrot and Ladiray 1988), as well as in
the Early Round House Horizon (PPNA) at Körtik Tepe on
the Tigris in the north (Özkaya and Coşkun 2011; Özkaya
et al. 2013). The direct association in graves between human
burials and other animal remains already begins during the
Middle Epipalaeolithic Geometric Kebaran in the southern
Levant, with the inclusion in a grave of an articulated fox
at Uyun el-Hammam (Maher et al. 2011). This custom
persists and can be observed during the Natufian as joint
human/dog burials at Eynan and Hayonim Terrace (Davis
and Valla 1978; Perrot and Ladiray 1988; Tchernov and
Valla 1997), in the PPNA with a cattle cranium in a burial
at Hatoula (Lechevallier and Ronen 1994), and the PPNB
when cattle and fox bones at Kfar HaHoresh and wild boar
at ‘Ain Ghazal were interred together with human burials
(Goring-Morris and Horwitz 2007; Horwitz Kolska and
Goring-Morris 2004; Lechevallier and Ronen 1994). In the
north one can mention besides the burials at Körtik Tepe
(see above) also the one with a sheep from Late PPNB levels
at Çatalhöyük (Russell and During 2006).
In examining the data set of burial practices in the greater
Levant it becomes quite apparent that those mortuary
practices were first observed during the Neolithic, occurred
throughout the region, and, while some seemingly appeared
first in the south, others appeared first in the north. Without
giving a detailed account, it will suffice to mention a few of
those occurrences.
‘Seated’ burials were first reported in the literature from
the Middle PPNB site of Tel Halula (Ortiz et al. 2013) in
the northern Levant, but similar burials have recently been
reported from the PPNA site of el-Hemmeh in the southern
Levant (Makarewicz and Rose 2011), and there is also the
Early Epipalaeolithic sitting burial at ‘Ayn Qasiyya (Richter
et al. 2010). The same is true for the custom of burying in
stone-lined cists, first reported from the PPNB northern
sites of Djadé and Çayönu. Similar burials were recovered
from the southern PPNB site of Ba’ja (Coqueugniot 2006;
Croucher 2012; Gebel et al. 2006a). In the same vein one
should note the existence of ‘charnel houses’ in the north at
PPNB Çayönu (Özdogan 1999) and in the south at PPNB
Shakrat Msiad (Kinzel et al. 2011). We may also include
herein the use of plastic materials related to burial practices
– gypsum/lime plaster was found in direct association with
burials in the southern Levantine PPNA sites of Wadi
Faynan 16 and el-Hemmeh (Finlayson and Mithen 2007;
Makarewicz and Rose 2011), as well as in the northern sites
at PPNA Körtik Tepe (Özkaya et al. 2013) and at Middle/
Late PPNB Tell abu Hureyra (Moore et al. 2000). Much the
same situation pertains with regards to the cinnabar pigment
originating in central Anatolia (Mellaart 1975), found on
skulls at Late PPNB Çatalhöyük (Boz and Hager 2013) and
used for colouring one of the plastered skulls from Middle/
Late PPNB Kfar Hahoresh (Goren et al. 2001).
Discussion
Sophisticated pretreatment of organic samples and
the introduction of detailed calibration curves in recent
years have revolutionized 14C dating for the period under
discussion, namely the very end of the Pleistocene and
the earlier Holocene. Additionally, greater awareness
among researchers brought about the submission of series
of samples, providing more precise data concerning their
contexts. However, while there are often discussions of
site-specific sequences (e.g. Boaretto et al. 2010; Edwards
et al. 2004; Khalaily et al. 2007; Mazurowski et al. 2009),
or sequences of selected groups of site (e.g. Bischoff 2006;
Weninger et al. 2009), updated detailed inter-regional
studies, i.e. northern vs. southern Levant, are lacking.
Additionally there are issues of outliers and, sometimes,
problematic contextual information and dubious dated
materials. Accordingly, the current state of Levantine
chronological frameworks is in flux, rendering meaningful
comparisons at the inter-regional level, and the granting
of ‘primacy’ of one area over another in specific domains,
problematic and moot.
Similar parallels and debates concern the processes
involved in plant and animal manipulation and initial
domestication. In recent decades it has become clear that such
early developments, whether slow or rapid, should be viewed
from a long-term perspective (Belfer-Cohen and Goring-
Morris 2011). Was there indeed but a single ‘core’ region
of origin as some have argued, e.g. Abbo et al. 2011? Taking
into consideration the problem of defining ‘beginnings’ and
the issue of their archaeological recognition, this is in our
opinion but a non sequitur. Yet, once the process had begun,
i.e. been archaeologically recognized, could innovations
and inventions occur sporadically and independently in a
number of places at about the same time? In other words,
can we observe polycentric processes (Willcox 2007; and
see Vigne et al. 2011; Zeder 2011 for overviews for both
plants and animals)? Here, recent research on Cyprus, and
its implications for developments on the mainland, are
instructive and pivotal. For, with the earliest (currently)
orderly colonization of the island during the PPNA and
earlier PPNB we find the systematic introduction of non-5Belfer-Cohen & Nigel Goring-Morris: North and South – Variable Trajectories of the Neolithic in the Levant
endemic carnivores, such as the dog, cat and fox, as well
as other species. Some are domesticated (goat, sheep and
cattle), others were never domesticated (fallow deer), while
still others appear first as wild specimens (wild boar) and
only later on as domesticates (pigs) (Vigne et al. 2009; 2012).
Evidently animals were being transported systematically
from the mainland, usually in sufficient numbers to establish
viable and sustainable herds – though after some time the
cattle disappeared from the faunal record at Shillourokambos
(Guilaine et al. 2011). Moreover, some of the domesticated
species are (at least currently) documented earlier on Cyprus
than on the mainland.
The early colonization of Cyprus conclusively
demonstrates the mobility of Early Neolithic groups and
even communities settling in new areas, sometimes, but not
always, previously unoccupied. In this context it is instructive
to ‘rescue’ the naïve early assumption by Kenyon (1970) that
the archaeological record of the southern Levant was based
upon waves of nomads from the ‘desert’. It is clear today, as
reflected in shifting site distributions throughout the Near
East during the terminal Pleistocene/early Holocene that
groups and even communities abandoned certain localities
and founded new settlements elsewhere. It is difficult to
pinpoint the scale, both in numbers and in geographical
spread involved, but we should perhaps brace ourselves for
surprises as regards the distances and intensity of such posited
movements. Indeed, some of the examples enumerated above,
such as variable but distinctive architectural developments,
funerary practices, symbolic motifs, aspects of knapping
techniques and specific typological characteristics are likely
to reflect the dynamics of groups moving over the landscape
to found new settlements, sometimes in isolation and
sometimes coalescing with other groups. Thus, retaining
‘old’ traditions, while incorporating new behaviours through
interactions between endemic groups and ‘new comers’, may
explain the blend and variability of the archaeological data
referred to above.
During the PPNA and, especially, during the course of
the PPNB, initial steps were taken to address issues stemming
from the stresses of living in larger communities for longer
periods of time, especially in the larger settlements in each
region. Other tensions would have would have derived from
differences in subsistence, disparities in access to actual
resources and knowledge, and the emergence of wealth at
the personal, extended family and community scales.
Local attempts at alleviating such tensions would have
been constrained by the ‘assets’ available, the knowledge
and manpower at hand (see discussion in Goring-Morris
and Belfer-Cohen 2014; Shelach et al. 2011). Moreover,
endeavours to cope with socio-economic inequalities may
have led to attempts to ‘level the playing field’ and emphasize
(previous) egalitarian values, especially in the symbolic
and ritual spheres, whether within settlements or between
nearby communities. Of course each community or portion
thereof brought with it its own particular traditions, which
were then incorporated within the broader fabric of social
relations. Local solutions led to separate and distinct, yet
parallel, outcomes as reflected in the significant material
variability apparent in the archaeological record. The Early
Neolithic architectural phenomena and the considerable
variability displayed can be easily explained by the above.
This may also be an explanation of the central role of
Göbekli Tepe as reflecting an amphictyony-like centre for
competing communities in the region (Belfer-Cohen and
Goring-Morris 2002, 44; Schmidt 2005, 15).
Each innovation or invention that one can observe in the
Early Neolithic took time to develop and was intrinsically
involved with other technologies, each accordingly differing
in tempo, scale and intensity between the different regions
(e.g., the dependence of the pottery industry on evolving
clay and plaster technologies, Ben-Michael 2014; Biton et
al. 2014; Schmandt-Besserat 1974; 1977a; 1977b). Even if
the very first notion pertaining to any particular invention
was conceived in the brain of one particular person, at a
certain time and in a specific locality, once the idea or the
concept spread, its implementation and testing in everyday
life could happen contemporaneously and independently in
several places. It could occur at the same time, or at some
stages earlier and other stages later in each locality, dependent
upon local circumstances and contexts.
There remains the issue of communication and
connections between communities. Exchange and trade
in valued commodities certainly increased significantly
from the Natufian, the PPNA through to the PPNB. Most
probably those could have been vehicles for the promotion
of additional information relevant to various aspects of
daily existence. Another possible means of disseminating
information and knowledge is the specialists groups, not
tied to specific locales, playing the ‘middlemen’ in such
processes. They would most probably have passed on not
just the actual goods, but also the ‘knowledge’ associated
with their production, akin to the guilds or itinerant tinkers
of the Middle Ages.
The existence of ‘small-world exchange networks’
probably provides a parsimonious framework to comprehend
such dynamics at various levels. These may have involved the
exchange of exotic items, other goods, or knowledge, with
certain sites functioning as central nodes in such processes
(and see Ortega et al. 2013). In the south, the megasite
villages along the ‘King’s Highway’ in Transjordan, as well as
those within the Rift valley may have served for major north-
south and south-north connections (Gebel 2004; Goring-
Morris et al. 2009), with subsidiary east-west and west-east
branches of exchange and communication. In the north the
major rivers may have served similar roles (Goring-Morris
and Belfer-Cohen 2011a).6 Gary Rollefson Festschrift
So, finally, what does all of the above have to do
with the nature of relations between the southern and
northern Levant? We believe that networks of interactions,
communications and exchange between these two regions
were already well developed by at least the end of the Late
Epipalaeolithic, e.g., the presence of northern obsidian
in southern Natufian sites (Delerue 2007; Khalaily and
Valla 2013). The complex dynamics and rapid tempo of
changes observed in the archaeological data, coupled with
the problems of exact dating, preclude the assignment of
‘primacy’ to one region or the other. Furthermore, when
one tries to follow the evolution of a particular aspect of
the material culture, it becomes apparent that what we
actually see is a constant influx of ideas and materials,
an ‘interaction sphere’ just like the famous PPNB koine
(Bar-Yosef and Belfer-Cohen 1989). Trying to deduce at
arbitrary points of the evolution process what was the
direction of the diffusion (north-to-south or south-to-
north) is rather counterproductive if we are to understand
the whole of the process examined. Rather, it is of interest
to try and unveil the mechanisms that enabled the pushing
forward of the process, and to ask which component was
more apt to be ‘accepted’ and incorporated within the
fabric of the new life-ways. What circumstances helped
in its adoption? What caused some of the archaeological
phenomena observed to be abandoned? And what dictated
the differences observed in similar realms within the two
regions? Looking for absolute ‘origins’ and ‘supremacy’
adds to the difficulties we face in trying to understand the
nature of Neolithic phenomena. The most appropriate
epitaph for such a futile enterprise is “looking through a
glass darkly”.
