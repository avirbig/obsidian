Instrumental Neutron Activation Analysis (INAA) is a nuclear analytic method that
has been applied with great success to archaeology for over four decades as a tool
for determining the origin of archaeological materials. The diversity of chemical elements whose concentration can be measured with high precision and accuracy makes
INAA ideal for determining the provenance of pottery and obsidian. In this paper we
first briefly survey the background of the development of INAA, and then present an
overview of investigations in which INAA was utilized in the study of archaeological
remains from the Land of Israel, from the early Prehistoric periods until the Middle
Ages. Special focus is placed on studies that opened up new vistas and understandings
on various issues in the archaeological record.
INtroductIoN
Among the various types of nuclear analytical methods
that are applicable to the analysis of materials, Neutron
Activation Analysis (NAA) has proven itself as the
most useful and accurate for the analysis of a wide
range of archaeological artifacts. In its more general
form, NAA involves the chemical preparation of the
samples, in order to extract specific elements of inter-
est with extreme sensitivity. NAA involving chemical
preparation is not suitable for archaeological investigations. The method that is employed in the analysis
of archaeological artifacts is Instrumental Neutron Activation Analysis (INAA). Although NAA and INAA
are often used interchangeably, in archaeology, INAA
is the method that is always used. The “Instrumental”
in INAA refers to the fact that there is no chemical
preparation of the samples, and that the analysis, from
beginning to end, is purely instrumental. It is thus
a method that determines the abundances of many
elements simultaneously, without having to resort to
chemical processes. This is one of the great advantages that INAA has over other methods of chemical
analysis. Sample preparation is purely mechanical,
and hence, it is rapid and largely free of myriads of
complications involved in other methods. The latter
involve time-consuming chemical preparation of the
sample, and chemical reactions may take place and as
a result influence the measuring process. Furthermore,
because high-energy photons are involved, matrix ef-
fects that can be significant in X-ray fluorescence are
minimal.124 Israel Journal of Earth Sciences Vol. 56, 2007
The use of neutron activation as an analytical tool is
attributed to George Charles de Hevesy (1885–1966;
see Levi, 1985). He realized that radioactive isotopic
species produced by neutron bombardments could
be identified through the unique half-life of the
radioisotope resulting from the neutron bombardment
and quantified from the magnitude of the radioactivity.
In its modern form the identification is done through the
unique gamma-ray spectrum that follows the neutron
activation, and the quantification is accomplished by
the measure of the gamma-ray intensity (in fact, INAA
refers to gamma-ray spectrometry following neutron
activation). It is from the gamma-ray spectrum that
elemental abundances are derived. Neutron activation
is the process by means of which the gamma-ray
spectrum is produced. Gamma-ray spectroscopy is in
many ways simpler than other types of spectroscopy.
The sample is transparent to the high-energy gammaray photons, typically in excess of 100 KeV, so that
there are no significant photon–sample interactions or,
in other words, no matrix effects (with some exception
below 100 KeV).
The neutrons involved in INAA are slow or
thermal neutrons (approx. 0.025 eV) whose source
today is a nuclear reactor. The activation caused
by slow neutrons (neutron capture reactions) was
discovered in 1934 by Enrico Fermi (1938), not
long after the neutron was discovered (in 1932) by
James Chadwick (1935). Thus, the basic science for
INAA was established at the dawn of the nuclear
age. However, the successful implementation of
INAA to archaeology had to await revolutionary
technological developments in solid state physics,
computers, digital electronics, and computational
procedures, as well as the accumulation of vast
quantities of gamma-ray spectrometric data. The first
successful application to archaeology was reported
by Sayre and Dodson (1957), who were inspired
by J.R. Oppenheimer (Harbottle, 1990; Speakman
and Glascock, 2007). Another early application was
reported by Emeleus (1958). Large-scale applications
to archaeology became a reality in the mid-1960s
with the advent of the lithium drifted (Sayre, 1966),
and later, the high purity germanium detector, high
speed computers, and computational procedures for
spectroscopy and statistical analysis. It was at this
time that Perlman and Asaro began their dedicated
and highly precise work on the archaeology of Israel
(Asaro and Adan-Bayewitz, 2007). A recent volume
of Archaeometry highlighted 50 years of INAA in
archaeology (Speakman and Glascock, 2007).
Over the past four decades, a number of laboratories
applied INAA to provenance problems relating to the
archaeology of the Land of Israel and its surroundings,
and among them the laboratory that was established in
the Institute of Archaeology of The Hebrew University
of Jerusalem, which operated until 1997 (Yellin,
2007).

Over the last forty-odd years, INAA has been used in
a wide variety of studies relating to the archaeological and historical periods in the Land of Israel. In this
short overview we have chosen to note only a selection of studies that aptly demonstrate the important
contribution that INAA has provided in the study of
antiquity in this region. We have selected studies that
relate to different periods, from Prehistoric through the
medieval periods, noting with each example the sig-
nificance of the findings and analyses. Although most
of the studies deal with the provenience of ceramics,
other materials were studied as well, notably obsidian,
basalt, and flint.
Prehistoric (before ca. 3500 bce)
The utility of INAA for determining the provenience
of non-ceramic archaeological materials was demonstrated in the study of obsidian and, to a lesser extent,
flint. Although other methods (e.g., optical emission
spectroscopy, X-ray fluorescence spectroscopy, fis-
sion track analysis) had been previously used for this
purpose (e.g., Renfrew et al., 1966; Durrani et al.,
1971), the accurate and wide-ranging data set that was
analyzed using INAA enabled an accurate and convincing demonstration of the origin of obsidian from
the Neolithic and Chalcolithic Periods. Archaeological
obsidian from sites in Israel have been traced to obsidian sources in central and eastern Anatolia (Perlman
and Yellin, 1978, 1980; Yellin and Garfinkel, 1986;
Yellin and Frachtenberg, 1992; Yellin and Gopher,
1992; Yellin, 1995, 1997; Yellin et al., 1996). These
analyses provided solid evidence for the long-range
trade and interaction that existed during late Prehistory—contacts that had been assumed, but not suf-
ficiently proven until then. While flint is a much more
difficult material to analyze (by any method), useful
results can be obtained (Frachtenberg and Yellin,
1992; Frachtenberg, 1993; Yellin, 1995). Additional
applications of INAA to Prehistoric archaeology can
be noted as well. Goren-Inbar et al. (1986) used INAAJ. Yellin and A.M. Maeir. Four decades of INAA in the archaeology of Israel 125

Early bronze Age (ca. 3500–2000 bce)
The advent of urbanism that is witnessed during the
Early Bronze Age brought about a wide range of social, economic, and political changes in the region.
This is explicitly seen in the ceramic assemblages, as
manifested in the technology, trade, and inter-regional
and international connections. INAA has contributed
particularly to the question of relations and contacts
between the various regions during this period. A joint
study combining INAA, petrography, and ICP clearly
demonstrated the Egyptian origin of select ceramics
from Early Bronze Age Canaan (Porat et al., 1991).
Pottery that was thought to be imported was shown to
be of local manufacture, both in the case of the Khirbet
Beth Yerah ware (which was produced locally under the
influence of foreign traditions; Esse and Hopke, 1986),
and in the case of a unique red and black bowl from
Jerusalem, which despite previous suggestions that it
is a foreign import, was demonstrated to be of local,
Jerusalem provenience (Maeir et al., 1992).
Towards the end of the Early Bronze Age the urban
nature of culture in the Land of Israel recedes and a
period of rural and/or nomadic lifestyles is seen. During
this period (the Early Bronze IV/Middle Bronze I), there
is little, if any, evidence of international trade between
the Land of Israel and other regions. On the other hand,
extensive evidence of localized ceramic cultures within
the land is seen. The local production of various groups
was once again clearly demonstrated by INAA (e.g.,
Yellin and Perlman, 1984; Falconer, 1987; subsequently
shown by petrography as well, see Goren, 1996).
Middle bronze Age (ca. 2000–1550 bce)
The Middle Bronze Age (MB) is often considered
one of the most vibrant periods in the history of the
Land of Israel. In addition to a return to a full-blown
urban culture, the archaeological evidence indicates an
advent of many new and sophisticated technological
developments (in ceramics, metallurgy, architecture,
etc.), and widespread evidence for international trade
and contacts. The ceramics from this period are particularly impressive, showing an improvement in the
production techniques and distribution. INAA has
contributed substantially to the understanding of this
period, through a wide series of studies that have analyzed the origin of various classes of pottery groups.
The contribution of INAA to the MB studies can be
demonstrated through the following examples:
1. Repeated INAA programs (e.g., Artzy and Asaro,
1979; Kaplan, 1980; Kaplan et al., 1982, 1984;
Yellin, 1984; McGovern et al., 1994; Maeir and
Yellin, 2008) that have analyzed the Tell el-Yahudiyeh wares, typical of the this period, have conclusively demonstrated that this group was produced at
various locations in Egypt and the Southern Levant
during the late MB IIA through MB IIB. These
studies have corroborated and expanded the typological studies of this ware family (e.g., Kaplan,
1980; Bietak, 1989; Maeir and Mullins, in press),
and have explicitly shown the international scope
of trade and cultural contact (including the influ-
ence on the production of vessel types over a wide
zone) during this period.
2. A large-scale project, which analyzed MB pottery
from a wide range of sites in Egypt, Canaan, Syria,
and elsewhere, attempted to show the scope of the
international trade during this period (McGovern
and Harbottle, 1997; McGovern, 2000). In fact,
McGovern claimed that the majority of the pottery
reaching Egypt was derived from southern Canaan.
Even though this suggestion has been challenged
based on a wide set of petrographic analyses (Co-
hen-Weinberger and Goren, 2004, who suggest a
northern Levant origin for this pottery), nevertheless, the scope and nature of the international trade
during this period has been demonstrated.
3. In several in-depth studies of pottery provenience in
the Jordan Valley during the MB, Falconer (1987),
Knapp et al. (1988), and Maeir and Yellin (2008)
have demonstrated the dynamics of pottery pro-
duction and exchange in this region. While much
of the pottery was locally produced, an interesting
web of intra- and inter-regional exchange of pottery was seen. As such, these studies contributed to
the understanding of such issues as site hierarchy,
regional trade, and production scales during this
period.
4. Several unique groups of pottery from this period
have been studied through INAA. These include
various imported wares in the Jordan valley (e.g.,126 Israel Journal of Earth Sciences Vol. 56, 2007
Egyptian imports; Maeir and Yellin, 2008), the so-
called “Red, White and Blue Ware”, which origi-
nates in the southern Coastal Plain Canaan (Maeir
2002; Maeir and Yellin, 2008), Cypriote pottery
(Yellin, 1984), and finally, the “Bichrome” ware
(which begins to appear in this period and continues
to the Late Bronze Age). This latter ware, which
was one of the first Levantine pottery groups to be
studied by INAA (Artzy, 1975; Artzy et al., 1978),
was conclusively demonstrated, for the most part,
to originate from Cyprus. This is in stark contrast to
the Canaanite provenience that had been suggested
on the basis of purely typological studies (e.g., Epstein, 1966). To a large extent, the surprising results
of the INAA study of this group first demonstrated
the important, and at times revolutionary, impact
that INAA could have on the archaeology of the
region.
5. In a study combining various fields (including
INAA and petrography), a fragment of a pithos
with a Linear A inscription from Tel Haror (in
the NW Negev) was analyzed (Oren et al., 1996).
Based on both methods of provenience analysis,
the Cretan origin of this unique shard was clearly
demonstrated, supporting the typological and epigraphic analyses. The origin of this piece served as
an excellent illustration of the long-range trade and
cultural relations that existed between the Aegean
and the Southern Levant during this period.
Late bronze Age (ca. 1550–1200 bce)
The international character of the Late Bronze Age,
during which there is extensive evidence for longrange and diverse trade and cultural contacts, is an
excellent test case for the utility and importance of
INAA studies. As mentioned above, perhaps the first
study that demonstrated the impact of INAA on SyroPalestinian archaeology was the provenience study
of the Middle/Late Bronze Age “Bichrome” pottery
(Artzy et al., 1973, 1976; Artzy, 1975). Following
this breakthrough study, time and again INAA studies
relating to the Late Bronze Age in the Land of Israel
have provided important insights into this period.
Several examples can be noted:
1. The web of international connections that is seen
during this period is well represented by the wide
variety of imported pottery found at virtually all
Late Bronze Age sites in Israel. INAA studies provided a subtler understanding of the mechanisms
behind this trade in pottery. For example, the study
of various imported Mycenaean pottery has demonstrated that although much of this pottery originates
in the Aegean (in particular, but not only, in the
Argolid; see, e.g., Gunneweg et al., 1992; Yellin
and Maeir, 1992; Gunneweg and Michel, 1999),
certain groups were produced in Cyprus or even
locally (e.g., Koehl and Yellin, 2007). The relations
between Canaan and Cyprus were analyzed as well,
whether they be Cypriote imports to Canaan (e.g.,
Artzy et al., 1973; Yellin, 1984), or Canaanite imports to Cyprus (Gunneweg et al., 1987).
2. The Egyptian rule in Canaan during the Late
Bronze Age was manifested, among other things,
by the presence of bona fide Egyptian or “Egyp-
tianizing” objects at various sites. INAA of pottery
displaying Egyptian characteristics from various
sites in Canaan made it possible to differentiate locally made, “Egyptianizing” vessels from those that
were actually imported to Canaan from Egypt (e.g.,
Goldberg et al., 1986; Yellin et al., 1986, 1990).
3. Perhaps the most important literary source for this
period is the el-Amarna letters. Following extensive
literary analyses of these texts, the first attempts to
chemically provenance these texts were done using
INAA (Artzy et al., 1976; see as well Yellin, 2001),
which led to the in-depth petrographic study by Goren et al. (2003). More recently, INAA was used in
conjunction with petrography to analyze Akkadian
(Goren et al., 2007) and Egyptian (Ben-Shlomo et
al., 2008) documents from Canaan as well.
All told, INAA studies on a variety of Late Bronze
Age materials have provided interesting insights into
the international and inter-regional relations during
this period, often at a “resolution” unavailable from
other avenues of archaeological research.
Iron Age I (ca. 1200–1000 bce)
The major changes that occurred at the end of the
Late Bronze Age and the beginning of the Iron Age
are evidenced throughout the entire ancient Near East.
The collapse of the Late Bronze Age world system,
along with the appearance of new cultural groups, are
but part of the major changes that occurred during the
beginning of the Iron Age. INAA studies have provided important insights into this period, at times even
revolutionary in their importance.
Several examples can be noted:
1. The appearance of the Philistine culture is one of

Discussions on the origins of this culture continue
to this day, but INAA provided some of the most
basic information. During the initial stage of the
Philistine culture, the Mycenaean IIIC pottery is
one of the hallmarks of this period. Although it is
almost virtually identical to Mycenaean IIIC pottery from the Aegean and Cyprus, INAA studies
demonstrated conclusively that most, if not all, of
this pottery from Philistia was in fact locally-produced (e.g., Asaro et al., 1971; Gunneweg et al.,
1986b, 1990)—a strong argument that immigrant
potters produced Mycenaean-style pottery in Philistia during this initial stage. At the same time,
INAA also demonstrated (e.g., Gunneweg et al.,
1990) that some of the Mycenaean IIIC pottery
from other regions in Israel was imported (from
Cyprus).
2. During more advanced stages of the Iron Age I, the
Philistines expanded their presence and influence to
other parts of Israel, beyond the region of Philistia.
Once again, INAA served as a method to demonstrate and pinpoint the origin of these connections
(e.g., Gunneweg et al., 1990; Gunneweg, 1994).
3. The unique material culture in the inland, moun-
tainous regions of Canaan, often associated with the
settlement of the Israelite tribes, has also been the
focus of INAA studies. A case in point is the study
of the so-called “Collared-rim jars,” often seen as
typical of the Israelite settlement. INAA studies of
this class of vessels (e.g., Yellin and Gunneweg,
1989b) demonstrated a very varied origin of these
vessels, indicating that the simplistic, monolithic
understanding of these vessels was unwarranted. A
very varied origin was also seen in the examination
of “Collared-rim jars” from Tel Nami (Salmon,
2001).
4. INAA studies of other regions in Israel (e.g.,
Megiddo—see Harrison and Hancock, 2005) have
shown the great variety of pottery types found at
sites during this period, once again demonstrating
the multifaceted nature of this period.
Iron Age II (ca. 1000–586 bce)
In the Iron Age II one sees the appearance of various
ethnic kingdoms and cultures in the Land of Israel
and neighboring regions (e.g., the kingdoms of Israel,
Judah, Edom, Moab, Ammon, along with Philistines,
Phoenicians, Arameans, etc.). Towards the 2nd half of
this period, the region comes under the control of the
large Mesopotamian empires (Assyria and Babylonia).
Over the last 30 years, INAA studies have provided important insights into the international and local relations
during this period from a wide range of perspectives.
1. Evidence of local trade of marked cooking pots during the early parts of this period was evidenced at
several sites in the north of the country (e.g., Sharon
et al., 1987).
2. The so-called “LMLK” jars have been regarded
for many years as one of the manifestations of the
Judean kingdom during the late 8th century bce.
Due to the different stamps on these jars, a vigorous
discussion on their origin(s) and meaning developed over the years. INAA studies (Mommsen et
al., 1984) conclusively demonstrated that these vessels have the same chemical composition, indicating a centralized production center(s) in the Judean
Shephelah (foothills) where such a composition
has been observed in a number of sites. This was
a somewhat surprising result, as scholars had assumed that either the jars were produced in various
locations within the Judean kingdom, or perhaps, in
the immediate vicinity of the capital, Jerusalem.
(3) Similarly, the later “Rosette” jars, also seen as a
manifestation of the 7th century bce Judean kingdom, were also shown, through INAA, to have
originated from the same region (Cahill, 1995;
Yellin and Cahill, 1998, 2004).
4. Unique pottery groups from the southern parts of
Israel were known to have typological similarities
to pottery from Transjordan. INAA studies of these
groups (e.g., Gunneweg and Mommsen, 1990,
1995; Gunneweg et al., 1991a) demonstrated the
connections and/or lack thereof for these various
types (the so-called Negbite, Midianite, Edomite,
and other pottery).
5. Other classes of Iron Age pottery have been examined as well, including earlier versions of the
“LMLK” jars (e.g., Ben-Shlomo et al., 2008), se-
lected pottery from specific sites (e.g., Yellin and
Perlman, 1978; Gunneweg et al., 1985; Gunneweg
and Yellin, 1991), and Cypriote, Phoenician, and
Greek imports (e.g., Yellin and Perlman, 1978;
Matthers et al., 1983; Yellin, 1989; Gunneweg and
Perlman, 1991; Ben-Shlomo et al., 2008). These
studies once again illuminated subtle and at times
unexpected aspects of the intra- and inter-regional
connections during this period.
6. Other classes of Iron Age finds were studied by
INAA, such as Judean figurines (Yellin, 1996) and
metal-production-related items from Tel Dan (Manea-Krichten et al., 1989).128 Israel Journal of Earth Sciences Vol. 56, 2007
Persian Period (ca. 586–330 bce)
Relatively few INAA studies have focused on the
Persian period. Nevertheless, several studies can be
noted, such as the study of finds from a Persian Period
shipwreck near Ma’agan Michael on the northern
coast of Israel (Yellin and Artzy, 2004), the study of
the so-called “MWSH” stamp impressions from Judah
(Zorn et al., 1994), and a study of various types of
pottery, both local and imported (Cypriote, Syrian,
Aegean, and other), from Tell el-Hesi (Bennet and
Blakely, 1989: 220–221).
hellenistic Period (ca. 330-66 bce)
Although the pottery from the Hellenistic Period has
not been the focus of many INAA studies, several
noteworthy studies on the origin of Hellenistic period
ceramics can be noted. For example, the surprising results on the origin of the Hellenistic and Roman Eastern Terra Sigillata ware, which indicated a Cypriote
origin for much of this pottery (Gunneweg et al., 1983;
but see Slane et al., 1994, who argue for a local Levantine origin for this, and related, wares), the unknown
origin of “braziers” from this period (Gunneweg and
Perlman, 1984) and pottery assemblages from specific
excavations (e.g., Ariel et al., 1985; Gunneweg and
Yellin, 1990, 1997).
roman–byzantine Period (ca. 66 bce–630 ce)
Roman–Byzantine Period ceramics have been a relatively major focus of the INAA studies conducted in
Israel over the last few decades. The various types of
ceramics studied have provided a wealth of archaeological and historical insights, and at the same time,
important methodological advances.
Ceramic types that are found throughout the Roman
Empire, including in the Land of Israel, were studied,
such as the Eastern Terra Sigillata ware (Gunneweg et
al., 1983) and trade amphorae (Gunneweg and Yellin,
1990). Similarly, the origin and trade of pottery groups
that were typical of certain cultures were also studied,
such as the “Nabataean fine wares” (Gunneweg et al.,
1991b), and the Judean “Pseudo-Nabataean” copies of
this ware (Gunneweg et al., 1986a).
At the same time, more specific pottery types
were also studied, such as examples from the pottery
assemblage at Qumran (Yellin et al., 2001; Gunneweg
and Balla, 2003; Michniewicz and Krzysko, 2003),
and Masada (Yellin, 1994), or specific types of pottery
from various sites, such as the “Herodian lamp”
(Gunneweg and Perlman, 1984–1985; Adan-Bayewitz
et al., 2008), or the “flower pots” from Herodian
Jericho (Yellin and Gunneweg, 1989a).
An extremely important contribution comes from
the local, “micro-provenience” studies that were
conducted primarily on Roman and Byzantine pottery
from the Galilee and the Golan (and Judea as well), in
which fine-tuned, local distribution patterns of various
types of pottery were discerned (e.g., Adan-Bayewitz
and Perlman, 1985, 1990; Adan-Bayewitz and Wieder,
1992; Adan-Bayewitz, 1993). These studies, which
combined INAA with petrography, micromorphology,
and XRF, convincingly demonstrated the powerful
analytic tools that INAA provides, not only for
questions of long-range, inter-regional connections,
but also for subtle, local studies as well, both relating
to differential distribution of specific types of vessels,
and aspects of intra-site spatial analysis of pottery
production.
Finally, in a recent provenience study of Early
Roman Period ceramics from Jerusalem and its vicinity,
in which INAA and High Precision X-ray Fluorescence
(HPXRF) were combined, Adan-Bayewitz et al. (2006)
report an unusually high concentration of silver in
the vessels originating from Jerusalem. This could be
indicative of the high level of economic activities in
Jerusalem during this period, associated with Herod’s
temple in the city.
Medieval Periods (post-630 ce)
Relatively few INAA provenience studies were
conducted on archaeological remains dating to the
medieval periods. Among the few that can be noted
are studies of fine table wares from the Crusader Pe-
riod Latin Kingdom of Jerusalem (Boas, 1994) and of
Crusader Period ceramics from Acre, in which INAA
and ICP were used in tandem (Waksman et al., 1997).
In addition one can note an unpublished study of the
Early Arab kilns from Bet She’an, conducted by G.
Shelach and J. Yellin.

its utility for fine-tuned, local provenience studies was
