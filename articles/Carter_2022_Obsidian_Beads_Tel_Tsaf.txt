The obsidian beads from Middle Chalcolithic Tel Tsaf (ca.
5,200–4,700 cal. BC), Jordan Valley, Israel: technology, provenance,
and socio‑economic significance
Danny Rosenberg1 · Branden Cesare Rizzuto 2 · Florian Klimscha3 · Tristan Carter 4
Received: 3 December 2021 / Accepted: 28 April 2022
© The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer-Verlag GmbH Germany, part of Springer Nature 2022
Abstract
It has long been recognized that the use of beads for bodily adornment was a powerful means of creating and expressing
social identities. Their symbolic significance and meaning-making lies in the intersection of how and where they are worn,
their life histories of production and ownership, the time and skill invested in their manufacture, plus the cosmological import
and values accorded their raw materials. While obsidian is known to have traveled to the southern Levant from various
Anatolian sources since the Epipalaeolithic period, its use to make beads is rare. Over eight seasons, excavations at Tel Tsaf
(ca. 5,200–4,700 cal. BC) in the Jordan Valley, Israel, have produced the richest obsidian bead assemblage in the southern
Levant, part of a larger set of objects, and raw materials that attest to this Middle Chalcolithic community’s participation
in long-distance exchange networks. This paper details the obsidian bead assemblage, its morphometric and technological
characteristics, and raw material sources based on their chemical compositions. It then discusses the assemblage’s broader
socio-economic significance, and the possible means through which members of the community came to procure them.
Keywords Obsidian beads · Chalcolithic · Tel Tsaf · Near East · Trade and Exchange
Introduction
In this paper, we detail the obsidian beads found at Middle Chalcolithic Tel Tsaf, the largest assemblage currently
known from the southern Levant. Methodologically, we
characterize the assemblage through reference to the beads’
techno-typological specificities, and the source of the obsidian employed to make them, as determined by a non-destructive elemental analysis using X-ray fluorescence (XRF) techniques, and report on comparanda from sites elsewhere in
the southern Levant, and beyond. The study’s primary aim
is to employ the beads as a means of further understanding the complexity of the obsidian trade and exchange networks connecting certain southern Levantine communities
with distant locales to the north; a fuller discussion on the
significance of the beads, vis-à-vis, their role in adornment
practices, and the creation and expression of social identities
will be presented elsewhere.
Over the past 50-plus years (since Cann and Renfrew
1964), obsidian sourcing studies have comprised a primary means of detailing long-distance trade and exchange
between Anatolian, Mesopotamian, and southern Levantine
populations in later prehistory (Ibanez et al. 2015, 2016;

Levantine sites from the closest geological sources of obsidian in Anatolia, ca. 1000 km to the north (Fig. 1), obsidian artefacts found in this region are frequently regarded

pan-regional socio-economics (Carter 2011; Garfinkel 2011;
Healey 2007, 2013). The procurement of obsidian by southern Levantine communities is also believed to have occurred
in tandem with the movement of other goods from the north
(e.g., Getzov 2011; Rosenberg et al. 2010; Streit 2020; Streit
and Garfinkel 2015).
Modelling the exchange of obsidian to the southern
Levant is challenging due to its multi-layer complexity.
Fundamental issues concerning the movement of obsidian
in the greater region were first discussed in the 1960s by
Cann and Renfrew (1964; see also Renfrew et al. 1966,
1968), who posited a ‘down-the-line’ model to explain
the apparent decrease in obsidian quantity as the distance
from the source grows, where goods are exchanged with
neighbouring groups of roughly equal status through a
variety of configurations that are essentially reciprocal.
Alternative hypotheses have also been forwarded, invoking
the circulation of obsidian via itinerant specialists (e.g.,
Binder 2002), or the existence of complex multi-nodal
exchange networks (Ibáñez et al. 2015). More recent studies of southern Levantine obsidian assemblages have considered in greater detail the means by which different raw
materials were circulating, and the uneven distribution of
source products and manufacturing activities amongst the
later prehistoric communities of the region. Such work
has indicated clearly (a) diachronic shifts in which raw
materials were being circulated, (b) differences in how
specific source products were circulating (e.g., preformed
cores, or ready-made blades), (c) that certain populations
had greater access to different obsidians, and likely acted
as regional intermediaries for these resources’ distribution (e.g., Carter et al. 2017, 2020; Garfinkel 2008, 2011;
Gopher et al. 1998, 2011; Ibánez et al. 2015; Ortega et al.
2014). One of the key discoveries was that different raw
materials were in circulation between the Pre-Pottery Neolithic and the Late Pottery Neolithic/Early Chalcolithic
periods, with Central Anatolia as the dominant source
region represented in the former period, while in the latter
most obsidian originated from eastern Anatolia (Campbell

A tiny amount of obsidian is first documented in the
Levant in the Upper Palaeolithic (Frahm and Hauck 2017;
Shimelmitz et al. 2018). Thereafter the amount of this exotic
material being procured by southern Levantine populations
slowly increases, reaching its peak during the Pre-Pottery
Neolithic period (Garfinkel 2011), followed by a gradual
decline in the use of obsidian from the Pottery Neolithic
onwards (e.g., Carter et al. 2017; Delerue 2007; Delerue
et al. 2012; Garfinkel 2008, 2011; Gopher et al. 1998, 2011;
Prausnitz 1969; Schechter et al. 2013, 2016; Perlman and
Yellin 1980; Rosen et al. 2005; Yellin et al. 1996; Yellin and
Frachtenberg 1992).
It is in the Chalcolithic, however, that we see the greatest
diversity of obsidian source products in southern Levantine
assemblages, with an expanded range of Cappadocian and
eastern Anatolian raw materials in circulation, together with
products from the Caucasus (Campbell and Healey 2018;
Frahm et al. 2016). This is best evidenced at ‘Ein el-Jarba
and Hagoshrim (Carter et al. 2020; Schechter et al. 2013),
the former scientifically shown to have obsidian from Göllü
Dağ and Nenezi Dağ (Cappadocia), Bingöl B, Gürgürbaba
Tepe, and Nemrut Dağ (eastern Anatolia), Pasinler-Eksisu,
and Syunik-Satanakar (Caucasus), plus the ‘Group 3d’
source of unknown origin.
These long-term shifts in southern Levantine raw material
procurement traditions form part of a supra-regional reconfiguration in raw material choices, modes of consumption,
and associated value regimes. In the seventh millennium
cal. BC of central Anatolia, we see the first use of obsidian
to make mirrors (Guilbeau in press; Vedder 2005), while
in northern Mesopotamia of the sixth millennium cal. BC,
we start to see not only the manufacture of tools and mirrors, but also vessels, and a range of adornments, such as
beads and pendants (Campbell and Healey 2018; Healey
and Campbell 2014).
Throughout the Late Epipalaeolithic, Neolithic, and Chalcolithic periods, it is notable how certain sites have produced considerably larger obsidian assemblages than their
contemporaries, and these, including Eynan (Delerue and
Poupeau 2007; Khalaily and Valla 2013), Jericho (Gopher
et al. 1998), Motza (Khalaily et al. 2007), Nahal Lavan 109
(Burian and Friedman 1988; Burian et al. 1999), Beisamoun
(Rosenberg 2010), Hagoshrim (Gopher et al. 2011), and ‘Ein
Zippori (Schechter et al. 2016). The size and structure of
these sites’ assemblages clearly reflect a greater complexity
of obsidian exchange and distribution within the southern
Levant than the down-the-line model posited by Renfrew
and colleagues in the 1960s. Of conceptual pertinence to
this paper, is the fact that from at least the sixth-millennium
cal. BC onwards, we witness the emergence of a few communities in northern Mesopotamia that appear to have both
acted as major hubs of procuring obsidian from an array of
sources from central and eastern Anatolia, and the Caucasus,
and as centres where skilled craftspeople transformed some
of these raw materials into adornments, and vessels (Healey
and Campbell 2014).
Our focus in this study is the use of obsidian in the
southern Levant during the Chalcolithic period. At this
time, obsidian from various sources is found in the form
of tool production waste, blanks, and end-products (mainly
fashioned from flakes and bladelets), plus a few items of
adornment (e.g., Schechter et al. 2013: Fig. 11, 2016) and
other elaborate objects, the most famous being a mirror from
Kabri (Prausnitz 1969; Stekelis 1958). It is the subject of
adornments, specifically beads, that concerns us here.
Beads, a form of material culture that were designed to
convey information about their manufacturers, owners, and
their society (e.g., DeCorse et al. 2003; Dubin 2009; Mellaart 1964, 1967; Sciama and Eicher 2001; Wright et al.
2008), have been produced by humans for more than a hundred thousand years (Bar-Yosef Mayer 2005; Vanhaeren
et al. 2006). While personal adornments are often considered
markers of identity and social status (Costin Wright Brumfiel 1998; White 2007; Wright and Garrard 2003), the specific use of obsidian to make beads in the southern Levant
may have provided the objects with additional magical, apotropaic, and/or religious significance (Cauvin 1998), in part
due to the material’s reflective qualities, rarity and distant
origin (cf. Decourt 1998; Helms 1988; Saunders 2001).
The background to Tel Tsaf
Tel Tsaf is situated in the Jordan Valley, near the modern city of Beit Shean (Fig. 1). The site was discovered in
the 1940s and has been excavated by several expeditions
since the late 1970s (Garfinkel et al. 2007, 2009; Gophna
and Sadeh 1988–1989; Tzori 1958). The current project,
initiated in 2013, has focused on various aspects of longdistance contacts, the community’s economy and social
organization, and ecology (Rosenberg and Klimscha 2018;
Rosenberg et al. 2017b, a). Tel Tsaf is a large site of ca. 5 ha
(Figs. 2 and 3) established over three low hills and the area
around them (Garfinkel et al. 2009; Horn et al. 2019), its
occupation spanning ca. 5,200–4,700 cal. BC (Rosenberg
et al. 2014; Streit and Garfinkel 2015). Several courtyard
buildings were exposed at the site (Garfinkel et al. 2009), the
structures built of sun-dried mudbricks that were coated with
plaster (Rosenberg et al. 2020). Excavations also revealed
many silos reflecting large-scale storage, plus roasting pits
that were likely related to feasting events (Ben-Shlomo et al.
2012; Garfinkel et al. 2007, 2009; Rosenberg et al. 2017b,
a). The faunal assemblage is large, diverse, and comprised of
mainly domesticated animals (Hill 2011), while the botanic
Page 3 of 21 113Archaeological and Anthropological Sciences (2022) 14: 1131 3remains (Graham 2014; Rosenberg et al. 2014) attest to the
earliest evidence for the Mediterranean diet in the Jordan
Valley (Rosenberg and Klimscha 2018, 2021; Rosenberg
et al. 2021).
Among the numerous finds from Tel Tsaf are some that
demonstrate the community’s participation in far-reaching
exchange networks, with evidence for connections with
Transjordan (beads), Egypt (beads, Nilotic shells), Anatolia
(obsidian), an unknown copper source in the north (the earliest evidence for smelted copper in the southern Levant [Garfinkel et al. 2014]), Mesopotamia (Ubaid style pottery and
figurines) and the Mediterranean (seashells). Notable is the
massive bead assemblage that consists of over 2,500 ostrich
eggshell beads (Garfinkel et al. 2009), dozens of beads made
from mostly non-local rocks and minerals (Silvain 2012),
clay, bone, and wooden beads, Theodoxus Jordani shell
beads (Rosenberg et al. 2022), plus the obsidian beads that
are the focus of this paper.
The Tel Tsaf obsidian beads
Twenty obsidian beads (Tables 1, 2, and 3, Fig. 4) have been
retrieved during the new Tel Tsaf excavations (Rosenberg
et al. 2014, 2021; Rosenberg and Klimscha 2018). Ten came
from Room C70, three in Room C377, and the rest in various
fills and pits. Worth mentioning are the eight beads found
in Locus 972, a surface/floor preserved in the northern part
of Room C70 (Fig. 5) that were probably part of a single
string that made up a bracelet, or necklace, one of several
unique finds from this context, attesting to the room’s socioeconomic significance (e.g., Rosenberg et al. 2017b, a).

Several attributes were recorded for the Tel Tsaf obsidian
beads (Tables 2 and 3). While obsidian knappers and artisans are known to have had the knowledge and expertise to
produce highly symmetrical and meticulously made objects
(Healey and Campbell 2014), most of the Tel Tsaf obsidian
beads are not perfectly round. This is interesting, given that
producing the shaft is a much more demanding task than
producing a round bead (making the bead requires much
effort but the artisan has a larger margin of error compared
to the production of the shafts). The production of obsidian
beads is difficult because the bead and aperture are small,
and the material is both hard to polish, while simultaneously
easy to fracture (see also Belcher 2011; Moorey 1999).
The Tel Tsaf beads are mostly made of black or blackgrey obsidian, while two have white spherulites (#12 and
#18). Most are intact (Table 2), and it seems that their imperfect symmetry did not have any notable effect on the beads’
use (most scars were smoothed over). Two are broken across
the perforation; these beads are apparently cruder, and it is
possible that after a rough preparation of the bead preform,
the hole was made.

The perforations (Table 3, Fig. 6) are in most cases round
and symmetrical, suggesting that they had been drilled
(probably with a bow-drill), even when there are no visible drill marks. Sometimes the shafts were made off-centre.
Pecking marks are still noted in the shaft’s opening, suggesting that pecking was used in an early stage of the boring
sequence, as with the Late Chalcolithic perforated flint discs
(Rosenberg et al. 2017a). The shaft was either initiated from
both faces (bidirectional) or from one (unifacially); however, directionality is not always clear. The minimum diameters of the perforation range between 0.90 and 2.47 mm
and their maximum external diameters range between 1.01
and 2.88 mm for side A and between 1.10 and 2.99 mm
for side B (sides A and B were independently selected for
each bead). Some variations in the size of the apertures were
noted as well, although we did not observe very small apertures like those reported from ‘Ein Zippori (Schechter et al.
2016: 7:2, 7:4).

Obsidian characterization
The 12 obsidian beads were geochemically analyzed to
determine the origin of the raw materials used to fashion the objects. The beads were elementally characterized
using non-destructive X-ray fluorescence (XRF) techniques.
The artefacts were initially analyzed using energy dispersive XRF (EDXRF) at the McMaster Archaeological XRF
Laboratory (MAX Lab), which has experience characterizing several other obsidian assemblages from the southern
Levant and neighboring regions (e.g., Carter et al. 2017,
2020; Orange et al. 2019), and hosts North America’s largest collection of geo-referenced samples from the Anatolian
sources.
Unfortunately, the data generated by the EDXRF analyses
were problematic, with their elemental concentrations never
quite matching those produced by the same instrument and
under similar conditions as the source samples. These results
were not entirely unexpected as experimental analysis shows
that small and thin samples can cause issues for EDXRF (see
Davis et al. 2011). If the detector’s field of view extends
beyond the artefact’s margins, then the relative deficit in
X-rays from the object can impact measured elemental concentration values. The same is true for samples that are thinner than the critical thickness (≈4 mm) needed to prevent
the transmission of incident X-rays through the sample in a
manner that will reduce the X-ray fluorescence peak intensities associated with certain elements of interest (Davis et al.
2011). With all but one of the Tel Tsaf beads exhibiting
thicknesses less than 4.0 mm, most of the beads do not meet
the critical thickness thresholds typical of EDXRF analyses
of obsidian.
The Thermo Scientific ARL Quant’X EDXRF at the
MAX Lab is equipped with an X-ray collimator with a
diameter of 8.8 mm. A collimator is a device that narrows a beam of particles or waves. Thus, the X-ray beam
diameter is determined by the diameter of the collimator,
and the object analyzed needs to be larger than the beam;
otherwise, there is interference between the object and the
background of the instrument. This interference is negligible when the sample is larger and is the only material
excited by the incident X-ray beam. However, the Tel Tsaf
beads have a maximum external diameter of 8.3 mm, in
addition to having a perforation in their centers (Fig. 4).

Five repeat measurements were conducted on a single
spot using the same orientation for each of the 12 obsidian beads from Tel Tsaf as well as several samples each
from geological sources in Anatolia and the Caucasus that
were considered potential raw material sources based on the
logic detailed above: Bingöl A, Bingöl B, East Göllü Dağ,
Gürgürbaba Tepe/Muş, Nemrut Dağ, Pasinler, Sarakımış,
and Suphan Dağ. All measurements were taken in dry air
using a 40 kV tube voltage, a 30 μA beam current, a 90 s
acquisition time, and a metal filter composed of a 300 μm
layer of aluminum, a 25 μm layer of titanium, and a 150 μm
layer of copper. A pressed-powder pellet of RGM-2 (obsidian rhyolite) from the United States Geological Survey was
also analyzed under the same conditions at the beginning
and end of each run of measurements. This permitted instrument drift and other factors which impact quantification to
be monitored. To prevent contamination of the instrument’s
sample window during the analyses, a 2.5 μm thick film of
Mylar was positioned between the samples and the instrument’s sample window. Tests on internationally certified
reference standards (see below) demonstrate that the use of
Mylar does not significantly impact the accuracy of analyses
(see also Rennie 2019, Appendix A).

The only peralkaline sources in the larger region are in
eastern Anatolia, specifically the southern outcrops of the
Bingöl volcanic massif (‘Bingöl A’) and 150 km further to
the east at Nemrut Dağ on the western shore of Lake Van

Discussion
The earliest reference to obsidian beads from the larger
region comes from the Middle Pre-Pottery Neolithic B
site of Tell Halula in the Middle Euphrates. This is a single butterfly bead whose green color suggests that it was
made from one of the eastern Anatolian peralkaline sources:
Bingöl A, or Nemrut Dağ (Alarashi 2016: 496, Fig. 4c,
Table 2; Delerue 2007: 40). Obsidian beads (disc beads
and less so other types) appear subsequently at several sites
dated mainly to the Late Neolithic and Chalcolithic (e.g.,
Coşkunsu 2011; Healey 2007; Healey and Campbell 2014:
figs. 3:7–9, 4:6–8; Watson 1983: 239).
Obsidian disc beads first appear in the southern Levant
probably during the Early Chalcolithic period (however see
below), made of various Anatolian raw materials. However,
obsidian disc beads are rare even at sites where large obsidian
assemblages were found, such as Hagoshrim (Gopher et al.
1998 and 2011; Schechter et al. 2013), Beisamoun (Rosenberg

With 20 beads, the Tel Tsaf assemblage clearly stands out
in a southern Levantine context. It remains here to consider
the obsidians employed, and the mechanisms by which these
adornments and/or raw materials were procured and redistributed amongst the region’s populations during the Middle
Chalcolithic periods (ca. 5,200–4,700 cal. BC).
That the Tel Tsaf beads were shown to be mostly made
of obsidian from the East Göllü Dağ, Nemrut Dağ / Bingöl
A, and Bingöl B sources was not unexpected, with these
raw materials well attested at southern Levantine sites from
the later Neolithic/Early Chalcolithic (e.g. ‘Ein el-Jarba, see
Carter et al. 2020). Moreover, East Göllü Dağ obsidian is
known to have been used for bead manufacture in an earlier period, at Halafian Domuztepe in southeast Anatolia
(Healey and Campbell 2014), while Nemrut Dağ and Bingöl
B raw materials were similarly employed to make adornments (including necklace links) at Halafian Tel Arpachiyah in northern Mesopotamia (Healey and Campbell 2014:
Table 1). Conversely, Pasinler obsidian is much more rarely
documented in archaeological assemblages (Chataigner et al.
2014: 362), restricted largely to sites close to the source,
specifically Sos Hüyük (Late Chalcolithic – Iron Age,
see Sagona et al. 1996), plus Tepecik Koy 1 and Tepecik
2 (Bronze Age, see Brennan 2000). Quantities of Pasinler
obsidian have, however, been documented from Halaf-period
Domuztepe in southeast Anatolia, distinctive in their reddish
hue (Campbell and Healey 2018: 150; Healey and Campbell
2009: Table 1). Reddish obsidian—which comprises both
Pasinler and Arteni obsidian at Domuztepe—was used to
make not only blades and flakes but also vessels (Healey
and Campbell 2009).
While there is no clear evidence for beads made of Pasinler obsidian from elsewhere, our discoveries fit the larger
pattern of an expanded range of obsidian sources being used
from the later Neolithic onwards by Near Eastern communities (Campbell and Healey 2018; Carter et al. 2020). The
greater use of obsidian from the Caucasus (northeast Anatolia and Armenia) is a characteristic of this shift in raw
material procurement traditions, with obsidian from two
Syunik sources in Armenia being used to make beads at
Early Dynastic (ca. 2700 cal. BC) Kish in southern Mesopotamia (Campbell and Healey 2017).
The evidence currently suggests that Tel Tsaf inhabitants procured these beads ready-made, with the excavation
having produced no preforms or waste products from the
manufacture of such adornments. That said, a separate MAX
Lab characterization study of obsidian tools from Tel Tsaf
(from another project) has identified tools made of East
Göllü Dağ, and Nemrut Dağ raw materials, whereby the
community, in theory, had the blanks from which the beads
could have been fashioned.
The Tel Tsaf obsidian disc beads were mostly found
complete, though some are slightly damaged (it is unclear
if this was during production, use, or post-deposition). The
beads were found in various contexts and layers in Area C
(Table 1), mostly on surfaces, floors, pits or fills, open areas,
and rooms. In one case, eight were found together, probably
a part of a single string representing a bracelet, or necklace;
interestingly these beads were made of obsidian from three
sources: five from East Göllü Dağ, two from either Bingöl
A or Nemrut Dağ A, and one from Pasinler Tizgi.
The study by Healey and Campbell (2014) clearly demonstrates that the manufacture and use of obsidian adornments
is a phenomenon that took off during the Halaf period. This
is best attested at Tell Kurdu and Domuztepe, the latter having evidence for a bead workshop (Campbell 2005), that
used an array of raw materials, including obsidian; in both
instances, non-peralkaline obsidian seems to have been preferred (Healey 2007: 181, 2013: 254–257, figs. 22.3–22.5;
Healey and Campbell 2014: 83–84, figs. 4–5). Some of these
beads, or the idea behind their manufacture, then made their
way to the southern Levant, part of a range of Halafian connections witnessed at such Early Chalcolithic sites as ‘Ein
el-Jarba, Kabri, Hagoshrim, and ‘Ein Zippori (Carter et al.
2020: 16–18). We suggest that the bead evidence from Tel
Tsaf, and other later sites, indicates that these relationships
between southern Levantine communities, and those based
in the northern Levant and northern Mesopotamia continued
during the timespan of the Early Ubaid, at least until ca.
4,700 cal. BC.
Sites in this area to the north with beads and other ornaments of obsidian include (Healey and Campbell in press)
Arpachiyah (Healey and Campbell 2014), Mezraa Teleilat
(Coşkunsu 2008: 31, Özdoğan 2011: figs. 64–65), and Tell
al-Judaidah (Braidwood and Braidwood 1960: 252, 385,
Fig. 190.5; Rennie 2019: table 2.0). Other sites from the
‘traditional sphere of Halaf influence’ and beyond (southern Mesopotamia and the Gulf) that yielded obsidian beads
include Chagar Bazar, Choga Mami, Tepe Gawra, Tell Aqab,
Tell Halaf, Umm Qseir, and Yarim Tepe (Healey and Campbell 2014: 93–94, Table 1).
Tel Tsaf, at ca. 5,200–4,700 cal. BC, represents a later
period of obsidian bead use, whereby Early Ubaid assemblages comprise more chronologically pertinent comparanda. Healey (2010: 174) notes how, as with the preceding Halaf period, obsidian was used to make a range of
adornments and vessels in the Early Ubaid, with decorative
113 Page 16 of 21 Archaeological and Anthropological Sciences (2022) 14: 1131 3items comprising the sole use for this raw material at a
workshop in Tülintepe (Upper Euphrates), and as-Sabiyah
(Kuwait). We can also refer to obsidian beads from Early
Ubaid contexts that have been found at Tell Kurdu (Amuq
Valley), Ras Shamra (Levantine coast), Tell Kosak Shamali
(Upper Euphrates), Oueili (Iraq), Tell Songor (Iraq), and
Khor (Qatar) (see Healey 2010: table 12.4 for a summary).
Where reported, the obsidian used to make these adornments is described as grey and translucent (Coqueugniot
1998: 292; Healey 2010: 174), characteristics we associate
strongly with East Göllü Dağ obsidian. This is not to say
that all beads are made of these southern Cappadocian raw
materials—as there are other Caucasian obsidians that have
a similar appearance (e.g., some of the Sarakımış products),
though it may point towards a common aesthetic.
Conclusion
The circulation of obsidian in the prehistoric southern
Levant has received significant attention, with discussions
focusing on the mechanisms by which these exotic raw materials circulated, the ways in which they were worked, and
the socio-economic significance of their uneven distribution
amongst the region’s inhabitants (e.g., Carter et al. 2017,
2020; Garfinkel 2011; Gopher et al. 1998; Ibánez et al. 2015;
Ortega et al. 2014; Schechter et al. 2013, 2016 inter alia).
Given the large distances from the source to site and the
overall rarity of obsidian in the southern Levant (Garfinkel 2011: Table 1), it is often suggested that those artefacts
made from these raw materials may have often enjoyed a
symbolic, or prestigious significance for these populations,
rather than a quotidian resource for household activities
(Gopher et al. 1998: 646; see also Carter et al. 2017: 308,
2020: 16–18; Healey 2007, 2013), the objects potentially
having a key role in mediating social and economic relationships (Healey and Campbell 2009).
The use of obsidian for adornment may be the most obvious expression of these raw materials’ role in the creation,
maintenance, and expression of social identities, their distinct colors, reflectivity, and position on the wearer, a powerful means of distinguishing an individual from their peers,
most of whom—at Tel Tsaf—were presumably wearing the
far more common ostrich shell beads (Garfınkel et al. 2009).
One wonders if bead manufacture represented an emblem of
a particular community (or sodality within that population),
such as Domuztepe, or Hagoshrim for example, with a few
end-products then being gifted to (or exchanged with) members of other settlements as a means of brokering important
external relations, a form of bridewealth for example. The
exchange of obsidian beads may have taken place in more
restricted social spaces, amongst different types of people,
occurring as a more isolated phenomenon seen mainly in
‘islands’ or capsules of distinct cultural phenomena, such
as Tel Tsaf.
While obsidian beads may have enjoyed a different meaning and status from tools made from the same raw materials,
there is clearly a close relationship between the two sets of
material culture objects, given that the former were often
made from the latter. Similarly, while the exchange of beads
and tools may have occurred at different times, and places,
it remains that these objects both ultimately derived from
the movement of preformed and/or part-worked blade cores
from northern Levantine and/or northern Mesopotamian
communities (Carter et al. 2020). It is important to appreciate the commonalities and distinctions between adornments
and other types of objects, specifically tools and implements,
rather than taking a reductionist view that focuses primarily
on ‘obsidian’, the shared raw material.
The value accorded to the products of exotic materials
from distant locales (cf. Helms 1988) may have been at
least part of the motivation for obtaining or making obsidian beads. Furthermore, the properties of obsidian (reflectivity, iridescence, or ‘brilliance’) and its possible association
with divinity or magical properties (cf. Aufrère 2001: 160;
Coqueugniot 1998; Decourt 1998; Helms 1993: 150–152;
Saunders 2001) may have been instrumental factors that
supported their circulation and use. If the obsidian beads
portray non-functional qualities and significance, then the
obsidian beads from the Middle Chalcolithic site (contemporary with the Early Ubaid phase 3), represent tokens of
connectivity and communication between the northern and
southern Levant and northern Mesopotamia, which continued through the fifth-millennium cal. BC. This is not to say
that the objects were not necessarily produced at another
southern Levantine site, but more a matter of the manufacture and use of such adornments can be viewed as linking
certain members of the Tel Tsaf community with ideological
practices that emerged in northern regions in the preceding
millennium.
Summing up, beads are one of the most notable objects
associated with personal adornment, symbolic representation, and status (Baysal 2019). The recovery of a relatively
large number of obsidian beads at Tel Tsaf, known as a hub
for long-distance trade and pan-regional contacts, can thus
be viewed as a token of the complexity of the ties between
the southern Levant and communities to the north. We can
see the Tel Tsaf beads as deeply meaningful objects, with
those who wore them being entangled (cf. Hodder 2014) in
a web of interpersonal relations, exchange networks, skilled
technical practices, and value regimes. These adornments
are more likely to have been gifted in avowedly social settings (cf. Mauss 1950; Godelier 1999), rather than circulating via a simple economic exchange, though this may be to
forge a false dichotomy for the movement of exotica in the
Middle Chalcolithic.
Page 17 of 21 113Archaeological and Anthropological Sciences (2022) 14: 1131 3Arguably the Tel Tsaf obsidian beads formed part of a
system of social distinction (cf. Bourdieu 1987), yet another
reflection of emerging social complexity during the end of
the sixth- and early fifth-millennia cal. BC (Rosenberg and
Klimscha 2021), a process that continued to intensify during
the Late Chalcolithic period, with its lavish copper industry
(e.g., Bar-Adon 1980; Levy and Shalev 1989).
