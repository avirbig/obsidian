A comprehensive program is now under way for determining the points of origin of
obsidian artifacts and flakes found in Israel. The present paper is confined to material
obtained from six Neolithic sites; obsidians dated to later periods will not be discussed
here (see explanation below).
The chemical compositions of the obsidians were determined by neutron activation
analysis (NAA), from which abundance values were obtained for more than twenty
chemical elements. The system for carrying out this analysis is a modification of one
described by Perlman and Asaro.1 The chemical profile serves as a 'fingerprint' which
can be compared, and if possible matched, with profiles of obsidians drawn from dif
ferent obsidian sources.
The region closest to Israel where obsidian flows are known to exist is Anatolia; the
collection of source materials consequently began there.2 Anatolia has a considerable
number of sources, and since not all of them have been sampled, one would expect to
find compositions among the specimens of archaeological obsidians which cannot yet
be matched with their sources. The principal conclusion reached in this paper, however,
results from source-material analyses which have been carried out.

An examination of the data, element by element, shows that there is a very close
correspondence between the groups from Beisamoun and Nahal Lavan and that both
match the source group from G?ll? Dag. The data for the Nenezi Dag source are
presented in order to highlight the contrast in the compositions of two sources that hap

amined are equally well, or even more easily, differentiated.
In order to acquire some perspective on the ease of distinguishing between the
sources, one should note the values of iron, given as 0.607 ? 0.017 and 0.884 ? 0.010.
Superficially, the mean values 0.607 and 0.884 do not appear to vary greatly, but the
difference between them (0.277) must be compared with the spreads in the two groups.
Thus, the statistical odds that a member of one group can be distinguished from the
other, on the basis of this one element alone, are astronomical.

Two groups of obsidian in Table 1 came from Pottery Neolithic contexts: three sam

mentioned 'break', because G?ll? Dag continued to be a source of obsidian into the
Chalcolithic period.
Although plausible arguments have been presented supporting the theory that there
was a single obsidian source during the PPN periods, this has by no means been proved.
We have no direct evidence that the Beisamoun specimens not from G?ll? Dag are later
than PPNB, and it may well turn out that well-attested PPN materials not yet analysed
will have proveniences different from G?ll? Dag.
Finally, an explanation is in order as to why only a brief mention is made of the
results from Chalcolithic sites and no data are given. The analyses of materials from
these sites revealed the existence of 12 different compositions which almost certainly in
dicate the same number of different sources. Since up to now we have succeeded in ob
taining source materials to identify conclusively only six of these, it was not considered
worthwhile to present results based on material whose origins cannot be pinpointed. Of
the finds covered in the present report, only one piece (from Beisamoun) comes from an
unknown source.
Despite the uncertainty in asserting that there was a single obsidian source during the
PPN periods, it is still rather puzzling that for over 1500 years, so much of the obsidian
did originate from the very same source. It should furthermore be noted that other
sources of obsidian are located at no great distance from G?ll? Dag. Perhaps we shall
obtain a clue to this phenomenon when analyses are made on obsidian of the same
period excavated from archaeological sites closer to the sources; that is, on PPN
material from Anatolia. Unfortunately, existing analytical work relevant to this subject
has been carried out by methods that are inadequate for making fine distinctions, and
we have too few specimens of source material and archaeological samples to reveal a
comprehensive picture.15

Turning now to the question of the nature of the obsidian 'trade'16, as reflected in the
sites under discussion, there is no evidence indicating that obsidian tools were fashioned
at these PPN sites in Israel. No cores have been found, and no reports have been
received of the discovery of the copious minute chips that provide a tell-tale sign of obsi
dian working. Larger flakes found in surface collections or by coarse sieving perhaps
arose from the breakage of implements. Until evidence to the contrary appears, we must
therefore not consider the obsidian trade in this period as a brisk traffic in the raw
material but rather as a modest trade involving the finished tools.17
How modest this traffic was is difficult to assess. The 62 pieces that we studied must
constitute only a very small fraction of the imports. Many times this number have
already come to light, and this must be multiplied by an unknown factor representing
those not yet uncovered in the habitation sites, those from sites not yet discovered, and
those which were dispersed in the surrounding countryside where they will never be en
countered except by chance. That this trade covered a long time span cannot be dis
puted. However, much more extensive and meticulous stratigraphie work must be
carried out before we can establish whether this trade was intermittent or sustained.
