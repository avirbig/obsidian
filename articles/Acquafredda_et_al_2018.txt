The X-ray fluorescence technique using peak intensity ratios of trace elements is one of the faster, less
expensive and less invasive methods for obsidian sourcing. Moreover, XRF peak intensity ratios are
particularly useful when the artifact is slightly altered, particularly affecting the alkali cations, or when its
surfaces are covered with a fine film of carbonate deposits formed while the sample was buried.
The limitation of this technique results from the dimensions of the obsidian samples: very small
obsidian samples, nowadays about 1 cm in size, are difficult to measure given the very few X-rays
emitted by their surface.
An aluminium holder closed with a very thin Mylar© polyester film was prepared and the specimen,
positioned at the centre of the surface of the Mylar film, was placed in the XRF spectrometer. The use of
recent technology XRF apparatus equipped with a 4 kW Rh anode X-ray tube coupled with an accurate
measurement of refined X-ray characteristic lines, that exclude the X-ray background contribution, of five
trace elements (Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Nb), made it possible to determine the source geological outcrop of
archaeological obsidian samples as little as 7 mm and as light as 40 mg.
A database of peak intensity ratios, background and interference free, of five trace elements was
produced for obsidian samples from Monte Arci, Palmarola, Lipari, Pantelleria, Gyali and Melos.
This database may be used by other laboratories provided the X-ray intensity of the trace elements Rb,
Sr, Y, Zr, Nb are measured as net intensities, that is purified from the contribution of the background and
from interelemental interferences.
A comparison between data obtained with two spectrometers, of different generation and differently
calibrated, confirmed the possibility of obtaining a database that can be shared with other laboratories.
The proposed XRF method was tested on Neolithic obsidians from four Apulian sites (Ripa Tetta at
Lucera, Palestra ex G.I.L. at Foggia, Pulo di Molfetta and Grotta di Santa Barbara at Polignano a Mare),
whose source outcrops had been also determined by SEM-EDS.

1. Introduction
The characterisation of obsidian can provide important information on cultural, social and economic relationships between
peoples in Mediterranean prehistory. For this reason, the study of
obsidian distribution has provided a well-defined outline of the
circulation of Mediterranean sources (Monte Arci, Palmarola, Lipari,
Pantelleria, Gyali and Melos) in the past. The identification of
obsidian sources is, of course, only the first step needed to study its
circulation: it is also essential to study the forms in which the
material moved from sources to sites and between sites in exchange networks, the quantity of obsidian used, the reduction
technology and the use-wear of obsidian artifacts. There is still
debate over the economic versus prestige value of the quantitatively very limited amounts of obsidian in many Neolithic contexts.
The provenance of obsidian artifacts can be determined using
numerous minimally destructive or non-destructive methods such
as laser ablation inductively coupled plasma-mass spectrometry
(Gratuze et al., 2001), electron-probe microanalysis (Tykot, 2002),

In particular the XRF peak intensity ratios technique is particularly useful when the artifact is slightly altered or covered by thin
carbonate encrustations, if compared with other surface analysis
technique like laser ablation inductively coupled plasma-mass
spectrometry (LA-ICP MS), Electron Probe MicroAnalysis (EPMA)
and SEM-EDS; in fact the XRF peak intensity ratios technique,
measuring emitting lines of heavy elements such us Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and
Nb, is less affected by analytical inaccuracies due to the chemical
contribution of the surface of the samples (Acquafredda et al.,
2013).
The aim of this research is to evaluate the possibility to refine
the wavelength dispersive XRF (WD-XRF) measures reviewing the
ED and WD methods proposed in the literature (Nelson et al., 1975;
De Francesco et al., 2008) and suggesting a non-destructive WDXRF procedure capable of producing a database shareable by
several laboratories, suitable for obsidian provenance information.
Obsidian samples to test the WD-XRF procedures were collected
at Monte Arci in Sardinia (Perdas Urias, Conca Cannas, Canale Perdera and Riu Solacera), Palmarola, Lipari, Pantelleria (Bagno di
Venere, Salto la Vecchia and Balata dei Turchi), Gyali and Melos
(Nihia, Adamas, Sarakiniko and Demenegaki) (Fig. 1); a detailed
description of the geological obsidian outcrops is found in
Acquafredda et al. (1999).
2. The XRF technique
Chemical quantitative analyses with a WD-XRF spectrometer
must satisfy Bragg's law (2dsinq ¼ nl); this means that the WD-XRF
spectrometers require that 1) the specimen, 2) the diffracting
crystal, and 3) the detector must all lie, at varying values of q value
(q is the rotation angle for the spectral scansion), on the same
focusing circle (Rowland's circle). With the geometry of a not fully
focused WD spectrometer, Bragg's law is satisfied using primary
and secondary collimators that focus the X-ray respectively on the
diffracting (dispersive) crystal and on the detector during the 2q
rotation of the goniometer; with the geometry of a fully focused
WD spectrometer, Bragg's law is satisfied using only a few slits, in
place of the focusing collimators: in this case, during the 2q rotation
of the goniometer, the instrument rotates and simultaneously
moves both the diffracting crystal and the detector along Roland's
circle.
Not fully focused WD spectrometers are normally used for bulk
analyses of the specimen (as powdered and pressed samples,
melted disk of powdered rocks, ...) while fully focused WD spectrometers are mounted on EPMA or on SEM. Both not fully focused
and fully focused geometries require the sample surface to be
perfectly flat; they can supply reproducible results only if X-ray
peak intensity ratios of contiguous elements are used as substitutes
for the elemental concentrations as in the case of the obsidian
source area determination (De Francesco et al., 2008, 2011).
During WD analyses, if the surface of the specimen is not
perfectly flat, as in the case of archaeological obsidian samples, it is
impossible to obtain quantitative chemical data; in this case, the Xray intensity of the signal also depends, from sample to sample and
between different samples, on the angle between the direction of
the X-ray source (normally the X-ray tube) and the complexity of
the surface of the specimen and simultaneously on the angle between the surface of the specimen and the direction of the detector.
One way of minimizing this surface effect is to detect the X-ray
fluorescence of chemical elements that are contiguous in the Periodic Table: regardless of the complexity of the surface, the X-ray
intensity ratio between two very close chemical elements will
remain practically identical. This does not allow for the calculation
of the concentrations of elements, but does tell us the precise intensity ratios of their X-ray lines if they are contiguous in the Periodic Table. This is what Nelson et al. (1975) proposed to
characterize the obsidian of the Pacific Northwest coast (USA),
employing an ED Silicon Lithium X-ray detector.

raw intensity data are collected with no calibration to concentrations. Moreover, to identify obsidian provenance, the XRF equipment, depending also on its exciting X-ray tube, is particularly
effective at revealing some incompatible elements, the “mid-Z”
trace elements (i.e. Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Nb); these elements accurately
characterize igneous rocks since their concentration varies between volcanic sources (Nelson et al., 1975; Forster and Grave,
2012; Chataigner and Gratuze, 2014a; Frahm, 2014).
The major analytical problems related to the geometry of the
sample can be partly resolved by using an ED spectrometer instead
of a WD spectrometer. Using the ED spectrometer no account is
taken of Bragg's law because there is no moving component during
the analysis and the dispersion of the X-ray is done in energy and
not in the wavelength. Moreover, for an ED spectrometer the total
solid angle within which the X-rays are collected is much greater
than for the WD spectrometer; additionally, the ED detector does
not require critical positioning with respect to the source of the Xrays (a geometry non perfectly focused on a Roland's circle) making
it preferable for the analysis of samples that are not perfectly flat
especially during SEM microanalyses (Ruste, 1979; Acquafredda
and Paglionico, 2004).
This is why, in recent years, the archaeometric use of ED X-ray
spectrometers (Phillips and Speakman, 2009; Nazaroff et al., 2010;
Shackley, 2012; Forster and Grave, 2012; Millhauser et al., 2011;
Frahm et al., 2014a,b; Conrey et al., 2014; Milic, 2014), that is
portable X-ray Fluorescence (pXRF, Milic 2014), Field-portable Xray Fluorescence (FpXRF) and Hand Held portable X-ray Fluorescence (HHpXRF, Frahm et al., 2014a), and especially the apparatus
equipped with the new Silicon Drift Detectors (SDD), has offered
new potential for very rapid obsidian source characterisation.
The potential for executing rapid and easy ED analyses, particularly useful in the training of students (Shackley, 2012), that are
sufficient for initially sorting artifacts, must not rule out combining
these cost-effective and non-destructive techniques with other
effective types of analysis (Chataigner and Gratuze, 2014a,b; Frahm,
2014); Neutron Activation Analysis (NAA), LA-ICP MS, SEM with ED
Spectrometers and whole sample WD-XRF have been shown to
present more instrumental precision and to provide more secure
source and sub-source attribution.
Finally, the WD-XRF technique is one of the most accurate and
simplest means of analysing rock specimens, and many research
laboratories that characterize geo-archaeological samples already
own a WD-XRF apparatus (Conrey et al., 2014).
3. The use of the WD-XRF spectrometer
The X-ray fluorescence technique using peak intensity ratios of
trace elements is very fast, taking about 20 min per sample (a total
time of 23 min: 21 min for the XRF analysis þ2 min per sample for
the operator to position the samples and start up the spectrometer), reproducible, accurate, inexpensive, non-destructive and
efficient to use because XRF spectrometers are commonly found in
research laboratories.
Fundamentally, the procedures involved in this technique present three problems:
1) the rapid positioning of the sample in the spectrometer sample
holder;
2) the potential for creating a shareable database of use not only
for the research laboratory that standardizes the XRF spectrometer but also for other laboratories;
3) the dimension of the obsidian samples to be characterized.

samples larger than one centimetre but they do limit the power
supply of the X-ray tube because excessively high temperatures
may deform the plexiglas.
Another possibility is to attach the specimen with a sticker to a
sample support that can then be placed in the specimen carrier (De
Francesco et al., 2008, 2011); in this case, too, the high temperatures reached during the analyses may deteriorate the adhesive,
usually double-sided tape, causing the obsidian to fall onto the
head of the X-ray tube or in any case inside the spectrometer. On
the other hand, the use of particularly effective adhesives for
bonding the sample to the support makes them difficult to remove
after the analysis: this procedure affects the time taken to perform
the analysis and may damage particularly fragile samples.
Finally, we tested the possibility of placing the specimens into
an aluminium holder closed at the bottom with a very thin Mylar©
polyester film. The Mylar film is almost transparent to the incoming
X-ray of the tube and above all to the penetrative wavelength of the
Ka lines exiting the sample, like that of the elements Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Nb.
Under these conditions, signal loss due to the Mylar film is
imperceptible and the specimen seems to be suspended on the
head of the tube; moreover the noise contribution at X-ray spectrum due to the support is negligible compared with other
aluminium, steel or plexiglas holders, increasing the potential for
effectively detecting the X-ray of the trace elements present in the
obsidian. Finally, even if the obsidian specimen is very small, it can
be quickly and easily positioned at the centre of the surface of the
Mylar film (Fig. 3) for subsequent WD-XRF analysis.
3.2. Production of a shareable database
It is possible to create a database that is also helpful for other
laboratories as long as the data are collected as “net peaks” (total Xray emission peak minus the background) of the trace elements Rb,
Sr, Y, Zr, Nb. In the emission region of these trace elements the
background trend is not linear; its shape, depending on the matrix
of the sample and the anode (Ag, Rh, W, ...) of the X-ray tube, can be
expressed by an exponential function (Leoni and Saitta, 1976). The
intensity IB of the background can be calculated as:
IB ¼ eal þ b
where l is the wavelength corresponding to the angular position of
the goniometer (2q values), and “a” and “b” are two constants that
depend on the instrumental conditions and on the composition of
the sample. In the case of Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Nb at least six sampling points
of the background must be carefully chosen and measured in the
wavelength frequency range from 0.073 to 0.095 nm (0.73 ÷ 0.95 Å)
so as to be able to calculate their values below the peak position of
the trace element (Fig. 4).
Moreover, an accurate measure of these elements must take into
account the overlap of the interfering lines and the possibility that
these trace elements may be present as impurities in the target of
the X-ray tube or in the material of the X-ray spectrometer cabinet.
To measure the trace elements Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Nb one must take into
account that the Y Ka1,2 has, as interfering line, the Rb Kb1, that the
Zr Ka1,2 has, as interfering line, the Sr Kb1 and that the Nb Ka1,2 has,
as interfering line, the Y Kb1 (Fig. 4).
In Nelson et al. (1975) these interferences were signalled and in
the proposed method the X-ray ratio was given as the sum of two
intensities, probably due to the difficulties of a severe mathematical
correction of these overlaps with data collected using an ED X-ray
detector. WD spectrometer analysis allows for a complete

Other parameters that may influence the X-ray intensity ratio of
the Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb emission lines are: a) the type of diffracting
crystal, b) the target of the X-ray tube and c) the power supply, in
particular the kV, of the X-ray tube.
These three different points will be discussed in greater detail
below:
a) The LiF 220 is the most common crystal used to detect
numerous trace elements (Leoni and Saitta, 1976); moreover, it
would be senseless to use a diffracting crystal different from the
LiF 220 to detect Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb because this diffracting
crystal, whose 2d lattice distance is 2.848 Å, is that which offers
a better dispersion of the Ka wavelength of these trace elements
without a significant signal loss; in fact the use of a crystal with a
lattice distance greater than 2.8 Å (LiF 200, PET ...), leads to a
strong overlap of the Ka lines of Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb with the
consequence that is impossible to detect correctly the exponential background (Leoni and Saitta, 1976); the use of a diffracting crystal with a lattice distance smaller than 2.8 Å, like the
LiF 420, leads to a significant X-ray signal loss.
b) Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb are normally detected using an Ag (Leoni and
Saitta, 1976) or Rh X-ray tube: in fact the spectral X-ray emission
of these two targets, characteristic lines and bremsstralung, is
very similar and represents the better condition to excite the Xray emission of these elements; on the other hand, new WD
spectrometers are sold with an Rh tube that is considered a
"general purpose X-ray tube". To test the proposition that WDXRF spectrometers, equipped with different X-ray tubes, can
provide data useable in a shareable database, the peak intensity
ratios were also measured by interposing different filters between the tube and the sample: an Al 200 mm tick filter, an Al
750 mm tick filter and a brass 100 mm tick filter; the details will
be discussed later together with the problems related to
obsidian provenance.
c) To detect X-ray lines of Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb effectively, the Rh Xray tube must be powered to achieve its maximum performance; in the case of our spectrometer, the power supply parameters were set at 60 kV, 66 mA. In order to check the
potential for producing a shareable database, other different
power supply parameters were used, specifically: 60 kV, 40 mA
and 40 kV, 60 mA. The data from these tests will also be discussed later together with the problems related to obsidian
provenance.
Further WD-XRF spectrometer parameters, not discussed in this
paragraph (the distance from the X-ray tube and the sample, the
distance between the lamellae of the primary collimator, the use of
a secondary collimator and so on) only have a negligible influence
on the X-ray intensity ratio of the trace elements Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and
Nb.
During all the experiments presented in this paper, the X-ray
intensity of Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb were accurately measured and the
contribution of the exponential background mathematically
subtracted.
3.3. The dimension of the obsidian samples
Obsidian samples larger than 5.15 cm cannot be placed into the
sample holder of a standard WD labXRF spectrometer without
resizing the specimen in a disruptive way. If the specimen is smaller
than 5 cm, it can be positioned at the centre of the surface of the
Mylar film and then placed in the XRF spectrometer, even if the
obsidian sample is very small (Fig. 3).
The potential for characterising very small obsidian fragments,
given the very few X-rays emitted by small specimens, depends on
the ability to follow the procedures described above; good results
also depend on minimizing the distance between the X-ray tube
and the specimen, from the power supply of the X-ray tube and
finally undertaking an accurate energy discrimination of the elements. In addition, for extremely small specimens as tiny as
6.6  5.7  1.4 mm (39.8 mg, Fig. 3), to avoid displacement of the
obsidian from the centre of the sample holder during the analysis, it
was necessary to acquire the X-ray data with the sample spinner in
the off position. This dimension may also represent the lower limit
of the size of an obsidian sample containing glass and
microphenocrysts.
4. Characterisation of central-eastern Mediterranean
obsidians
The analyses were carried out at the Department of “Scienze
della Terra e Geoambientali” at Bari University where a welldefined outline of the circulation of various Mediterranean sources of obsidian was obtained by WD-XRF and by SEM-EDS
(Acquafredda et al., 1999; Acquafredda and Paglionico, 2004;
Acquafredda and Muntoni, 2005, 2008; Acquafredda et al., 2011,
2013), mainly concerning their distribution in southern and central Italian Neolithic sites. Regarding the four Sardinian obsidian
geological sources SA, SB1, SB2 and SC, only the two sources SA and
SC have been characterized in this research, which were the most
important, the most used and long distance traded during the
Neolithic period (Tykot, 2002; Luglie, 2012).
WD X-ray fluorescence analyses of 76 geological samples
collected at Monte Arci (25 samples), Palmarola (eight samples),
Lipari (ten samples), Pantelleria (six samples), Gyali (seven samples) and Melos (20 samples) were carried out with a spectrometer
(PANalytical AXIOS-Advanced) equipped with a 4 kW Rh Super
Sharp end window X-ray Tube (Rh-SST) set at 60 kV, 66 mA to
achieve maximum performance.
To better discriminate the Ka X-ray lines of trace elements such
as Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb, a LiF 220 (2.848 Å of 2d lattice distance)
diffracting crystal coupled with the “very fine” primary collimator
(parallel metal foil blades spaced 150 mm) were used; a scintillator
detector was selected to reveal the X-ray lines; measurement of
peak and background counting time was set at 100 s. The spectrometer set with these parameters made it possible to detect trace
elements like Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb, on powdered pellet samples, as
low as one part per million (ppm) with a repeatability that is of the
order of a fraction of ppm; such a high sensitivity allows us to
determine, in a completely non-destructive way, the provenance of
archaeological obsidian samples as small as 7 mm and as light as
40 mg (Fig. 3).

P. Acquafredda et al. / Quaternary International 468 (2018) 169e1781745. Tests of the ability to create a shared WD-XRF database for
obsidian samples
As argued before, the potential for creating a shared WD-XRF
database depends fundamentally on the analytical procedures
adopted to collect X-ray intensities. These are essentially:
1) the use of Mylar;
2) the target of the X-ray tube;
3) the power supply, kV and mA, of the X-ray tube;
4) the use of different spectrometers.
5.1. The use of Mylar
To verify the possibility of using a Mylar© polyester film during
the characterisation of obsidian artifacts, the powdered pellets of
Lipari rocks were re-analysed, covering their surface with a double
Mylar film. The X-ray intensity ratio obtained clearly showed that
the data obtained with and without the use of Mylar coincide
perfectly (Fig. 6a).
5.2. The target of the X-ray tube
Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb are normally detected using an Ag (Leoni and
Saitta, 1976) or Rh X-ray tube. To simulate the possibility of
changing the X-ray tube, powdered obsidian samples of the Mediterranean basin were also analysed using different filters; in
particular an Al 200 mm tick filter (for samples from Lipari only), an
Al 750 mm tick filter (for all the samples) and a brass 100 mm tick
filter (for all the samples). A rapid examination of the results (Fig. 6)
shows that the Al 200 mm tick and 750 mm tick filters have no in-
fluence on the X-ray intensity ratio whilst the brass 100 mm tick
filter appreciably shifts the ratios (Fig. 6a); the use of a log-log plot
(Fig. 6b), that minimizes non-significant differences, shows that
even using a 100 mm tick brass filter all the Mediterranean source
areas are clearly distinguishable.
5.3. The power supply, kV and mA, of the X-ray tube
Testing the variation of the power supply of the X-ray tube is
very important: according to Kramers’ law the intensity of the
bremsstralung depends strongly on the target, the current and the
voltage of the tube but its shape is largely voltage-controlled. This is
particularly important for the elements Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb because
their Ka emissions are on the descending slope of the bremsstralung of an Ag or Rh X-ray tube. The change in intensity and shape of
the bremsstralung can be easily calculated (Leoni and Saitta, 1976),
and consequently the background can be correctly subtracted.
There remains the problem that in the case of Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb
even a small variation in the exciting radiation, in this case mainly
the bremsstralung, could lead to a variation, for the same sample, in
their X-ray intensity ratio.
The powdered obsidian samples from the Mediterranean basin
were also measured using an X-ray tube power supply of 60 kV
40 mA (for samples from Lipari only) and of 40 kV 60 mA (for all the
samples). The latter condition is unusual because heavy elements
such as Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb are normally analysed with kV that are

5.4. The use of different spectrometers
Finally, the X-ray ratios presently measured using a Panalytical
AXIOS-Advanced spectrometer equipped with a 4 kW Rh end
window X-ray tube, were compared with the data obtained using a
Philips PW1480 automatic spectrometer equipped with a 3 kW Rh
side window X-ray tube (Acquafredda et al., 1999; De Francesco
et al., 2011).
The main differences between the two types of spectrometer
are:
i) the X-ray tube-sample distance (smaller in the case of the
AXIOS spectrometer);
ii) the distance between the lamellae of the primary collimator
(150 mm for the AXIOS spectrometer and 300 mm for the
PW1480 spectrometer);
iii) the X-ray tube power supply set at 60 kV, 66 mA for the
AXIOS spectrometer (this research) and at 80 kV, 35 mA for
the PW1480 spectrometer (Acquafredda et al., 1999);
iv) the different efficiencies of the scintillator detector.
The comparison of the data obtained with the two different
spectrometers (Fig. 7) clearly shows that there is a good fit between
data obtained with different instruments and also in different
research laboratories: evidently the distance from the X-ray tube
and the sample, the distance between the lamellae of the primary
collimator, and so on, only have a negligible influence on the X-ray
intensity ratio of the trace elements Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb.
6. A quick verification of the proposed method using Apulian
Neolithic obsidians
The proposed XRF method was tested by analyzing Neolithic
obsidians from the four Apulian sites of Ripa Tetta at Lucera (seven
samples), Palestra ex G.I.L. at Foggia (four samples), Pulo di Molfetta
(Acquafredda and Muntoni, 2008: five samples) and Grotta di Santa
Barbara at Polignano a Mare (Acquafredda and Larocca, 2017: one
sample) whose source outcrops were also determined by SEM-EDS:
the analytical results gave the same provenance indication using
the data obtained with SEM-EDS or XRF peak intensity ratios
(Fig. 8).
Four samples from Ripa Tetta, characterized with SEM-EDS,
have SiO2 and CaO contents that make it uncertain whether they
come from the Lipari or Monte Arci or Palmarola outcrops; this is
certainly due to the presence of a very fine carbonate encrustation
covering the surface of the samples (Acquafredda and Muntoni,
2008). To attribute them with certainty to one of the Mediterranean outcrops using the SEM-EDS technique, it is possible to submerge the specimen in cold acetic acid for at least 24 h to remove
the carbonate film (Acquafredda and Muntoni, 2008); after the
chemical attack with acetic acid the polished surface of the
obsidian can be easily re-analysed using ED microanalysis. The
uncertain attribution of these samples can also be resolved, again
with SEM-EDS, by analyzing the very tiny microphenocrysts present in the glass as suggested in Acquafredda and Muntoni (2008).
Alternatively, the provenance of the obsidian can be characterized
by WD-XRF using the X-ray intensity ratios of trace elements such
as Rb, Sr, Y, Zr and Nb (Fig. 8) avoiding any chemical treatment; in
fact the very fine carbonate films covering the specimen surface
absorb the intensity of these trace elements only minimally, and in
any case do not alter their ratio.
The two Neolithic sites, whose obsidian provenance data are
presented here for the first time (Ripa Tetta at Lucera, Palestra ex
G.I.L. at Foggia), are two of the ditched villages typical of the Early
and Middle Neolithic in the Apulian Tavoliere area (southern Italy).
The Tavoliere plain, the most extensive alluvial basin in southern
Italy (about 4600 km2 ), was densely settled in the Neolithic period
from 6200 to 4000 BC. During this time the plain was the scene of
systematic agricultural exploitation and animal husbandry.
Neolithic groups adapted their productive economy to the favourable and homogeneous environments provided by geology and
climate. These villages have one or more large, circular, often
concentric ditches enclosing inhabited areas subdivided by broad
empty spaces.
The Ripa Tetta village (Lucera, FG), one of these settlements, was
the object of regular excavations by the University of Pisa, Dipartimento di Scienze Archeologiche, from 1982 to 1992 (Tozzi, 2002).
The site was surrounded by one small circular ditch and was settled

Lipari appears to have been the main source of obsidian in the
central Mediterranean area and it clearly has the widest distribution throughout Italy. Only four Ripa Tetta obsidian samples came
from Palmarola. Our results, therefore, are consistent with those
observed at many other Early to Late Neolithic sites in southern
Italy: obsidian from Lipari is predominant, and sometimes the only
source present, in all Neolithic periods. Obsidian from the Aeolian
Archipelago was probably traded to the southern Italian peninsula
by sea and along inland routes. The presence of Palmarola obsidian
at Early Neolithic sites in the Tavoliere, such as Ripa Tetta, together
with artifacts from Lipari, suggest land-based and trans-Apennine
exchange networks from the Tyrrhenian coast to inland Irpinia and
then to the Tavoliere, the Ofanto Valley and the Murge Plateau
along the Adriatic coast.
7. Conclusion
The X-ray fluorescence technique using peak intensity ratios of
trace elements provides very good, reproducible, shareable and
cheap data for provenance information on obsidian samples,
especially in the case of slight alterations particularly affecting the
alkali cations of the artifact (Acquafredda and Muntoni, 2008;
Acquafredda et al., 2013). This analytical method is particularly
useful for obsidian samples covered by thin carbonate encrustations or for specimens whose surface, when very pore-rich, is
difficult to analyze with other methodologies like LA-ICP MS or
SEM-EDS (Acquafredda and Muntoni, 2008; Acquafredda et al.,
2013).
This technique is a very powerful tool for creating a shareable
database useful for Mediterranean obsidian provenance attribution
because it is very rapid (about 20 min per sample), reproducible,
accurate, inexpensive, non-destructive and easily used because XRF
equipment is fairly widespread in many research laboratories.
An accurate measurement of refined X-ray emission lines, that
exclude the X-ray background contribution, made it possible to
