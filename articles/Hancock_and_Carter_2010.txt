To assess the analytical accuracies and precisions of archaeometric elemental analyses by different
techniques, a relatively homogeneous material such as obsidian must be studied. An assessment of
published elemental concentration data from two Anatolian obsidian sources shows that while in most
cases analytical accuracy is as high as is commonly expected, in some cases it is not. It also shows that the
dispersions of elemental concentration data (indicators of analytical precisions) coming from modern
analytical procedures are akin to the estimated homogeneity of the obsidian. Based on this latter
observation, if one has element dispersion data from a single analytical technique, with a single source of
obsidian as a control, data sets that contain multiple, but similar sources of obsidian may be
differentiated.

1. Introduction
Readers of scientific papers tend to assume that elemental
analyses are as accurate and precise as is possible. But, all such
analyses are dependent on combinations of technique-, instrument-, and human-related factors that can lead occasionally to low
quality data (e.g. Fluegel, 2009).
To avoid data problems, scientists have produced standard
reference materials that may be used to help guarantee the accuracies of analyses from different laboratories (e.g. Glascock, 1999).
This approach has allowed data generated by a variety of analytical
techniques to be exchanged as seamlessly as possible. However,
a potential problem with the analysis of ‘‘standard’’ reference
materials is that much more care and effort may be applied to their
analysis than to the analyses of the archaeological or geological
materials that are the foci of these research projects.
To assess the accuracies of elemental analysis data in the
archaeometric literature, it is not appropriate to focus on materials
such as ancient and historic glasses and metals that have minor and
trace element homogeneities of around 10  20%, or more, much
like medium to coarse-ware ceramics. For these materials it is
difficult to separate the material sources of data dispersion from the
analytical sources. But, if one compares data from a homogeneous
material such as obsidian there is a better chance of confirming the
accuracies and precisions of analyses, especially when a specific
obsidian source, or flow, is studied.
While obsidian, or more specifically the products of a single flow
of obsidian, is usually described as a relatively homogeneous
material (e.g. Pollard and Heron, 2008; Kilikoglou et al., 1997), the
actual degree of homogeneity is usually not defined, although it is
noted that the partitioning of trace elements between the glassy
and crystalline phases may increase their heterogeneity (e.g.
Pollard and Heron, 2008). This means that on a fine scale, as is the
case for the distribution of Au particles in sediments or ceramics,
the concentrations of some trace elements may be much more
variable than expected, especially when small samples are
analysed.
The homogeneity of major, minor and trace elements in
obsidian is probably in the vicinity of 1% through to 6  10% at
most (M.D. Glascock, University of Missouri, personal communication 2007). This level of homogeneity is confirmed by Hughes
(1994) who reported results from the XRF analyses of 200

This presumably implies that multiple analyses of any obsidianforming flow in the world should produce data with similar
concentration dispersions. If this is so, then the finding of much
larger elemental concentration dispersions (e.g. Shackley, 1998,
2002), may imply chronologically different flows, or the occurrence
of other geological processes (Shackley, 2002).
Alongside the issue of obsidian’s homogeneity, one can further
note that the scattering of elemental concentration data tends to
follow the precisions of the individual concentration measurements (e.g. Hancock, 1982; Bishop and Blackman, 2002). As such, it
may be possible to infer the precisions of measurement for different
analytical techniques from the standard deviations of groups of
published elemental concentration means. The overall reproducibility of published data can thus be assessed by calculating the
averages of the group percentage errors, along with their associated
standard deviations.
The studies central to this paper generated data from analyses of
archaeological artifacts and source materials relating to the various
outcrops of the Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ massif and nearby Nenezi Dag ̆ in
southern Cappadocia, Turkey. The obsidians of these volcanoes
have been the subject of long-term scientific interest (cf. Renfrew
et al., 1966) as they represent two of the most archaeologically
important sources in the Eastern Mediterranean, their raw materials having been exploited for tool manufacture by prehistoric
communities in Anatolia, Cyprus and the Levant, from the EpiPalaeolithic until the Late Bronze Age (Chataigner, 1998;
Coqueugniot, 1998). Over the past 40 years, the elemental characterisation of obsidian from these sources has variously involved the
use of analytical techniques such as OES, XRF, ICP and INAA.
Theoretically, we can expect that improvements in analytical
techniques and instruments over time should result in less
dispersed concentration data for the analysis of a homogeneous
material such as obsidian. The more precisely we define an individual obsidian flow, the better the chance that we can discriminate
the products of different outcrops within a source area. This in turn
implies that if a data set from a single analytical technique exhibits
dispersions greater than expected for a single ‘source’ (a geochemical/archaeometric construct), then we may have evidence
that more than one outcrop (a geological phenomenon) is actually
represented.
Although analytical accuracy and precision are intimately
intertwined, points relating primarily to analytical accuracy will be
addressed first, followed by those relating to analytical precision (as
revealed by data dispersions). In this paper, it is assumed that the
numbers of significant digits published by authors reflected their
confidence in the relative quality of the concentration data for
different elements.
2. Chemical differences in obsidians
To establish the magnitudes of differences in elemental
concentrations reported by numerous researchers, we have chosen
to focus on data generated from the products of Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ and
Nenezi Dag ̆ in southern Cappadocia, just two of Anatolia’s
numerous obsidian bearing volcanoes (cf. Yellin, 1995; Poidevin,
1998).
The former massif is geologically complex, with a series of
outcrops whose products have been geochemically grouped by
Poidevin (1998: 115–122) into two compositional source groups,
namely: East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ (comprising the East Kayırlı, Komu ̈ rcu ̈ and
Sirça Deresi flows) and West Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ (comprising Go ̈ sterli,
Kayırlı-village and North Bozko ̈ y flows). More recent geo-archaeological and archaeometric studies have suggested that these two
source groups may eventually be further discriminated on the basis
of combined geochemical, geo-chronological and mineralogical
analyses (Gratuze et al., 2005; Poupeau et al., 2005). At Nenezi Dag ̆
the situation is quite different, since post-eruption landscape
processes have largely obscured the actual source(s). This means
that we have no idea as to how many outcrops were accessible
during prehistory (Poidevin, 1998).
Table 1 shows summary minor and trace elemental concentration data from Carter and Shackley (2007) for archaeological
obsidian samples associated with East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ and Nenezi Dag ̆.
Notwithstanding the relatively broad dispersions of the EDXRF data
(see Davis et al., 1998), specific elements analysed by the technique
(e.g. Sr, Zr, Zn, and Rb) clearly discriminates East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ from
Nenezi Dag ̆, making this a powerful, non-destructive, obsidiansorting technique.
Table 2 presents summary data calculated from Keller and Seifried (1990) for archaeological obsidian associated with Ko ̈ mu ̈ rcu ̈
(East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ - EGD) and Bozko ̈ y (West Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ – WGD). The
major element concentrations were generated by XRF and presented by the authors with 3–4 significant digits, while the minor
and trace element concentrations were presented with 1–3
significant digits. No concentration differences between the
obsidians from the two locations are seen for the major elements or
for seven minor and trace elements that were measured with
poorer precisions. So, out of the 15 elements whose concentrations
were reported, only four of the more-accurately-measured trace
elements show small (Rb, Sr and Zr) to large (Ba) concentration
differences.
Similarly, INAA data for 13 (the same ones reported by Keller and
Seifried, 1990 – see Table 2) out of 39 elements from Oddone et al.
(1997) that were used to generate Table 3 show similar effects. The
major elements are remarkably similar in concentration, while
some trace elements, especially Ba, show clear differences between
the obsidians of EGD and WGD.
Data in Tables 1–3 illustrate our point that the more precise the
analytical measurements (assuming that precisions of measurement follow data dispersions), the smaller the concentration
differences that may be differentiated.

On the other hand, Table 6 shows that a comparison of overlapping ICP data from Carter et al. (2006) with INAA data from
Oddone et al. (1997) for obsidian from Nenezi Dag ̆ yields more

(45 in the former, three in the latter), but this is probably not
a dominant factor. To date, and unlike the East and West Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆
obsidians whose heterogeneity reflects the different outcrops
subsumed within these compositional groups, the Nenezi Dag ̆ data
appear to be relatively homogeneous (Poidevin, 1998), suggesting
that the above analytical differences are a result of distinct
analytical techniques. Unfortunately the few fission track dates
associated with Nenezi Dag ̆ products do not offer any further
clarification as to the geological simplicity/complexity of the source
(Bigazzi et al., 1998). That said, one does risk a circular argument
here. In the future, if a single lab/technique study were to display
greater elemental data dispersion how should this be interpreted:
as a reflection of analytical inconsistency, or the first evidence of
more than one outcrop chemistry?

INAA produced low Sr, Ti and Zr. Could it be that at least some of
these elements are among the less precisely measureable elements
by their respective techniques?
4. Analytical precision and the group percentage
error approach to assessing data
As alluded to above, since the scattering of elemental concentration data tends to follow the precisions of the individual
concentration measurements (e.g. Hancock, 1982; Bishop and
Blackman, 2002), it may be possible to infer the precisions of
measurement of different elements for the analytical technique(s)
employed using the standard deviations of groups of literaturereported elemental concentration means.
Table 9 presents the relative group precisions for four XRFanalysed samples from the Ko ̈ mu ̈ rcu ̈ outcrop on East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆
from Keller and Seifried (1990). In this example, two elements (Si
and Al) cluster very tightly (better than 0.2% relative), possibly
reflecting their natural homogeneity, or perhaps indicating that
these data were normalised during the analytical process. Four
elements (Na, K, Rb, and Zr) cluster around the 1% mark, while
two elements (Sr and Ba) cluster around the 7% mark, their
dispersions fitting the proposal of Glascock (Personal communication 2007). Considering all eight elements, the average group
percentage error is 2.3% and the standard deviation (s) associated
with this average percentage error is 2.9%.
The group percentage error approach may not be a particularly
sensitive method for sorting out differences among data sets, since
a few poor sets of concentration measurements may bias the
generated data towards larger than expected values (see Table 10).
The resulting group percentage error (and associated standard
deviation) data are partially technique-specific, but may also be
illustrative of problems with the use of a particular analytical
instrument, with the inclusion of specific, less-well measured
elemental concentrations, or even with analytical error.
For all its potential failings, and although it was originally
intended to provide only a rapid assessment of published elemental
concentration data, the group percentage error approach might act
as a tool in locating lower than expected qualities of data as well as
in identifying previously unrecognized chemical groupings.

dispersed as are data generated by other techniques. This dispersion may be caused by variability in the diameters and thicknesses

analytical data (e.g. Ga, Nb, Th, and Y, see Table 1), as is clearly the

(1990) and from Poidevin (1998), where the removal of data from
one element caused a dramatic change in overall data dispersions
(see Table 10).

5.1. Use of percentage error information to uncover
new chemical groups
As is well known to analysts of obsidian, and confirmed here, the
dispersions of elemental concentrations found in obsidians tend to
be technique-dependent. So, within a data set from one technique,
and with a control group from an apparent single flow of obsidian
(e.g. possibly Nenezi Dag ̆), it may be possible to search for small
differences in obsidian chemistries by simply checking the
percentage errors of existing chemical groupings. Also, since Keller
and Seifried (1990) found only 4 out of 15 reported elements to be
diagnostic in sorting the obsidians from Ko ̈ mu ̈ rcu ̈ (EGD) and Bozko ̈ y (WGD - see Table 3), it may be that the number of diagnostic
elements that separate closely associated obsidians are a low
percentage (<25%) of the total number of elements analysed.
Presumably, if one analyses for more elements, then number of
potentially diagnostic elements will increase, resulting in them
being a higher or lower percentage of the total number analysed.
Of particular interest seems to be the variability of Ba concentrations reported by Carter et al. (2006), whose data set fulfils the
above requirements. While most of the other well measured
elements remained relatively consistent in their group percentage
errors, for both East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ and Nenezi Dag ̆ obsidians, the Ba
percentage errors varied by nearly a factor of two between them
(see Table 11). These data thus offer further confirmation of the
claim by Gratuze et al. (2005) that the ‘EGD’ source group of Poidevin (1998) does not do justice to the complexity of Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆’s
geology (see also Poupeau et al., 2005).
It should be noted that there is no such elemental concentration
dispersion difference in the Nenezi Dag ̆ obsidians. This tentatively
confirms that the Nenezi Dag ̆ obsidians analysed by Carter et al.
(2006) are relatively homogeneous and that they may come from
one obsidian flow.

slightly scattered data (Fig. 1) as proposed by Poupeau et al. (2005).

The low Ba concentrations in 12 of the 55 East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆
samples (Table 12) are possibly indicative of a second distinct
obsidian flow. If so, these two chemistries could represent two of
the ten East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ obsidian sources proposed by Gratuze et al.
(2005). Of these ten proposed sources, it is easier to say what these
two new chemistries are not. They are not from Sirça Deresi, since
the Ba and Sr concentrations are too high (cf. Poupeau et al., 2005).
If one applies a concentration data factor of about 1.2 to the LAICP-MS data of Gratuze et al. (2005) to bring them into line with the
ICP data of Carter et al. (2006), then the closest matches are with
obsidians from Kaletepe for the high-Ba samples, and with Birtlikeler and Ekinlik for the 12 low-Ba samples.
To add to the complexity of this data interpretation, the low Ba
group is directly comparable with the East Go ̈ llu ̈ Dag ̆ – Ko ̈ mu ̈ rcu ̈
obsidian, with no calibration corrections. If this observation is
reasonable then the high-Ba group of Carter et al. (2006) becomes
a new chemical group to add to the ten source groups proposed by
Gratuze et al. (2005).
6. Conclusions
This is not the first time that elemental analyses of obsidian have
been employed as a means of critically reviewing the performances
of different analytical techniques (cf. Glascock, 1999), however here
we have the added dimension of temporality, with the various
studies under consideration involving five different methods over
40þ years. An assessment of this elemental data, generated from
the analysis of two southern Cappadocian obsidians, shows mainly
accurate, but some inaccurate data from different analytical techniques. No one technique appears to be superior so, as is archaeometrically usual, the choice of technique depends on a combination
of accessibility, cost, and whether or not archaeological constraints
permit the use of destructive analyses. The implication of this
finding is that similar inconsistencies in data are probably lurking
in published and unpublished elemental concentration data associated with the analyses of other archaeological materials. Given
the magnitudes of many of the analytical differences that appear in
obsidian analyses, such data dispersion, if present, would be
important in the analysis of fine ware ceramics, but probably less so
with regard in the case of more heterogeneous materials such as
glasses, metals and coarse-ware ceramics.
The assessment has also revealed that data, from which it was
possible to calculate average group percentage errors (mirroring
the precisions of analyses), published over the last two decades,
show no temporal trends, and appear to have reached the levels of
the homogeneity of the material analysed.
The data analysis approach used in this paper may be useful on
an intra-technique basis in establishing previously unnoticed
chemical sub-groups. But, this remains speculation until expert
sampling of in-situ obsidian, followed by elemental and, possibly,
stable isotopic analyses, is conducted to establish the actual
heterogeneity of each obsidian flow. As such, archaeometrists await
with great interest the final peer-reviewed publication of the
various analyses undertaken by Gratuze et al. (2005) on the Go ̈ llu ̈
Dag ̆ massif and the remaining large Neolithic – Chalcolithic data set
from Çatalho ̈ yu ̈ k (in excess of 300 samples) that should significantly improve our understanding of the complexities of these
extremely important Cappadocian obsidian sources and their
differential exploitation through time (cf. Binder, 2002; Carter et al.,
2005; Chataigner, 1998).
