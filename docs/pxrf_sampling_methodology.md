## Analysis Methodology

### Instrumentation and Software
The chemical element analysis of the obsidian items selected for this study is conducted using a **Niton Portable X-Ray Fluorescence (pXRF)** spectrometer. The instrument features a beam diameter (spot size) of **8 mm**. 

The device is operated via a USB connection to a laptop using **NDT (Niton Data Transfer) software**. The specific analytical mode selected for this study is **Soils and Minerals: Mining Cu/Zn**.

### Testing Parameters
To ensure that the measurement values are not compromised by insufficient exposure time, a total integration time of **120 seconds** per item was established following preliminary trials (which began at a minimum of 60 seconds). This duration serves as a safety margin for data stability. 

The instrument utilizes various filters to determine the concentrations of different elements. The time distribution for each filter is as follows:

| Filter | Exposure Time (seconds) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Main | 30 |
| Low | 25 |
| High | 25 |
| Light | 40 |
| **Total** | **120** |

---

### Obsidian Handling and Protocol
Measurements are performed using a dedicated test stand. 
* **Large Items:** Items with dimensions exceeding the 8 mm aperture are placed in a longitudinal orientation on the testing surface.
* **Small Items:** For items smaller than the aperture, a plastic cylinder with a thin polyethylene film base is used to secure the sample. (Note: Forster and Grave [2012] utilized paper for this purpose, demonstrating it does not interfere with results).

Except for a few specific cases, each item is tested at least twice: first on its flattest and smoothest side, and a second time on the opposing side. Additional measurements are taken if results appear atypical or to verify the instrument's repeatability. At the start of each session or after a system restart, the last obsidian item from the previous session is re-tested to ensure instrument consistency.

Following each analysis, the results for specific elements (**Si, K, Ca, Fe, Rb, Zr**) are recorded alongside the item details to monitor trends and consistency.

### Sample Condition and Geometry
The obsidian items were not cleaned prior to testing; many retain varying degrees of soil or clay residue. Only a few pieces out of the hundreds tested exhibit a calcareous cortex. Most samples are production waste or core-shaping flakes.



In the accompanying data table, I have noted the **beam coverage** for specific samples to account for geometric effects:
* **Full Coverage:** The item covers the entire 8 mm circular path of the beam.
* **Partial (Medium):** The item covers a significant portion but not the entire aperture.
* **Minimal (Small/Tiny):** The item is significantly smaller than the beam diameter, covering only a very small fraction of the aperture.
* **Remarks:** I have also indicated if the item had visible dirt, encrustations, or other physical irregularities.

### Technical Considerations for Niton pXRF
Analyzing small or irregularly shaped (non-flat) samples can present challenges for precision and repeatability (Milic 2014, Davis et al. 2011). The Niton pXRF compensates for these "geometry effects" in several ways:

1.  **Heavy Element Reliability:** Research suggests prioritizing heavy elements (**Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Nb**) as they are less affected by the surface topography of the item compared to lighter elements (Forster et al. 2011).
2.  **Uncleaned/Irregular Samples:** As demonstrated by Frahm (2013), pXRF analysis of small, irregular, and uncleaned obsidian fragments is viable without compromising accuracy or repeatability, provided the calibration and methodology remain consistent.
3.  **Niton Proprietary Algorithms:** The Niton systems (such as the XL3t or XL5 series) use fundamental parameters (FP) and internal scaling to account for varying sample mass and density, which is critical when the 8 mm beam is not fully obstructed by the sample.
